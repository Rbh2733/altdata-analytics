# Vendor Signals Lab

Private software and AI vendors disclose almost nothing: no quarterly reports, no reported revenue, no analyst consensus. Investors who need to track them anyway are left with exhaust: hiring activity, web traffic, and the sliver of customer spend that happens to cross an observable payment rail. This lab builds that measurement problem end to end: a simulated population of 480 fictional private vendors across six segments with a knowable ground truth, three deliberately imperfect exhaust streams derived from it, and an estimation and evaluation stack that turns the exhaust into a coverage-aware operating-health composite, scored against the truth it never reads.

This lab's sibling, `panel-estimation-lab`, calibrates its estimators to quarterly reported actuals. This one removes that anchor on purpose, because private vendors report nothing. What replaces it: every vendor-quarter carries an explicit coverage tier (A, B, or C) so no number claims precision it doesn't have; claims are rank-based rather than level-based; size is reported as a half-decade band, never a point; and the closest thing to ground truth this lab ever sees is a sparse set of outcome events (fundings, shutdowns, and a handful of disclosed acquisition prices). "Signal" is used throughout in its plain measurement sense: a measured indicator of operating health, nothing more. **This lab measures operating health and recommends nothing.**

Every number below was produced by this code and ships in `outputs/` so it is checkable without running anything.

**Three measured numbers, upfront.** The composite's rank quality by tier is **0.597 (A) vs 0.281 (C)**, a real gradient, and the blended 0.382 is reported only for reference because it oversells what tier C actually knows; the seed-12 robustness check below shows the A-over-B half of that gradient does not survive a different noise draw, which is reported rather than smoothed. The recovered lead structure holds where it was designed to hold and not everywhere: jobs leads revenue realization by a **stable 2 quarters** in both the frozen run and the seed-12 robustness check, while web and spend's leads sit at 2.5-3.0 quarters and swap ordering between seeds. On outcomes, **20 of 25 shutdown vendors (80.0%)** sat in the bottom composite quartile two quarters before shutting down; 3 scored above 50 the quarter before, and are named below with the mechanism.

## What this demonstrates

- **Sample and panel precision under real coverage gaps.** Three exhaust sources with different coverage, lag, and noise, combined into a single composite that is honest about how much of the population it can actually see (tier A 11.0%, B 38.3%, C 50.7% of vendor-quarters).
- **Segment taxonomy as a testable economic claim.** The AI bucket is split into application-layer and infrastructure archetypes with different frozen exhaust signatures, and the split is then measured (coverage-tier mix, rank quality, a dedicated SQL cut) instead of asserted. The "Why this segment split" section below carries the rationale and the measured result, including where it is unflattering.
- **A natural instinct for anomalies, made mechanical.** Four planted pathologies, each judged by a fixed, data-independent rule, with population-wide firing counts reported alongside the catches. In this world one plant is caught three quarters late and only by half, another's production correction is withheld by the temporal fence, and an unplanted vendor trips a detector at organic scale; all three are reported in full rather than smoothed.
- **Proactive use of an AI tool with a validation harness.** A deterministic mock title-classifier ships as the default (92.9% accuracy on 210 hand-labeled titles, three designed confusions measured in the open) with an optional live Claude adapter behind the same interface and a row-coverage guarantee.
- **SQL fluency.** A six-cut DuckDB layer over the shop's own outputs: coverage pivots, a percentile leaderboard, a lead-lag cut expressed as window functions, an anomaly report, a share-shift cut, and a two-segment comparison cut, none of it touching truth.
- **Intellectual honesty about a genuinely hard measurement problem.** No reported actuals exist here by design; the README's own limitations section names every place the generator made the shop's life easier than a real one would be.

## The world, and why synthetic

Ground truth is only knowable in a simulation: nobody hands a real analyst the true ARR of a private company to grade an estimate against. So the lab builds a population of 480 fictional vendors (any resemblance to real software or AI companies is unintended) across six segments, each following a 12-quarter latent health trajectory: `simulation/params.py` fixes an archetype mix of steady growth (40%), acceleration (20%), stall (20%), decline (12%), and wind-down (8%), each with one or two random change points. Hiring responds to a plan change immediately; web usage catches up fully one quarter later; ARR realizes the new growth rate gradually, a third of the delta at the quarter after the change, the rest by two quarters after. That staggered construction is the whole basis of the lead-lag physics measured in the Results section below, and that section reports whatever actually emerged, including where it didn't fully hold.

Outcome events (funding, shutdown, acquisition) come from a per-vendor runway and hazard model, also in `simulation/outcomes.py`, and live only in `data/truth/`, read only by `evaluation/`. Real funding announcements are public in the real world and a real shop would consume them; keeping them judge-only here costs realism but buys a clean out-of-sample outcome check, and that tradeoff is deliberate.

## Why this segment split

The sixth segment, `ai_infrastructure`, was added on 2026-07-22 (parameters frozen before any scoring ran; see the Honesty protocol section). The rationale is methodological, informed by independent sector research at the level of general economic patterns, and names no company: the same class of claim as "SaaS businesses collect cash before delivering service," not a call on anything.

AI vendors are not one economic bucket. Application-layer AI products mostly behave like ordinary usage-billed software: a consumer or prosumer surface, some self-serve billing that crosses card rails, web traffic that actually correlates with usage. An AI infrastructure vendor sells compute and platform capacity into capex cycles through enterprise, invoice-billed contracts: a card panel almost never sees one (the same invisible-billing mechanism this lab's `data_infrastructure` segment already models, taken further), their web surface is documentation and careers pages only loosely coupled to usage that actually flows through APIs and clusters, and their hiring concentrates in cluster and GPU operations, ML infrastructure, and applied research rather than the sales-heavy motions of application vendors. If that distinction is real, it should be codeable as a parameter signature and then show up in the measurement, not just in the labels.

The frozen signature (all in `simulation/params.py` and `estimation/levels.py` before any scoring): 60 vendors, deliberately the smallest segment, since the infrastructure layer is more concentrated than the application layer it serves; spend-channel probability 0.05, below even `data_infrastructure`'s 0.12; an extra 0.45 web-coverage miss probability on top of the size skew, plus a monthly web noise sigma of 0.35 against the global 0.18; a low traffic constant; a job-function mix weighted toward two new title categories (`ml_infrastructure`, `research`) that no other segment's postings draw on; and shop-side level-chain guesses of $300k revenue per employee and $60 per annual visit. Stated expectation, written before the run: the segment should live almost entirely in tier C, spend should be near-invisible, web should be weak, and hiring should be the one channel that reads at all where tracking exists.

Measured outcome, from the committed run. The split is real and it is expensive:

- Coverage: 80.0% of `ai_infrastructure` vendor-quarters land in tier C vs 38.4% for `ai_applications` (pooled across all quarters; the full pivot is the sixth SQL cut). Source presence: spend 8.3% vs 42.9%, web 42.9% vs 84.1%, jobs 23.3% vs 44.2%.
- All twelve of the segment's tier-A vendor-quarters belong to a single vendor (V0436); only five of its sixty vendors ever appear inside the panel's rails at all.
- Rank quality by segment: **0.217** for `ai_infrastructure`, the lowest of the six, vs **0.468** for `ai_applications`, the highest (median Spearman, same machinery as the headline table). Splitting the segment out did not make it measurable; it made the unmeasurability visible and priced, which is the point of coverage tiers.
- Two humbling wrinkles, reported rather than trimmed. First, the raw first-cross counts in the SQL cut are dominated by web in both AI segments (web's near-universal presence and noise let it cross the percentile rule first most often); the hiring-first ordering shows up in the event-study leads conditional on presence, not in unconditioned first-cross counts, and both cuts are shipped. Second, in the pre-fix run disclosed below, the coverage-cliff detector fired four times on this segment's near-empty spend channel (a covered count of zero against trailing means of 1.0 and below): a QA rule built for a panel-visible segment misreads a segment the panel structurally cannot see, which is itself a finding about operating at the dark end of the coverage spectrum.

## The three exhaust families

| source | coverage | lag vs revenue realization | noise | one distortion |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| job postings | 39.8% of vendors tracked, but 66.5% of employment (skewed to larger vendors) | leads by 2 quarters (median, both seeds) | background 3% legitimate re-lists | repost storm: same requisitions re-listed every 2-3 weeks |
| web traffic | 84.2% of vendors covered (skews away from the smallest, and away from ai_infrastructure by construction: 45.0% there) | ~1 quarter by construction; the detector reads 2.5-3.0 and this moved between seeds | level off by 2-3x per vendor forever; usable only as log-growth; ai_infrastructure's monthly sigma is roughly doubled | one vendor's traffic goes to 6x for 5 weeks |
| customer spend panel | 30.0% of vendors ever in a payment channel (segment-dependent, 8.3% to 58.9%) | ~0 by construction; the detector reads 2-3 and this moved between seeds | 5-8% relative noise on growth | one vendor's descriptor fragments into two new, unmapped strings |

A tracked vendor with zero postings that quarter is an **observed zero** (`tracked_zero`, informative: hiring froze), not a missing read; an untracked vendor is genuinely missing. The same zero-vs-missing distinction applies to spend (`present` / `thin` / `absent`, gated on 8+ transactions over the trailing 2 quarters).

## Coverage tiers: the philosophy

There is no anchor to calibrate against, so the only honest move is to say, per vendor-quarter, exactly how much was actually observed, and let every downstream claim inherit that uncertainty rather than launder it. Tier is purely mechanical: A = all three sources present that quarter, B = two, C = one or zero (`estimation/coverage.py`). The tier mix and its drivers, final scored quarter:

| segment | A | B | C | typical reason for C |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| devtools | 21 | 41 | 28 | self-serve spend channel exists but many vendors are still small |
| data_infrastructure | 7 | 19 | 59 | invoice-billed; almost no spend-panel visibility |
| ai_applications | 18 | 40 | 37 | widest dispersion; also the coverage-cliff segment |
| security | 3 | 22 | 45 | enterprise motion, weak spend-panel presence |
| vertical_saas | 4 | 37 | 39 | mid-pack on every rail |
| ai_infrastructure | 1 | 11 | 48 | designed dark: invoice billing plus a thin, noisy web surface |

`ai_infrastructure` is the structural floor of this table: its one tier-A vendor at the final quarter is the same single vendor that accounts for every tier-A vendor-quarter the segment has ever had. Invoice billing genuinely has near-zero spend-panel visibility, and the composite says so structurally rather than pretending otherwise. No headline number in this README is reported unstratified for this reason; the one blended figure below is labeled reference-only, and the population-pooled threshold strip is a robustness disclosure, not a result.

## The four pathologies, and what they cost uncorrected

| pathology | vendor / window | magnitude | detected by | naive cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| bot-traffic spike | V0069 (devtools), 2024-04/05 | traffic x6 for 5 weeks | monthly log-return z = 5.25 vs trailing 6 months (full-history diagnostic; the production correction is withheld, see below) | a false stall read at the spike quarter in the naive series |
| job repost storm | V0290 (security), 2024Q3-Q4 | raw 21 and 18 postings vs 5 unique | unique/raw ratio 0.238 and 0.278 (population baseline near 1.0) | naive hiring-intensity overstated ~4x |
| descriptor fragmentation | V0347 (vertical_saas), 2025Q1 | known descriptor fades over 3 weeks, two unmapped strings take over | bridged only at the 2025Q4 horizon, and only one of the two fragments (overlap 1.000, amount ratio 0.955, cadence ratio 1.243 against a 1.25 ceiling) | a false stall flag at 2025Q1 that fires in the naive and the fenced read alike |
| coverage cliff | ai_applications segment, 2025Q2-Q3 | covered-vendor count 0 vs a trailing mean of 44.0 and 33.0 | segment-level covered count drop > 50% | a mass false-stall read across ~40 vendors if spend absence were mistaken for spend decline |

Population-wide firing counts, reported because a rule with a hidden false-positive rate is not a rule anyone should trust. The bot-spike z-rule raises 66 raw candidates and 60 survive the no-corroboration filter (59 of them background, not the plant), full-history diagnostic counts; the per-quarter time-fenced production read applies 49 of those 60 corrections, and the planted V0069 is among the withheld: at its own 2024Q2 horizon the vendor's partial-quarter spend read shows a log growth of -0.470 (the built-in log(2/3) = -0.41 publication-lag skew plus noise), which clears the 0.35 no-move threshold, so the fenced read treats the spike as corroborated by a real spend move and keeps it. The repost-storm rule fires on 5 vendor-quarters: the two planted, plus three on V0208 (ai_applications), an organically compounding vendor (headcount 439 to 2,113 over the calendar) whose 353-505 raw postings per quarter collide inside a deliberately narrow title bank into ratios of 0.397, 0.354, and 0.307. That is the detector's false-positive cost surfacing at organic scale, not a plant, and it is reported as such. The relist-collapse window collapses 5,476 postings population-wide beyond first listings, a total that includes the flagged storm quarters themselves; the bulk of it is coincidental title-plus-location collisions rather than genuine re-lists. None of the three planted vendors carries a truth inflection inside its plant window (verified before the freeze); the segment-wide cliff necessarily overlaps whatever inflections its roughly 40 vendors were having on their own, which is part of what makes it the most dangerous of the four.

**Outcome validation** (the centerpiece, deliberately its least flattering table):

- Shutdowns: 25 total, all 25 with a scored composite two quarters prior. **80.0%** sat in the bottom population quartile; median composite one quarter before shutdown was **28.2** vs a population median of 50.0. Three shutdown vendors scored *above* 50 the quarter before shutting down: V0136 (tier C, web-only, composite 52.6, shut down 2024Q4), V0269 (tier C, web-only, composite 51.3, shut down 2025Q4), V0285 (tier B, jobs and web, composite 53.6, shut down 2025Q3). The tier-C web-only decliner failure mode this design predicted before any run accounts for two of the three.
- Funding: 156 of 166 rounds scored. Median composite one quarter before a raise was **50.0**, identical to the population median. This is impurity by design, not a null result being misread: every vendor draws the same base funding hazard each quarter regardless of health, with an elevated hazard reserved for post-inflection accelerators only, so uniform-hazard rounds dominate the count and a median cannot see the thin accelerator tilt.
- Disclosed acquisitions (25 priced, graded at the acquisition quarter itself rather than the later disclosure quarter, since exhaust darkens the quarter after acquisition by design and that is exactly when the disclosure lands): band hit rate **32.0%**, within-one-band **48.0%**, median absolute log10 error **0.559** (about 3.6x). This sits below the design's own "roughly half to two thirds correct-band" expectation and well below the five-segment run's showing, which is reported as the humbling it is: without a reported-actuals anchor, size is a band, not a number, and in this world the fixed level-chain assumptions aged badly.

## Honest failures

**1. A correction that cannot arrive in real time, arrives at half strength, and then manufactures its own artifact.** V0347's descriptor fragmentation: at 2025Q1 the false stall fires in the naive read (composite 39.3) and in the honestly time-fenced read (35.9) alike, because the bridge that would reconnect the new descriptors to the vendor does not exist at any horizon before 2025Q4; the vendor's committed spend status drains to `absent` by 2025Q3 and it demotes to tier B. When the bridge finally builds at the 2025Q4 horizon it captures only one of the two fragments: that fragment passes at a cadence ratio of 1.243 against the frozen 1.25 ceiling, 0.007 from rejection, while the second fragment fails the same window at every horizon (1.278 at the same 2025Q4 read, against the same 1.25 ceiling) and never bridges, so roughly half the vendor's spend stays dark forever. And the arrival of the correction is itself a distortion: reconnected spend snaps the fenced composite to 83.9 at 2025Q4 with an acceleration flag the naive read does not fire. A live deployment would have eaten the false stall for three quarters and then flagged a fake acceleration on the repair. `outputs/naive_vs_qa.csv` carries both trajectories for every vendor-quarter, so all of this is checkable.

**2. A correction that dampens but does not neutralize.** V0290's repost storm at 2024Q3: the naive composite reads 84.2 and fires an acceleration flag; deduped, the read drops to 73.0, but the flag still fires, because even the collapsed unique-requisition series moves enough against the vendor's own two-quarter history to clear the +15 rule. The dedupe removes the 4x overstatement of hiring intensity; it does not remove the flag. The five-segment run's cleaner outcome (flag extinguished by the correction) did not reproduce here, and the difference is the plant vendor's size: on a small vendor, integer posting counts leave the corrected series lumpy.

**3. Vendors the composite got wrong at the worst moment.** Three of the 25 shutdown vendors scored above 50 the quarter before dying (the trio named in the outcome table above). Two are the exact "tier-C web-only decliner" failure mode the design intent predicted before any run: a single noisy source with tier-C shrinkage pulling the composite toward 50 is not enough insulation against a genuine surprise. The third, V0285, was tier B with jobs and web both present, jobs percentile at 31 and web at 94: the composite's frozen weights let one noisy strong source outvote one weak honest one.

## Architecture and fences

```
simulation/   THE WORLD (only importer of params.py)
estimation/   THE SHOP (loader.py is the sole gateway; cannot import simulation or evaluation)
evaluation/   THE JUDGE (only reader of data/truth/; never imports simulation)
sql/          shop-side DuckDB layer; truth never registered as a view
data/exhaust/ data/public/   what the shop can read
data/truth/                  what only the judge can read
outputs/                     everything committed and checkable
```

Three enforcement layers, each with a test: (1) an AST walk confirms no module under `estimation/` or `sql/` imports `simulation` or `evaluation`, no string literal in those packages names a truth path, and `simulation.params` is imported nowhere outside `simulation/`. (2) `estimation/loader.py` is the only I/O gateway on the shop side; it resolves every path and raises `PermissionError` outside `data/exhaust/` and `data/public/`, including `..` traversal, and a monkeypatched `builtins.open` during a full estimation run opens nothing under `data/truth/`. (3) `loader.as_of(quarter)` returns exhaust dated inside or before that quarter (spend carries an extra one-month publication lag on top). The delete-the-future test physically truncates every exhaust and truth row after 2024Q4 in a copied tree and asserts the pipeline's quarter-<=2024Q4 outputs are byte-identical to the same pipeline run on the full tree, restricted the same way. `run_all.py` runs generation, estimation, and evaluation as three separate stages; the estimation stage receives only exhaust and public paths.

## The AI tagger

`estimation/tagger_mock.py` is a deterministic keyword-rule classifier (no network, no key) and ships the published numbers: **92.9%** accuracy on 210 hand-labeled titles across six functions (engineering, ml_infrastructure, research, sales, support, other). Three designed confusions survive by construction, five validation rows each, all falling through to the generic "engineer" rule against a different true label: `Support Engineering Manager` (true support), `ML Platform Engineer` (true ml_infrastructure, carrying none of the ml-infrastructure phrase anchors), and `Research Engineer` (true research, neither a "research scientist" nor a "researcher" by whole-word match). The validation set measures all three rather than hiding them; they are the entire off-diagonal mass of the committed confusion matrix. `estimation/tagger_claude.py` is a drop-in behind the same interface: batched strict JSON, gated on `ANTHROPIC_API_KEY`, verifying every batch returns every sent row exactly once and failing loudly (never falling back to the mock) on a missing key or a dropped row. Function mix is a supplementary reported cut only and is never a composite input, so the tagger's blast radius on every headline number above is zero by construction. Named generator convenience: the template-generated titles flatter a keyword classifier; 92.9% is a property of this dataset, not a general claim.

## The SQL layer

`python sql/run_sql.py` runs six cuts via DuckDB and writes `outputs/sql_report.md`: a coverage-tier pivot by segment and quarter (the ai_applications coverage-cliff quarters read as a visible anomaly in the pivot itself); a composite leaderboard, top and bottom 5 per tier, where a tier-C extreme literally cannot appear post-shrinkage; a lead-lag cut expressed with window functions, demonstrating hiring-first ordering inside SQL without ever touching truth; an anomaly report (posting-volume outliers, corrected traffic months, spend-row counts by segment-month around the cliff); a spend-share-shift cut by segment via `LAG`, with the cliff quarters footnoted as a measurement artifact rather than a market move; and the segment-comparison cut added with the sixth segment, `ai_infrastructure` vs `ai_applications` on tier mix, per-source presence, and first-crossing source per flagged vendor-quarter, which is where the "Why this segment split" numbers above come from. `postings_tagged`, `traffic_clean`, and `spend_resolved` are rebuilt in memory from the same estimation-layer functions the QA pass uses and registered as views directly, rather than committed as CSVs, since committing them would roughly double `data/exhaust/`'s size to store row-level duplicates of information already in `coverage_matrix.csv` and `level_bands.csv`. Truth is never registered as a view.

## Honesty protocol

World parameters, plant specs, composite weights, shrinkage, tier rules, presence rules, QA thresholds, and inflection thresholds were frozen in `simulation/params.py`, `estimation/qa.py`, `estimation/composite.py`, and `estimation/inflection.py` before any scoring ran. The six-segment expansion of 2026-07-22 re-froze under the same protocol: the `ai_infrastructure` parameter signature (segment count, spend-channel probability, web coverage and noise overrides, traffic constant, function mix) and the extended tagger taxonomy (two new functions, a 210-row validation set replacing the 150-row one, with the accuracy moving from the five-segment world's 95.3% to 92.9% as two new designed confusions joined the measured set) were all written and frozen before the pipeline ran against the expanded world. Because the segment set participates in the generator's shared random-draw sequence, the expansion re-rolls the entire world: vendor IDs in this README do not correspond to the five-segment run's IDs, and every measured number here was re-measured from the expansion run. Pre-freeze inspections performed and disclosed: population counts verified against the frozen spec; disclosure count landed at 28 (comfortably above the 6-disclosure minimum, no seed redraw needed); none of the three planted vendors carries a truth inflection inside its plant window (verified directly).

Five post-freeze fixes are disclosed. The first four predate the six-segment expansion; their quoted magnitudes and vendor IDs describe the original five-segment world and are preserved below as the historical record rather than restated against a world that no longer exists. The code properties they describe still hold, and their delete-the-future tests still pass against the expanded world.

The first two were the narrow, cosmetic kind: the disclosed-acquisition grading originally compared the level-band estimate against the *disclosure* quarter, which is guaranteed dark (exhaust stops the quarter after acquisition, exactly the disclosure lag), producing a meaningless 100% miss; it now grades at the acquisition quarter itself, the shop's last live read. And `estimation/levels.py` originally stamped a vendor-quarter with zero source coverage as level_band `<1M`, the same label a genuinely observed small vendor gets; it now stamps a distinct `no_data` band (`level_method` stays `none`) so an absence of evidence is never rendered as evidence of smallness. Neither changed any generator parameter, threshold, or weight.

The third is not cosmetic and is disclosed at length because it is the kind of bug this lab exists to catch in someone else's pipeline. `estimation/features_spend.py` built the descriptor-fragmentation bridge and the coverage-cliff flags once per production run, from `shop.as_of(final_q)`, and applied that single, hindsight-informed bridge to every historical quarter's spend read, including quarters before the bridge's own evidence existed. In the five-segment world this inflated the fragmentation vendor's plant-quarter composite from an honest 23.4 to a fabricated 68.9, exactly the number that run's honest-failure narrative had leaned on as proof the QA layer works in real time; it does not, not that quarter. The fix re-derives both the fragmentation bridge and the coverage-cliff flags fresh per quarter from `shop.as_of(t)`, mirroring the property `features_jobs.compute` already had, with the unit-level delete-the-future test in `tests/test_features.py::test_features_spend_unit_delete_the_future`. `estimation/qa.py`'s `detect_coverage_cliff` was also bounded defensively to the horizon actually present in whatever slice it is handed. No generator parameter, plant spec, composite weight, shrinkage constant, or threshold was touched; only the temporal scope of two QA detectors' inputs changed.

The fourth is the same species of bug, found in the sibling module by looking for the same shape. `estimation/features_web.py` ran the bot-spike no-corroboration check once per production run, from `shop.as_of(final_q)`: the spend it corroborated against was resolved through a bridge built with full-run hindsight, and its quarterly spend totals were complete in hindsight but not at their own close (the panel's one-month publication lag means a quarter's last spend month is never visible as of that same quarter's end). The fix mirrors the third exactly, with the matching unit-level check in `tests/test_features.py::test_features_web_unit_delete_the_future`, built on a fixture where a post-t bridge genuinely flips quarter t's keep-or-correct decision. In the five-segment world the lag half of that leak had withheld seven hindsight spike corrections once fenced honestly; in the current world the same fence is what withholds the planted V0069's own correction, as the pathology section above reports. As with the third fix, only the temporal scope of one QA corrector's inputs changed.

The fifth fix belongs to the six-segment expansion and is disclosed in full because it re-rolled measured results after a first scoring run had been seen. The expanded world's first frozen run produced, among other numbers, a tier rank-quality gradient of A 0.609 / B 0.564 / C 0.259 (blended 0.429), and its QA report showed two plants effectively missing from the experiment: the repost-storm vendor had drawn a security vendor with roughly 8 lifetime postings, whose multiplied storm never reached the detector's own raw>=10 floor (a null experiment, not an honest miss: there was nothing to detect), and the fragmentation vendor's two fragments failed the bridge's cadence window at ratios of 1.44 and 1.58 against the frozen [0.8, 1.25], so the plant was never caught at any horizon. The same run also showed the coverage-cliff rule firing four times on `ai_infrastructure`'s near-empty spend channel (covered counts of zero against trailing means of 1.0, 0.5, 0.3, 0.2). Inspecting the plant picker showed it had never checked that a plant vendor was alive, tracked, covered, or big enough to carry its plant; the five-segment run had simply gotten lucky, and its README's claim that plant vendors were "force-included in their exhaust channel" was only ever implemented for the fragmentation vendor's spend membership. The fix makes the documented apparatus real: all three vendor plants now require the vendor alive through the plant window (the fragmentation vendor through the end of the calendar, since the migrated descriptors are its permanent state), the storm picker gets the same middle-half size restriction the bot-spike picker always had plus a minimum organic-requisition gate of 10 across the storm window, and the storm and spike vendors are force-included in the tracked and covered sets by post-draw override, the exact pattern `exhaust_spend.py` already used, preserving the random stream. What did not change: no generator growth parameter, plant magnitude, shop threshold, composite weight, or shrinkage constant; `data/truth/`'s financials and outcome events are byte-identical between the two runs (verified directly), because the fix executes after all truth-side draws. What did change: plant assignments and all three exhaust streams re-rolled, and with them every measured number; the committed run in this README is the post-fix run. The epistemic cost is stated plainly rather than waved off: re-rolling exhaust after seeing a first run's scores is a second draw, and the fragmentation catch in the committed run (one fragment of two, at a cadence ratio of 1.243 against a 1.25 ceiling) should be read with that in mind. The first run's fragmentation miss is disclosed here precisely so the second run's knife-edge catch cannot masquerade as a first-try result; the seed-12 check below, where both fragments bridge cleanly but the bot spike disappears instead, is a further reminder that which plant a fixed rule catches is substantially a property of the noise draw.

Post-freeze robustness, all labeled as such, computed in `robustness_check.py` and committed in `outputs/robustness_seed_check.md`:

- **Seed-12 rerun**: the tier rank-quality gradient does **not** fully survive. The frozen run's A 0.597 > B 0.563 > C 0.281 becomes A 0.577 / B 0.578 / C 0.278 under seed 12: monotonic in the frozen run, not monotonic under seed 12, where B nominally edges A in the third decimal. The honest summary is that A and B are statistically indistinguishable under reseeding while the C separation survives everything; the five-segment run's A-over-B margin had already collapsed from 19 points to 3 under its own seed-12 check, and the expanded world completes that collapse. Jobs' 2-quarter lead over revenue is exactly stable across seeds; web and spend's leads both move to 3.0 under seed 12 and their ordering flips. Plants under seed 12: the repost storm is caught in one of its two quarters (raw 29, unique 8), the fragmentation vendor bridges cleanly on both fragments, the coverage cliff is caught, and the bot spike is **not** caught: its planted April log-return of +1.65 sits inside a trailing window whose own volatility keeps z under 4, meaning the same variance floor that stops a lucky flat window from manufacturing spikes also lets a genuinely noisy vendor absorb one. A QA suite tuned against one seed's noise draw is not guaranteed to survive a different one, which is the honest lesson of running this check at all.
- **Threshold sensitivity** (10 / 15 / 20-point delta, k=2, floor/ceiling held fixed at the frozen 55/45 so only the delta moves, population-pooled across type and tier, `tests/test_inflection.py` reproduces these exactly): precision rises smoothly (10.5% -> 12.5% -> 14.2%) as recall falls (83.3% -> 78.6% -> 67.8%) and flag volume drops (2,184 -> 1,736 -> 1,317). No cliff, no threshold that looks obviously "right"; the frozen 15-point choice sits in the middle of a smooth tradeoff.
- **Equal-weights composite** (1/3 jobs, web, spend instead of 0.35/0.20/0.45, `tests/test_composite.py` reproduces these exactly): the tier gradient survives (A 0.630, B 0.529, C 0.281, tier C is unchanged since it is usually built from a single source regardless of weighting), so the headline gradient is not an artifact of the specific frozen weights.
- **Shuffle test** (in `tests/test_evaluation_math.py`): permuting composites within quarter collapses a genuine 0.8+ Spearman correlation to under 0.15, confirming the rank-quality metric is actually measuring something and not a construction artifact.

## Limitations, stated plainly

- The job-tracking curve is a two-parameter logistic fit to hit 50% at 40 employees and 90% at 200 exactly, with the ~25%-at-15 design point then falling out at 20.8%; applied to this population's actual (smaller-skewed) headcount distribution, it yields 39.8% of vendors tracked covering 66.5% of employment, both below the design's own rough expectation (~62% / ~85%). Reported as measured, not re-tuned to hit the target.
- The relist-collapse window absorbs 5,476 background postings population-wide, not "a handful": most are coincidental title-plus-location collisions from a deliberately narrow title bank. The same collision mechanism pushes one large organically-hiring vendor (V0208) over the storm detector's ratio threshold in three quarters with no storm planted, so the narrow bank inflates the detector's false-positive rate as well as the dedupe's cost. A real ATS-backed fingerprint would use a stable requisition id and have neither problem.
- The plant vendors are force-included in their exhaust channels (spend-in-channel, jobs-tracked, web-covered) and, since the fifth disclosed fix, gated on being alive and big enough to carry their plants; all of it is generator convenience so the pathologies are experiments rather than lottery draws, disclosed here, not discovered by the shop.
- Inflection precision is genuinely weak (6-20%): the composite's percentile-rank construction is more volatile quarter to quarter than the underlying archetypes are, so the +/-15-point rule fires on ordinary re-ranking noise far more often than on real regime changes. Recall stays healthy because true transitions are usually large; precision does not, because so are plenty of non-events. The threshold-sensitivity check above shows this is a smooth tradeoff, not a bug at one specific number.
- No uncertainty intervals on the composite. A panelist-style bootstrap does not transfer cleanly to a three-source percentile composite with tier-dependent shrinkage; this is the natural next stage, declined here rather than shipped half-built.
- Hand-set, frozen composite weights (0.35 / 0.20 / 0.45), not learned: 480 synthetic vendors is not enough signal to fit a weighting without it grading its own homework. The V0285 shutdown miss above is what that choice costs when the weighted sources disagree.
- QA finds at most the four pathologies its author planted (a floor on difficulty, not a ceiling), and in this very world it clears that floor only partially: the fragmentation bridge arrives three quarters late and half-strength, the planted spike's production correction is withheld by the temporal fence, and the seed-12 check drops the spike detection entirely.
- The level-band chain (`estimation/levels.py`) uses one fixed, deliberately crude assumption set for the whole world; it is meant to be humbled by the disclosed-acquisition table, and this run humbled it harder than the design expected (32.0% band hit rate against a "half to two thirds" hope).
- 784 of 5,760 level-band rows (13.6%) carry `level_method == "none"`: zero sources observed that vendor-quarter, nearly double the five-segment world's share, driven by the deliberately dark sixth segment. These are stamped `level_band == "no_data"`, a non-size label distinct from the six real bands; a consumer of `level_bands.csv` who ignores the method column should treat them as missing, not as a measured size.
- Single-machine scale, method over deployment. Nothing here estimates any real company, and the vendors do not exist.

This is descriptive measurement research on synthetic data; it is not investment advice.
