# Layer 7: Identity Resolution & Clean Rooms

Identity resolution and clean rooms are the substrate under every other layer of this ecosystem. This is the infrastructure that lets Wallet-layer transaction data get matched to Glass-layer viewership data without either party exchanging raw personally identifiable information: PII, emails, phone numbers, name-and-address records, is hashed, salted, or tokenized into persistent identifiers, and matching happens inside a controlled server environment where neither side sees the other's raw records. Whoever owns the match keys and the matching environment collects a toll on every data collaboration that crosses it, which is why the layer's defining 2026 event is an ownership change: the last major independent operator of that toll booth, LiveRamp, agreed in May 2026 to sell itself to Publicis Groupe, and the neutral-intermediary model that the whole open-web data economy was built on is being replaced by holding-company ownership on one side and retail walled gardens, Amazon Marketing Cloud and Walmart Connect, on the other.

The consolidation cycle is nearly complete. LiveRamp itself acquired clean-room pioneer Habu in 2024. WPP acquired InfoSum in April 2025 and embedded it in GroupM. Omnicom completed its acquisition of Interpublic Group in November 2025, a combined entity with over $25 billion in revenue, folding IPG's Acxiom data asset into Omnicom's proprietary Omni platform. With Publicis taking LiveRamp, the three largest global agency holding companies will wholly own the foundational identity, data-collaboration, and clean-room assets that power the open internet, converting shared utilities into competitive moats. The findings below draw on two unlabeled Gemini Deep Research passes conducted roughly nine minutes apart on 2026-07-07, one serving as the base for the Publicis/LiveRamp material, the other supplying the Trade Desk audit-dispute narrative and the AI-assistant traffic table below.

## The LiveRamp/Publicis deal, and the client exodus it has already triggered

On May 17, 2026, LiveRamp Holdings, Inc. entered a definitive Agreement and Plan of Merger with MMS USA Holdings, Inc., a wholly owned Publicis Groupe subsidiary, in an all-cash transaction at $38.50 per share, a 29.8% premium over LiveRamp's $29.66 close on May 15, 2026, the last trading day before announcement. Per Publicis Groupe's own press release, the anchor citation for the deal terms: enterprise value $2.167 billion, total equity value $2.546 billion, with $379 million in acquired net cash bridging the two exactly ($2.546 billion minus $379 million equals $2.167 billion).

The DEFM14A merger proxy filed with the SEC details the negotiation endgame: LiveRamp CEO Scott Howe negotiated Publicis up to a "best and final" $38.50 per share, memorialized in a written response on April 26, 2026, countersigned by Publicis on April 30, with board approvals on May 15 and 16 and an Evercore fairness opinion delivered to the LiveRamp board on May 16. Structurally, newly formed Covey Merger Sub merges into LiveRamp, which survives as a wholly owned subsidiary; approval requires the affirmative vote of at least 66 2/3% of outstanding shares, and LiveRamp had 60,786,315 shares of common stock outstanding as of the record date. A virtual special meeting of stockholders is set for August 17, 2026, and the LiveRamp board unanimously recommended adoption. The agreement carries a $32.35 million break fee under specified termination scenarios, including LiveRamp terminating to accept a superior proposal, "no-shop" restrictions with a narrow fiduciary-out for bona fide written acquisition proposals before shareholder approval, and conversion of outstanding LiveRamp equity awards into restricted cash awards at $38.50 per share on existing vesting schedules, accelerating on qualifying terminations within 24 months of closing.

The financing and integration parameters that follow are single-source, appearing in only one of the two research passes and uncorroborated by the other, so they should be treated as directionally reliable rather than fully settled: the transaction has no financing condition, Publicis intends to fund the all-cash consideration from cash on hand plus new corporate bonds issued in the second half of 2026, with modeled peak net financial leverage of roughly 1.2x in fiscal 2027, preserving its BBB+ and Baa1 investment-grade ratings. LiveRamp lands in Publicis's Technology reporting segment, roughly 14% of net revenue, housing Publicis Sapient, Epsilon, and the Marcel AI operating system, distinct from the Epsilon reporting line, operating as an independent business with Scott Howe continuing as CEO reporting directly to Publicis CEO Arthur Sadoun. On a calendarized 2026 consensus, LiveRamp is projected at roughly $126 million of non-GAAP EBITDA, including an $80 million share-based-compensation adjustment to Publicis accounting standards, with $50 million in anticipated pre-tax run-rate savings making the deal accretive to headline EPS in year one. On the strength of the deal, Publicis raised its constant-currency targets for fiscal 2027 and 2028, net revenue growth to 7% to 8% (from 6% to 7%) and headline EPS growth to 8% to 10% (from 7% to 9%).

The transaction remains pending, not closed, and is advancing through its gating items. Preliminary proxy materials went to employees June 2, 2026, and Publicis and LiveRamp filed HSR premerger notifications with the DOJ and FTC on June 11, 2026. Closing is subject to HSR clearance, CFIUS review, and non-US antitrust and foreign direct investment approvals, with an outside date of May 16, 2027 and a three-month extension mechanism, though both parties publicly target closing before the end of calendar 2026. Shareholder rights firm Johnson Fistel, PLLP opened a customary investigation in June 2026 into whether the LiveRamp board ran a fair process, which has not affected the timeline. Both source passes shared one genuine blind spot here: neither re-confirmed the deal's status as of its own research date despite being asked to. That check was performed separately, against Publicis Groupe's own press release, and confirmed nothing had changed as of that check: signed May 17, 2026, still pending regulatory approval and the shareholder vote, no reported change in terms, competing bid, or regulatory objection. The deal remains a live situation until the vote and regulatory clearances land.

The acquisition completes a deliberate multi-year Publicis buildout from advertising agency into vertically integrated marketing-technology enterprise: Epsilon in 2019 ($4.4 billion, deterministic CRM identity, roughly 250 million consumer profiles per Publicis's own 2019 acquisition announcement), CitrusAd in 2021 (retail media technology), Profitero in 2022 (commerce analytics), and Lotame in March 2025 (1.6 billion global identifiers added to the Epsilon stack). LiveRamp supplies the missing piece, open-web identity infrastructure and the clean-room platform connecting brands to more than 25,000 publisher domains and over 500 technology partners, and Publicis frames the combination as "data co-creation," secure cross-partner dataset enrichment used to train bespoke enterprise AI agents, synthesizing Epsilon's deterministic identity assets with LiveRamp's multi-market collaboration platform so that co-created datasets, rather than static public large language models, become the training foundation for client-specific agents.

LiveRamp's market position rested for over a decade on structural neutrality: its RampID graph and Authenticated Traffic Solution intermediated between brands, publishers, agencies, and retail media networks precisely because LiveRamp had no competing media-execution interest. The acquisition replaces that structural neutrality with contractual promises. Publicis has publicly committed to open access and interoperability with no customer restrictions, but competing holding companies now face running critical identity workflows, audience definition, and outcome verification through infrastructure owned by their primary rival, and the attrition this has produced is not hypothetical. WPP CEO Cindy Rose confirmed on the record at Cannes Lions in June 2026 that WPP has initiated plans to stop using LiveRamp entirely following the acquisition; pressed on the rationale, she answered "What do you think? Next question." WPP is migrating clients to InfoSum, routing data processing through GroupM and WPP Open. Omnicom has accelerated its planned exit, originally set for when its contract ran out in Q1 2028, to roughly May 2027, per CEO John Wren's remarks moving up the "drop dead date" following the acquisition announcement, migrating to Real ID, the proprietary identity framework it has been building inside Acxiom specifically to eliminate external programmatic tolls. Named industry reactions run the same direction: ID5 CEO Mathieu Roche noted that Publicis's own communications, which tout the competitive media advantage the deal brings Publicis clients, directly contradict the neutral-utility framing; CleanTap co-founder Will Rand argued LiveRamp's dominance was tolerated only because it lacked structural alignment with any single buyer or seller, a trust dynamic no corporate firewall restores; Doug Ray of Ray Media Advisory called it "the end of the neutral middle" and drew the parallel to IPG's 2018 Acxiom acquisition, after which Acxiom's growth stalled as competing agencies quietly routed around it, advising publishers and retail media networks to pursue dual-pathway identity architectures; and Christopher Feo, SVP of Sales at Unity CRO, reported a sharp increase in exploratory calls from non-Publicis clients seeking alternative onboarding and clean-room vendors.

The enterprise exposure is most acute in media execution. LiveRamp's Authenticated Traffic Solution underpins Google PAIR clean-room matching inside Display & Video 360, Customer Match passthrough, and data distribution into Google Ad Manager curation, and once Publicis controls LiveRamp, it simultaneously owns the identity graph defining which audiences DV360 can reach and executes media spend for its own clients through the same pipes; when one vertically integrated entity controls both execution and the identity infrastructure feeding measurement, independent verification is structurally compromised. Consultants advising non-Publicis advertisers now recommend treating LiveRamp strictly as a connector rather than a source of truth, with a five-part defensive playbook before close: map every LiveRamp-dependent workflow, move core identity logic and primary data resolution into proprietary cloud environments, renegotiate contracts for explicit portability, export, and audit rights, run parallel tests with alternative identity frameworks, and elevate the first-party data model to stack-level infrastructure. For independent agencies the deal accelerates existing margin compression, forcing a pivot from billable hours toward proprietary AI-driven predictive consumer-intelligence products to compete with holding companies that own integrated data and distribution.

## UID2 becomes the reinforced independent alternative, and The Trade Desk's audit fight with Publicis resolves

The Trade Desk's open-source Unified ID 2.0 is the primary beneficiary of LiveRamp losing its independence. Mechanically, UID2 takes deterministic first-party data, authenticated email addresses or phone numbers, hashes and salts it into an encrypted alphanumeric identifier, and functions as an omnichannel, cross-device ID across CTV, mobile apps, and browsers, with consumer privacy handled through a centralized opt-out portal that participating DSPs and publishers must honor programmatically. UID2 has scaled to adoption by more than 200 publishers and identity providers, including Disney, Spotify, and The Washington Post. Following the Publicis-LiveRamp announcement, TTD's value proposition as the buy-side identifier divorced from holding-company ownership strengthened materially; Rich Greenfield of LightShed flagged the risk of Publicis building a buy-side data-and-intelligence walled garden, and ad tech stocks including The Trade Desk, Criteo, and Viant Technology re-rated upward on the news as analysts anticipated demand for independent infrastructure and further M&A. Supply partners continue to integrate multiple identifier protocols in parallel, UID2, TransUnion, ConnectID, RampID, to maximize addressable match rates, so the practical near-term pattern is dual-pathway rather than wholesale substitution. TTD has reinforced its position with its own deals: the Sincera acquisition in early 2025 and an investment in custom-bidding developer Chalice AI, whose federated AI infrastructure trains custom models informing bidding decisions across fragmented independent publisher inventory, a direct counter to walled-garden closed-loop measurement that lets brands optimize reach without surrendering data sovereignty.

The reaffirmation that followed the Publicis-LiveRamp deal has a backstory that recasts it as a resolution rather than a steady state. In March 2026, Publicis formally advised its clients to pause spending with The Trade Desk, citing an alleged failed third-party audit by Ebiquity and FirmDecisions and accusing TTD of "fee stacking," improperly layering DSP ad tech fees on top of other supply-chain charges non-transparently. TTD denied the allegations, pointing to its independent SOC 1 compliance and arguing the audit's data requests violated customer and partner confidentiality agreements, while offering more granular alternatives. On Publicis's Q1 2026 earnings call, Arthur Sadoun defended the pause as fiduciary duty while explicitly denying any plan to build a rival self-serve DSP. The dispute contributed to a temporary 13% decline in TTD's stock amid broader ad tech volatility. On June 12, 2026, the two companies issued a joint statement resolving the dispute: Publicis restored The Trade Desk to its recommended DSP list, stating the audit's identified differences had been addressed. The ninety-day arc from "pause spending" to joint reaffirmation demonstrates real interdependence: agencies cannot forgo TTD's omnichannel inventory and UID2 targeting, and DSPs cannot sever ties with holding-company-controlled media budgets. The underlying friction, holding companies building proprietary data stacks versus independent platforms offering open-source rails, remains the defining tension of 2026 regardless of that specific resolution.

TTD's own agentic buildout continued through this period. Its Q2 Kokai platform updates introduced granular per-component optimizations, customizable lookback windows, and Deal Desk upgrades, and the centerpiece, the Koa Agents campaign-management system, is in pilot with major agency executives, built on an interoperability framework called the Open Agentic Kit that connects to external AI models including Anthropic's Claude, letting a Koa Agent ingest a raw agency media plan, reformat it into a system-compatible template, and programmatically build the campaign inside the DSP.

## Amazon Marketing Cloud versus Walmart Connect: the walled-garden clean-room economics

Amazon's advertising business is the third-largest global digital ad platform behind Google and Meta. Per Amazon's own Q1 2026 earnings release, Q1 2026 advertising services revenue was $17.24 billion, up 24% year over year, with trailing-twelve-month advertising revenue over $70 billion (Andy Jassy: "Advertising grew to over $70 billion in TTM revenue"). The platform supports over 2 million active advertisers, with average Sponsored Products CPCs reaching $1.20 to $1.80 in 2026, up roughly 15% year over year, and high-competition categories such as supplements and electronics running two to three times those averages. The ad stack is increasingly inseparable from Amazon's AI infrastructure: AWS grew 28% to $37.6 billion in Q1 2026, Amazon's Anthropic investment totals $13 billion, and AI-generated creative tools and sponsored formats now run inside the Rufus assistant.

Amazon Marketing Cloud is the privacy-safe clean room connecting upper-funnel DSP impressions, Prime Video, Twitch, third-party streaming TV, to lower-funnel Sponsored Products conversions, mapping the "Golden Triangle" of off-Amazon traffic, Amazon Attribution tags on external Google, Meta, and TikTok traffic, Sponsored Display, and AMC itself, so a brand can trace an off-platform interaction to a downstream Amazon purchase, organic search lift, or Subscribe & Save conversion. Access is SQL-native at the event level, with an instructional query library covering path-to-purchase modeling, audience-overlap deduplication for frequency waste, and isolation of ad sequences driving new-to-brand conversions; one 2026 practitioner analysis puts the average return-on-ad-spend improvement from shifting bids toward high repeat-buyer-velocity campaigns at 34.1%. Amazon has layered on a natural-language "Ads Agent" in beta and ML Custom Models allowing custom bidding algorithms deployed inside the clean room, and a secondary industry of SaaS platforms (Pacvue, Skai, Intentwise) and AI wrappers now connects AMC's SQL infrastructure to LLM interfaces for brands without data-science teams.

Two pricing and access mechanics matter for everyone tolled by this garden, both settled directly against Amazon's own documentation. First, the free tier: per Amazon's AMC "Paid features overview" page, all Amazon first-party signals covered under AMC Paid Features, including Amazon Retail Purchases, Prime Video Insights, and Brand Store Insights, are free to query from June 1 through December 31, 2026, in all regions where AMC Paid Features are available, an explicitly time-limited promotion with a stated end date rather than a permanent policy (December 31, 2026 is worth revisiting as a date, since the page does not state what happens after). Third-party signals, Experian and NCS demographic segments, remain paid at customized, non-published subscription fees ranging from a few hundred to several thousand dollars per month by account volume. Either way, the strategic effect for 2026 is the same: enterprise-grade clean-room analytics on Amazon's first-party data at zero marginal cost undercuts any independent clean-room vendor trying to charge for basic matching. Second, the lookback windows: per Amazon's advertising blog, Amazon did two separate things, extending the ad-traffic lookback window from 13 to 25 months following its unBoxed 2025 event, and separately unlocking up to 5 years of historical data for store-purchase signals and video viewership, two different datasets, both expansions real, not a single contested figure.

Walmart Connect is smaller and faster-growing, and increasingly plumbed through the very asset Publicis is acquiring. Walmart's ad-business figures, settled against Walmart's own SEC 8-K earnings releases (Walmart's fiscal year ends January 31): FY2025 global advertising revenue was $4.4 billion, up 27% for the full year, and FY2026 global advertising revenue was $6.4 billion, up 46% year over year, with Q4 FY2026 US Walmart Connect growth of 41%. Growth was boosted by Walmart's integration of the Vizio operating system and hardware, which expanded its connected-TV inventory footprint, and on percentage growth Walmart Connect is outpacing Amazon's ad business from a base roughly one-tenth the size. The economics rest on a gross margin profile of roughly 70%, achieved by algorithmically expanding sponsored placements across search, display, and CTV, shrinking organic visibility and forcing suppliers into pay-to-play to hold share; Walmart is explicitly shifting from price leader to profit leader by monetizing digital real estate, with ad profits functionally subsidizing fulfillment and automation capital expenditure.

The clean-room and data stack underneath it is Walmart Luminate, rebranding to Scintilla, the proprietary data-services platform behind Walmart Connect, providing suppliers "Digital Landscapes" insights across omnichannel sales, category performance, customer penetration, and web traffic, spanning online and in-store, with Scintilla subscribers outpacing non-subscribers on total omnichannel sales growth of 15%. Measurement runs on a multi-touch attribution model unified across onsite, offsite, and physical in-club transactions. In April 2026, Walmart Connect launched closed-loop social ads with Meta, letting brands measure how Meta ad spend translates into physical in-store and online Walmart transactions without exporting first-party shopper data, an integration powered by a LiveRamp clean room, which narrows the offsite measurement gap that historically favored AMC and, notably, wires Walmart's flagship offsite product through the asset Publicis is acquiring. Walmart has also dropped minimum advertising bid requirements to lower entry barriers and capture mid-market brand spend that previously defaulted to Amazon. Neither source pass isolates a true AMC-only revenue line; both proxy AMC's scale with total Amazon advertising revenue, and this remains a genuinely open gap in the public record rather than something this research closed.

## Agentic commerce and non-human identity are rewriting what this layer has to verify

Agentic commerce, autonomous AI agents executing the commerce lifecycle from discovery through transaction and post-purchase management, changes what identity resolution has to verify. A 2026 IBM Institute for Business Value study finds 45% of consumers already use AI for parts of the buying journey; McKinsey sizes the global agentic-commerce opportunity at $3 trillion to $5 trillion by 2030; Gartner projects AI agents intermediating $15 trillion in B2B purchases by 2028. An agent does not browse rendered HTML, evaluate banner creative, or respond to retargeting cookies, it converts natural-language constraints ("running shoes, size 10, under $120, delivered by Thursday, flexible returns") into machine-readable parameters and evaluates merchants programmatically, stripping marketing copy down to structured attributes. Discoverability therefore shifts to structured metadata, JSON-LD and Schema.org markup exposing price, availability, shipping, and return terms as data rather than prose; a merchant whose delivery and returns data exists only as visual page elements is invisible to a querying agent, which converts fulfillment speed and return logistics from backend operations into pre-purchase ranking signals, and platforms like nShift are exposing carrier options, delivery estimates, and pricing through agent-consumable APIs.

Two complementary protocol families are emerging to govern this. Commerce-transaction protocols include the Universal Commerce Protocol, launched by Google at the National Retail Federation show in January 2026, enabling agent-to-agent transactions across retailers, payment gateways, and AI systems through a single open standard; the Agentic Commerce Protocol, built by OpenAI and Stripe and launched September 2025, powering secure checkout sessions inside AI interfaces such as ChatGPT with tokenized payment limits keeping humans in control of financial policy; and Model Context Protocol extensions letting AI systems query live retailer source-of-truth data directly rather than scraping, with mid-2026 revisions refining authentication attack surfaces for autonomous agents. Agent-identity and authentication protocols address a separate problem: by 2026, non-human identities, workload identities, service accounts, agent certificates, outnumber human identities in large enterprises by at least 40 to 1, and agents operate at machine speed, spawn nested child sessions, and are vulnerable to prompt-injection and coercion, so static once-per-session login credentials do not suffice. The AgentID Protocol, submitted as an IETF Internet-Draft on March 14, 2026, defines the Agent Identity Token, a cryptographically signed JWT carrying claims for agent identity, owner registration, capability, and delegation chains with scope attenuation, answering which agent is requesting, who is accountable, and what it is authorized to do; around it sit OAuth 2.1 with PKCE for the user-consent phase of delegated workflows, SPIFFE/SPIRE for platform-attested cryptographic workload identities replacing hard-coded API keys, Model Context Protocol runtime guardrails governing tool invocation, and Ping Identity's "Identity for AI" suite, generally available March 2026, for real-time agentic-session detection and continuous contextual authorization at every tool call.

This is already operational, not speculative: Omnicom CEO John Wren has disclosed that the holding group has executed real-world media buys for several clients using autonomous AI agents negotiating and transacting directly with publisher interfaces, bypassing the traditional programmatic ad tech stack and its intermediary tolls entirely. Publicis's stated rationale for LiveRamp points the same direction, framing clean rooms as secure data co-creation environments producing the enriched datasets that train enterprise AI agents. The implication for this layer is that RampID and UID2, built to deterministically identify humans, will have to extend to verifying and tracing agentic authorization, and the protocol stack above is where that verification is being standardized.

## Comscore's CustomIQ becomes both an independent auditor and an AI-traffic measurement authority

Comscore (Nasdaq: SCOR) closed a comprehensive recapitalization on December 29, 2025 (common stockholder approval December 19), eliminating an $18.0 million annual preferred dividend and removing a prior minimum special dividend right of $47.0 million. Mechanically, legacy Series B preferred held by Charter Communications and Cerberus Capital Management was exchanged, with $80.8 million of liquidation preference converted into 9.86 million common shares at an effective $8.19 per share, roughly a 50% premium to the $5.465 90-day volume-weighted average price, and the remaining $183.7 million converted into 12.67 million shares of new Series C preferred at $14.50 per share, zero annual dividend, initially convertible 1:1. On May 27, 2026, Comscore divested its Comscore Movies box-office measurement business, using proceeds to eliminate $40 million in senior debt and completing the transition to a pure-play digital advertising measurement company. On May 28, 2026, Matt McLaughlin, former DoubleVerify COO and a major common stockholder, was appointed CEO, with Jon Carpenter moving to senior advisor, and Stuart Frankel joined the board as Audit Committee chair; Frankel co-founded and led Narrative Science, a generative AI firm acquired by Salesforce in 2021, and previously served on DoubleClick's senior leadership team before its Google acquisition, an appointment that signals the AI-measurement pivot explicitly.

CustomIQ ran the Q1 2026 independent analysis that validated LiveRamp's identity graph: a 99.5% match rate on full PII with complete address data, and 89.8% on email-only records, measured by testing LiveRamp's device linkages against approximately 6,000 Total Home Panels across permutations of name, address, phone, and email. The industry baseline for hashed-email-only solutions typically plateaus at 40% to 60% match rates, so the validation empirically established LiveRamp's deterministic graph as a different tier of asset, third-party evidence that underwrote the valuation Publicis paid. There is a structural irony worth naming directly: the independent auditor validated the asset just before the asset stopped being independent, which makes standing third-party match-rate verification more valuable, not less, in the post-acquisition landscape.

Comscore is positioning CustomIQ as a standalone identity-adjacent product line measuring the shift of consumer intent from search to AI assistants, headlined by the mid-2026 Comscore AI Intelligence Report, "The Last Click Factor," on consumers outsourcing purchase and research journeys to AI assistants. Total US mobile visitation to leading AI assistants reached 54.3 million unique visitors in December 2025, up 107% year over year; US desktop unique visitors rose to 44.4 million by March 2026, up 21.3% from February; and Anthropic's Claude was the fastest-growing platform in Q1 2026, up 130.1% month over month in March to 2.66 million visitors. The fuller picture, by platform, for US mobile unique visitors: the total category grew from 26.2 million in December 2024 to 54.3 million in December 2025 (up 107%, with 83.0 million on desktop by the same date); OpenAI's ChatGPT grew from 18.7 million to 34.5 million (up 84%, 56.4 million desktop); Google's Gemini grew from 5.4 million to 12.8 million (up 137%, 12.3 million desktop); Microsoft's Copilot grew from 3.0 million to 10.6 million (up 246%, 33.4 million desktop); Perplexity grew from 1.3 million to 4.7 million (up 265%, 1.4 million desktop); and Anthropic's Claude, not yet broken out on mobile in December 2024, reached 1.1 million desktop visitors by December 2025.

The paradox CustomIQ surfaces is that AI tools currently send less than 1% of traditional referral traffic to publishers, but the visitors they do send convert at rates up to eleven times higher in some segments. CustomIQ is, in effect, measuring the fragmentation of intent capture itself, the same substrate-level shift the agentic protocols above are being built to handle.
