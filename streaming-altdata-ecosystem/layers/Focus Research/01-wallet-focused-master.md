# Layer 1 Focused Research: Wallet (Subscriber Transaction Intelligence)

Streaming now runs on free cash flow optimization, ARPU expansion, and unit profitability rather than unconstrained subscriber acquisition, and the gatekeepers (Amazon, Apple, Roku, Netflix) bury standalone streaming economics inside consolidated reporting lines and proprietary clean rooms. The wallet layer exists to reconstruct ground truth from money movement: credit and debit card transaction streams, banking API feeds, and digital invoice data, modeled into cohort survival curves, premium-to-ad-supported plan-mix migration, and cross-platform wallet allocation. This document consolidates a focused research pass run against four known gaps from prior wallet-layer work: a confirmed dollar figure that any wallet vendor pays banks for data access, a named client for Antenna's Subscriber Views product, primary-source confirmation of per-pull API fees at Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and Citi, and any genuinely new zero-party entrant. The pass closes the fourth gap outright, adds directional but not primary evidence on the first and third, and leaves the second untouched; each verdict is flagged in place below. The structural finding sits above all four: the wallet layer's unit economics and legal viability are inverting simultaneously on the cost side (bank API tolls) and the compliance side (California's DROP enforcement), and the escape route runs through zero-party data.

## What the layer actually sells

The product sold to multi-strategy hedge funds, legacy studios, and Big Tech strategy teams is not raw transaction feeds but deterministic triangulation of consumer spending over time. Because Amazon and Apple intentionally blend standalone streaming economics into consolidated segments (Apple's "Services," Amazon's "Subscription Services") with no disclosed standalone ARPU or churn, analysts construct synthetic proxies: observing Amazon Channels subscription volume through a transaction panel, setting it against disclosed consolidated revenue growth, and backing into an implied Prime Video ARPU. The same panels track where a churned subscriber's discretionary entertainment budget goes, whether to a competitor like Netflix or out of entertainment entirely into retail. Gatekeepers weaponize this data asymmetry to control billing relationships and dictate revenue splits in carriage disputes; the wallet layer is the counter-instrument.

The mechanics depend on continuous longitudinal observation. Calculating an M+1 (Month 1) retention rate, or tracking a serial churner across a rolling twelve-month window, requires observing the absence of a cancellation as closely as the presence of a renewal. Historically that meant aggressive background polling: an aggregator holding a user's authenticated banking credentials could query the bank's servers multiple times per day, detecting a streaming charge the moment it cleared and feeding real-time models that quantitative funds used to trade the delta between panel-indicated subscriber growth and Wall Street consensus. The entire economics of high-frequency longitudinal tracking rested on one assumption: the marginal cost of a server ping was effectively zero.

## The incumbent hierarchy, and the Subscriber Views gap

The competitive structure crystallized through 2025 and 2026 into a defined oligopoly. YipitData is the scaled multi-sector incumbent: a $475 million Series E led by The Carlyle Group with prior investment from Norwest Venture Partners, the 2023 absorption of 1010data, institutional investors holding 60 to 75 percent of voting power, and reported maneuvering toward a 2026-2027 IPO. Consumer Edge altered the balance by acquiring Earnest Analytics in April 2025, bolting Earnest's specialized healthcare and pharmacy claims datasets onto its transaction intelligence for a cross-vertical consumer view, though HIPAA de-identification requirements keep the underlying data architectures functionally siloed. Bloomberg Second Measure and M Science occupy similar multi-sector ground as fully integrated corporate subsidiaries, leveraging massive aggregate receipt panels mapped to financial KPIs.

Antenna remains the pure-play streaming specialist, its category leadership resting on targeted capitalization (BDMI, Raine Ventures, Foundry Group) rather than multi-sector scale. It focuses on longitudinal SVOD behavior, specifically isolating 12-month resubscribe trajectories, and its Subscriber Views product fuses Automatic Content Recognition tuning data from the hardware layer directly with its transaction panels. On the known gap: this pass adds product mechanics but names no Subscriber Views client. That gap stays open.

## The JPMorgan precedent, and what the fee gap still lacks

The zero-marginal-cost assumption fractured permanently in the second half of 2025, when the industry-wide migration from credential-based screen scraping to secure, tokenized Financial Data Exchange (FDX) developer APIs gave banks the technical visibility to measure, throttle, and ultimately price third-party data requests.

JPMorgan Chase quantified the burden first. Internal documentation revealed 1.89 billion API data requests from aggregators in June 2025 alone, of which only about 13 percent traced to a direct consumer-initiated action (executing a payment, logging into a budgeting app); the remaining 87 percent was automated background pulling that fed risk engines, analytics panels, and data resale. One unnamed aggregator, widely reported by industry sources to be Plaid, accounted for 1.08 billion of those requests in a single month, with just 6 percent linked to active consumer engagement. Framing the traffic as infrastructure strain and fraud exposure, JPMorgan opened aggressive negotiations; initial projections circulated in the financial press put Plaid's potential exposure at up to $300 million in new annual data access fees under the proposed tiered schedule. By September 2025 the two sides reached a landmark paid data-access agreement, and by November 2025 JPMorgan had updated contracts with Plaid, Yodlee (Envestnet), Morningstar, and Akoya, the entities responsible for more than 95 percent of third-party data requests routed into the bank.

On the known gap, precision matters: this pass does not close the confirmed-dollar-figure question. The exact JPMorgan-Plaid terms remain proprietary under a customized arrangement; "fractions of a cent per data pull" is industry-consensus characterization, not a contract figure, and the $300 million number is a pre-agreement press projection, not a disclosed payment. The nearest hard number anywhere in the pass is Akoya's reported $2,000 per month minimum commitment (below), and even that carries a "reportedly." Directional evidence, not closure.

## Contagion mechanics: Bank of America, Akoya, and the Citi assertion

The JPMorgan deal established a commercial blueprint the rest of the tier-1 banks adopted, but the evidence quality varies by institution, which bears directly on the third known gap.

Bank of America is the best documented, though what the primary sources confirm is consent architecture, not fees. In December 2025 Plaid announced a mandatory API migration for all existing Bank of America connections, with the new API rolling out for new connections from February through mid-March 2026. Existing connections on legacy infrastructure are being systematically disconnected between mid-March and late October 2026; aggregators must push users through an "Update Mode" re-authentication flow, and Plaid engineered a PENDING_DISCONNECT webhook to warn downstream clients, after which a consumer who fails to re-authenticate within one week lands in a terminal ITEM_LOGIN_REQUIRED state that permanently severs the feed. Once migrated, the bank enforces a hard 12-month consent expiration plus automatic expiration after any rolling 180-day access gap. For vendors modeling multi-year subscriber lifetime value, that 12-month authentication cliff injects severe survivorship bias: a consumer who churned from a streaming service six months ago will not re-authenticate a bank connection to sustain someone else's research panel.

Wells Fargo and PNC have directed their data-sharing architecture toward Akoya, the data access network jointly owned by Fidelity Investments, The Clearing House, and 11 major member banks including Bank of America, Capital One, Citi, and JPMorgan Chase. Following JPMorgan's fee posture, Wells Fargo and PNC executives explicitly endorsed the tolling model and steered aggregators to Akoya's API infrastructure while choking off legacy screen-scraping vectors; in October 2025 PNC and Plaid settled years of litigation with a bilateral data access agreement routed through Akoya's FDX-aligned network. Akoya operates as an enterprise-grade toll bridge, with opaque pricing reportedly mandating a $2,000 per month minimum plus per-call and per-connection fees, a floor that prices out independent developers and mid-tier analytics firms and forces them through mega-aggregators who pass down their own rising wholesale costs.

On the known gap: primary-source confirmation of per-pull fees at Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and Citi is not achieved. Bank of America's mechanics are documented through Plaid's own changelog and OAuth documentation, but those documents establish migration and consent expiration, not a per-pull price. Wells Fargo's participation rests on executive endorsement and Akoya routing. Citi appears only as an Akoya co-owner and by the source's assertion that it is aligning its commercial API strategy with JPMorgan; no Citi-specific fee document surfaced. The contagion claim stands on directional evidence.

|---|---|---|
| JPMorgan Chase | Direct bilateral fractional-cent fee per API pull | Sets the variable-cost precedent; penalizes wasteful background polling |
| Bank of America | Forced API migration (March through October 2026); 12-month hard consent expiration; 180-day inactivity timeout | Severe panel survivorship bias; breaks multi-year cohort tracking |
| Wells Fargo / PNC | Mandated routing through the bank-owned Akoya network | Enterprise pricing floor (reported $2,000/month minimum plus per-call fees); eliminates independent scraping |

## Downstream margin compression

Plaid publicly stated it would not immediately pass the JPMorgan fees down to its roughly 7,000 clients, but the arithmetic guarantees eventual compression. Industry analyses of the personal finance and aggregation market estimate that a minimum viable Plaid integration at a modest 10,000-user scale already runs $180,000 to $360,000 in annual recurring operational cost, and mid-market alt-data vendors reliant on high-frequency transaction parsing face an estimated 20 to 30 percent increase in raw data ingestion costs through 2026 as the fee model permeates the banking sector. The wholesale repricing rewrites the gross margins of the entire alternative data supply chain, and it forces consolidation: scaled incumbents like YipitData have the balance sheet to absorb rising acquisition costs while pushing toward a public offering, while narrow streaming-focused providers without enterprise capital will find variable-cost polling for daily SVOD sign-up tracking economically unviable, accelerating distressed roll-ups and platform liquidations.

## The federal vacuum: Section 1033 collapses

The banks' tolling window was opened by regulatory failure. The CFPB's Personal Financial Data Rights rule under Section 1033 of Dodd-Frank, finalized in October 2024, explicitly prohibited data providers from charging third parties for covered data access, with compliance phasing in from April 1, 2026 for the largest depository institutions. In October 2025, Judge Danny Reeves of the Eastern District of Kentucky issued a preliminary injunction in Forcht Bank, N.A. v. CFPB, finding the Bureau highly likely exceeded its statutory authority (particularly the fee prohibition and the mandate that banks build third-party developer APIs without cost recovery) and that the rule was arbitrary and capricious under the Administrative Procedure Act for relying on consensus standards that did not exist. After the early-2026 leadership change (Director Rohit Chopra's departure, Acting Director Russell Vought's installation), the agency abandoned its defense, told the court it intends an interim final rule to rescind and substantially rewrite the 1033 regulations, and issued an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking explicitly reopening whether data providers may charge cost-recovery fees. The April 2026 deadline is effectively moot, and JPMorgan moved into the vacuum: data access in the United States is now a priced commodity, not a federally protected free utility.

## The state-level clamp: California's Delete Act and DROP

Into the federal vacuum stepped California. Senate Bill 362, the Delete Act, enacted October 10, 2023, required the California Privacy Protection Agency to build a centralized deletion mechanism, the Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform (DROP), which went live for consumer registration on January 1, 2026. As of mid-2026, over 500 data brokers are registered in the state and over 215,000 consumers have registered deletion requests. Beginning August 1, 2026, brokers must query DROP at least once every 45 days, delete all personal information and derived inferences for matched consumers within 90 days (a duty extending to downstream service providers and contractors), and permanently refrain from selling or sharing new personal information about them. Failure to register costs $200 per day; far worse, each unaddressed deletion request accrues $200 per day, so a systemic database synchronization failure at a wallet vendor could compound into tens of millions of dollars of liability within a single month. For Consumer Edge and YipitData, whose enterprise value rests on massive passive historical transaction databases mapping SVOD churn and cross-shopping, the engineering overhead of continuous 45-day suppression sweeps across fragmented data lakes is staggering.

The statute's escape hatch is definitional. A "data broker" is a business that knowingly collects and sells personal information about consumers with whom it has no direct relationship, and the CPPA's late-2024 regulations define a direct relationship strictly: the consumer must have "intentionally interact[ed] with a business for the purpose of obtaining information about, accessing, purchasing, using, or requesting the business's products or services within the preceding three years." Mere data collection does not qualify. A consumer who connects a bank through Plaid to reach a budgeting app has no direct relationship with the hedge fund data vendor buying the anonymized exhaust, so that vendor is a regulated broker fully exposed to DROP sweeps. This boundary is the primary catalyst behind the market's architectural pivot.

## Zero-party disruption: the gap that closed

Zero-party data is information a consumer intentionally and explicitly shares with a platform in exchange for a direct reciprocal reward, through specialized mobile apps, digital wallets, browser extensions, and gamified credit platforms. Because the consumer intentionally installs the app and participates in the reward ecosystem, the platform holds a legally recognized direct relationship under the CPPA definition and sits outside the data broker category, categorically immune to DROP deletion sweeps. And because many of these platforms initiate transactions, issue proprietary virtual cards, or run merchant-funded closed-loop networks, they capture point-of-sale data natively, bypassing the Plaid/JPMorgan wholesale API layer and its fractional-cent tolls entirely. Scale is no longer the barrier it was: receipt-scanning app Fetch Rewards commands over 17 million users and Ibotta over 40 million, each generating proprietary SKU-level and transaction-level datasets.

On the known gap: this is the one the pass genuinely closes. Coverd is a new, named, capitalized zero-party entrant not present in prior wallet-layer research. Backed by Andreessen Horowitz, the Coverd Card replaces delayed points portals with instantaneous algorithmic rebates: cardholders can unlock up to 100 percent instant cash back at the moment a transaction clears, with rebate volume computed dynamically from purchase characteristics, recurring spending hierarchies, and time-of-day tracking, and rewards poolable into in-app games of skill and chance for electronics and event tickets. By May 2026 Coverd had distributed over $12 million in cash back in a single month, pushing lifetime rewards past $25 million. Analytically, Coverd is a highly engaged first-party panel wearing a credit card's clothing: it issues the card and processes the transaction natively, capturing exact SKU-level or merchant-level clearing data in real time with none of the 30-day lag inherent to third-party bank polling. It owns the transaction, nullifying API costs, and owns the relationship, nullifying DROP exposure.

Wildfire Systems is a second, adjacent entrant: a B2B2C infrastructure layer embedding shopping rewards and cash-back extensions into the interfaces of financial institutions, fintechs, and technology companies, with access to over 50,000 merchant programs on a merchant-funded model. Its Commerce Intelligence engine captures deterministic intent and conversion data at the digital point of sale, before the transaction ever reaches a bank statement, harvesting cross-shopping behavior, promotional elasticity, and subscription acquisition metrics entirely outside the open-banking perimeter. Revolut illustrates the neobank variant: timed cash-back "quests" produced a 590 percent uplift in transactions per user versus non-gamified segments and a 625 percent boost in incremental sales during campaign windows, and 2026 data shows the gamification features lifting saving habits 22 percent and average user savings 20 percent, turning routine banking into a predictive behavioral dataset on price sensitivity and brand loyalty. A fourth vector is gift card infrastructure: the digital gift card market hit $47.15 billion globally in 2025 and is projected past $98 billion by 2034, and when a fintech lets a user convert a stablecoin balance into a Netflix or Apple gift card in-app, it captures deterministic streaming subscription telemetry without ever touching a legacy checking account.

| Merchant-funded extensions (Wildfire) | Browser and app-level offer activation and referral tracking | Captures intent and conversion together; bypasses banking exhaust |

## What institutional buyers inherit

For funds trading ahead of quarterly earnings, the degradation of the traditional supply chain introduces compounding friction. Synthetic proxy construction (an Apple TV+ M+1 retention rate, a backed-into standalone ARPU for the Disney+/Hulu/Max bundled tier) has always relied on the scale and near-zero acquisition cost of millions of passively scraped bank accounts. As tolls compress margins, aggregators will cut background polling frequency; panels that delivered daily transaction updates may revert to weekly or bi-weekly batches, destroying the temporal latency edge quantitative strategies depend on. Bank of America's 12-month consent expirations will artificially truncate cohort survival curves, leaving insurmountable gaps in longitudinal data and rendering historical streaming projection models dangerously inaccurate. The predictable response is capital migrating from broad passive panels toward specialized zero-party pools, including hedge funds striking direct data-licensing agreements with platforms like Coverd, Wildfire, and major point-of-sale applications, cutting out traditional alt-data middleware entirely.

The trade-off is methodological. Passive panels, whatever their flaws, approximated a representative macroeconomic cross-section by scraping millions of randomized accounts. Zero-party platforms require active opt-in to gamified reward structures, so their user bases skew younger, digitally native, and highly price-sensitive. A studio pricing a classic catalog acquisition on retention data drawn exclusively from a gamified fintech app will systematically under-represent older, higher-income, less price-sensitive demographics; correcting the skew demands aggressive statistical smoothing and synthetic demographic weighting. The vendors that merge legacy API scale with zero-party determinism and legal safety are positioned to dominate the 2026-2027 cycle and command the valuation premiums that go with it.

## Outlook

Three conclusions carry forward. First, the API toll bridge is permanent: the JPMorgan precedent, adopted across Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and Citi, converts data access into a variable-cost commodity, consolidates the mid-market vendor tier, and entrenches bank-owned networks like Akoya. Second, state privacy mandates have outpaced federal action: with Section 1033 collapsed, DROP's August 2026 enforcement creates compounding liability for any broker built on passive third-party exhaust, and vendors without continuous automated compliance will be regulated out of existence. Third, zero-party architecture dictates survival: the strategic high ground has shifted from aggregating the most bank data to engineering the most compelling direct consumer relationships. The gatekeepers who rely on opacity for commercial leverage will find their defenses pierced not by scrapers but by their own consumers, trading deterministic transaction histories to zero-party platforms for fractional rewards.
