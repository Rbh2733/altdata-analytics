# Layer 0 Focused Research: Ecosystem Gatekeepers

This master consolidates four focused research passes on the Layer 0 boundary condition: three broad gatekeeper passes (a falsification review, a structural economics audit, and a full gatekeeper brief) plus a dedicated reverse-flow audit. All four open from the same premise, stated here once: the direct-to-consumer streaming economy has permanently transitioned from capital-subsidized subscriber acquisition into a strict regime of free cash flow optimization, average revenue per user expansion, and unit profitability, and it is that transition that makes gatekeeper opacity structurally binding. Because major platforms bury standalone streaming economics inside consolidated reporting lines and walled-garden environments, studios, agencies, and institutional investors are forced to buy their way back to ground truth through the seven vendor layers beneath this one: Wallet (transaction intelligence), Glass (hardware telemetry), Shield (fraud and verification), Demand/Valuation, Funnel (downstream intent), Sports-Rights, and Identity/Clean Rooms.

The gatekeepers themselves sort into two variants. Variant A, the data-withholding monetizers (Amazon, Roku, and now Netflix), decline to disclose granular economics to the studios and creators supplying their content while running proprietary clean rooms that monetize exactly that behavioral data for advertisers. Variant B, the distribution and rights-chokepoint controllers (Google/YouTube, Disney, and the combined Fox-Roku enterprise), obscure standalone economics through bundle pricing, live-event exclusivity, and carriage leverage rather than ad-tech alone. Apple fits neither cleanly: it withholds everything and monetizes almost nothing. Two consolidation events reshaped the layer during the research window: Fox Corporation's June 2026 agreement to acquire Roku, and the Paramount Skydance (PSKY) acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery, which the research concludes disqualifies that combined entity from gatekeeper status altogether.

The largest single addition in this consolidation is the reverse flow: the capital and data that move from the vendor layers back up to the gatekeepers through inventory expropriation, data licensing, API tolls, and mandatory cloud compute. That material has its own major section below. One framing conflict should be flagged at the outset: two of the passes describe the reverse flow as "undocumented," while the falsification pass explicitly contradicts that label, finding the flow highly visible in public financial disclosures, ad-tech API documentation, and press releases. The substantive findings agree; the disagreement is over whether the flow was ever actually hidden.

## Amazon: the architecture of absolute asymmetry

Amazon is the purest, most heavily scaled execution of the Variant A model. It blends Prime Video's standalone economics into its "Subscription Services" reporting line, fully obscuring average revenue per user and churn from the studios and licensors supplying the platform, while operating Amazon Marketing Cloud (AMC) and the Amazon DSP as sell-side infrastructure that sells access to that same audience. As of Q1 2026, Amazon's advertising business generated over $70 billion in trailing-twelve-month revenue, with first-quarter advertising services revenue surging 24% year over year to $17.24 billion. The gatekeeper brief separately reports that Amazon's global advertising revenue "surpassed $56.2 billion in 2025," but that dating cannot be reconciled with the same document's report of a $67 billion to $68 billion annualized run rate by the end of 2025, nor with the $70 billion trailing figure by Q1 2026; the $56 billion mark almost certainly belongs to full-year 2024, and the dating conflict is carried open here. On the run-rate figures, Amazon ranks as the third-largest digital advertising platform globally behind Google and Meta, holding an 8.6% share of all global media dollars, generating more revenue than the entire global music industry, roughly $20 billion more than YouTube, and about triple the gross revenue of the global newspaper industry. eMarketer projects the business exceeds $90 billion by 2027.

The asymmetry between suppliers and advertisers is explicit. Jennifer Salke, head of Amazon Studios, has publicly stated that Prime Video does not report viewership data to creators or the press, framing the platform as an ecosystem perk designed to "entertain and delight Prime customers" rather than a volume-driven network; creators are told Amazon "knows what the numbers are" and will discuss performance only conceptually. Prime Video reporting for channel partners is heavily aggregated: end-of-month subscription counts and total minutes streamed, with the viewer journey obfuscated. Advertisers inside AMC and the Amazon DSP, by contrast, get granular access to that exact behavioral telemetry, connecting upper-funnel DSP streaming TV impressions directly to lower-funnel Sponsored Products conversions.

The sources disagree on how AMC access is currently priced, and the disagreement appears to be partly chronological. The gatekeeper brief describes "Paid Features" requiring a premium monthly subscription to unlock Prime Video Insights, Prime Video Channel Insights, Flexible Shopping Insights, and Amazon Retail Purchases. The structural economics audit reports that in late 2025 and 2026 Amazon removed the explicit paywalls on premium datasets including Audience Segment Insights and Amazon Retail Purchases, offering them at no cost through the end of 2026; the falsification pass narrows this further, reporting that as of June 2, 2026 the Amazon Retail Purchases and Flexible Shopping Insights datasets are available to all AMC users at no additional cost. The reverse-flow audit meanwhile confirms paid signals persist for specific datasets (Experian Auto Vehicle Purchase intelligence, and Audience Segment Insights for non-endemic advertisers, billed a la carte). The reconciliation adopted here: paid features were introduced, then selectively zero-priced through end of 2026 as a lock-in maneuver, with certain third-party and non-endemic signals still carrying subscription fees. The strategic read is consistent across all sources: free data access is a loss leader that shifts the taxation mechanism from explicit licensing fees to AWS compute, querying, and storage costs (clean room instances are hosted entirely on AWS, including S3 staging), and above all to media spend routed through the Amazon DSP.

Unit economics of the ad machine, per the gatekeeper brief: upwards of 80% of Amazon's advertising revenue originates from bottom-of-funnel Sponsored Products, while the DSP reaches an average monthly ad-supported audience of more than 300 million people in the United States alone. Sponsored Products campaigns convert 19% better when combined with DSP audience targeting. DSP access minimums conflict across sources: the gatekeeper brief says the platform previously required $35,000 minimum monthly spend through managed service and now offers self-service at $5,000 per month, while the reverse-flow audit puts managed-service minimums at approximately $50,000 per month; both figures are carried open. Published 2026 pricing tiers:

| Amazon ad format | 2026 average CPC / CPM | Average ACoS target | Funnel position |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sponsored Products | $1.20 to $1.80 CPC | 20% to 35% | Bottom (purchase) |
| Sponsored Brands | $1.50 to $2.50 CPC | 25% to 40% | Middle (consideration) |
| Sponsored Brands Video | $0.80 to $1.50 CPC | 15% to 30% | Middle (consideration) |
| Sponsored Display | $0.50 to $1.20 CPC | 30% to 50% | Middle (consideration) |
| Sponsored TV / DSP | $15 to $35 CPM | Brand awareness focus | Top (awareness) |

The second-order implication is that Amazon's posture breaks traditional Hollywood intellectual property valuation. Studios denied the viewership data Amazon sells to advertisers cannot price the backend value of the shows and films they license to Prime Video, which is precisely what forces the entire entertainment industry onto the Demand/Valuation vendor layer to synthesize demand from social signals before negotiating renewals.

## Apple: ecosystem bait, not a smaller Amazon

Apple blends Apple TV+ into its Services line with zero standalone disclosure of average revenue per user, churn, or viewership, but the intent behind the opacity diverges completely from Amazon's. Apple operates no programmatic data-monetization clean room; there is no Apple equivalent of AMC or the Roku Data Cloud. Eddy Cue has stated on the record that there are no plans to introduce an ad-supported Apple TV+ tier, arguing it is better for consumers not to be interrupted with advertisements. Apple TV+ functions purely as non-monetized ecosystem bait: a premium acquisition and retention mechanism for iPhones, iPads, MacBooks, and the broader Services bundle spanning Apple Music and iCloud. Apple consequently sits entirely outside the identity resolution and clean-room dynamics that define Amazon, Roku, and Netflix.

The single exception is live sports. Through its exclusive global Major League Soccer distribution deal, Apple negotiated exclusive data monetization rights covering sports betting handles, merchandise sales, and creator telemetry: a signal that while Apple refuses to build ad-tech for scripted television, it treats deep integration of sports transactional data as a more defensible asset than content exclusivity alone. For the Sports-Rights vendor layer, this closed-loop MLS arrangement amounts to a total lockout.

## Roku before Fox: the hardware Trojan horse

Prior to June 2026, Roku was the definitive independent Variant A gatekeeper: an advertising and data brokering company using low-margin streaming hardware as a Trojan horse to capture household viewing telemetry directly from the living room. Its recast 2025 reporting replaced the combined Platform segment with three segments (Advertising, Subscriptions, Devices) and made the centrality of data monetization explicit:

| Roku metric (recast FY2025) | Reported value | Change |
|---|---|---|
| Total net revenue | $4.74 billion | +15% |
| Platform revenue | $4.14 billion | +18% |
| Advertising revenue | $2.33 billion | +13% (68% surge in video ad impressions) |
| Subscriptions revenue | $1.82 billion | +25% (Frndly TV acquisition, Howdy launch) |
| Devices revenue | $0.59 billion | Managed at break-even |
| Net income | $88.4 million | Return to profitability |
| Adjusted EBITDA | $420.5 million | Significant expansion |
| Total streaming hours | 145.6 billion | +15% |
| ARPU | $41.49 | As of Q4 2024 |
| Streaming households | 89.8 million | As of Q4 2024 |

One source conflict sits inside these figures: the reverse-flow audit describes Roku as generating "over $3.2 billion in platform revenue, roughly 86% of total net revenue," which cannot be squared with the recast FY2025 disclosure of $4.14 billion in platform revenue (over 87% of total); the recast figure is the operative one, and the $3.2 billion reading is treated as stale. Deal communications also cite over 100 million global streaming households for Roku, against the last disclosed metric of 89.8 million at Q4 2024; both are carried with their dates.

Momentum accelerated into Q1 2026: total net revenue of $1.25 billion (up 22%), platform revenue up 28% to $1.13 billion (Advertising $612.7 million, Subscriptions $518.5 million), net income of $85.7 million, and adjusted EPS of $0.57 against Wall Street estimates of $0.32 to $0.35, a 71% to 78% beat. Monetization runs through the Roku Data Cloud (a clean room built on Snowflake's Media Data Cloud that matches ACR exposure data against IP addresses and advertiser CRM data), the OneView demand-side platform, and the self-serve Roku Ads Manager. None of the four passes isolates Roku Data Cloud or OneView revenue; both remain bundled inside Platform and Advertising reporting. This scale gave Roku near-monopolistic leverage over the Identity and Glass measurement layers, and its absolute reliance on ad monetization is precisely the exposure the Fox transaction resolved.

## The Fox-Roku convergence: a super-gatekeeper under construction

On June 15, 2026, Fox Corporation announced a definitive agreement, unanimously approved by both boards and backed by Roku founder Anthony Wood, to acquire Roku at approximately $22 billion in enterprise value. The transaction collapses the historical boundary between the two gatekeeper variants: a Variant B rights-chokepoint controller (NFL, MLB, FIFA World Cup, high-tier college athletics, Fox News) absorbing a Variant A hardware-telemetry engine.

| Fox-Roku transaction mechanics | Terms |
|---|---|
| Enterprise value | ~$22 billion |
| Consideration per Roku share | $160.00 ($96.00 cash plus 0.9693 FOXA shares; 60% cash component) |
| Financing | $12.0 billion fully committed bridge from Morgan Stanley Senior Funding; ~$8 billion in new transaction debt intended |
| Pro forma ownership | ~73% legacy Fox holders, ~27% Roku holders |
| Pro forma leverage | ~2.8x net leverage on trailing-twelve-month EBITDA, including a 50% credit for projected synergies |
| Projected cost synergies | ~$400 million annual run rate; free cash flow per share accretion by year two |
| Targeted close | First half of calendar 2027 |

The equity market reacted violently to the leverage profile and impending dilution: Fox shares plunged nearly 17% in the sessions after announcement, dragging the floating value of the package for Roku holders from the $160 headline to roughly $146 per share. The transaction remains conditional on antitrust and regulatory clearance, with particular scrutiny attached to a major content owner taking control of the leading independent television operating system. Anthony Wood joins the Fox board at close, and deal speculation alongside Roku's impending S&P MidCap 400 inclusion drove institutional buying into the stock.

The strategic rationale is a cure for Fox's programmatic deficit. Pre-deal Fox was an ad-dependent linear yield company: $1.56 billion in total ad revenue in Q1 2026, a successfully incubated Tubi (over 100 million monthly active users, revenue projected to approach $1.5 billion in fiscal 2026), but no authenticated first-party data, no hardware telemetry, and a legacy AdRise platform under Tubi that operates predominantly without user logins. Roku delivers hardwired access to more than 100 million global streaming households, chipset-level Automatic Content Recognition (ACR) that tracks viewing across the entire television glass regardless of app, and a full programmatic stack. The integration blueprint routes Fox Sports viewership data into the Roku Data Cloud for deterministic, lower-funnel attribution (linking an NFL or World Cup ad exposure to a digital checkout or app install), and opens premium sports inventory to small and medium businesses through the Roku Ads Manager, recently augmented by an API integration with the AI-powered social advertising platform Smartly: performance marketers can upload social creative into Fox and Tubi inventory and optimize for conversions, app installs, or Shopify cart additions via interactive Action Ads, with Shopify Checkout executable from the Roku remote. Lachlan Murdoch has framed the deal as a deliberate pivot into high-growth digital verticals, with Roku's home screen discovery engine routing viewers into Fox's live sports broadcasts; Fox also secures permanent home-screen placement for Tubi and Fox One, replicating the "territory" strategy of cable-era channel positioning.

On the consumer surface, Tubi and The Roku Channel remain distinct: The Roku Channel is an expansive FAST aggregator with over 600 linear-style channels wired into the Roku electronic program guide, while Tubi runs a curated AVOD library with roughly 150 live channels, and approximately 80% of The Roku Channel's FAST lineup does not exist on Tubi. Backend ad serving unifies through OneView and the Roku Ads Manager. Nielsen Gauge data from March 2026 put The Roku Channel at 3.0% and Tubi at 2.2% of total US streaming time; the combined entity is projected at 5.2% to 6.3% of US streaming time, surpassing Prime Video and trailing only YouTube and Netflix, in a FAST sector estimated to have generated $3.9 billion in advertising revenue in 2025.

One directive from the underlying research brief must be marked as unanswerable: the instruction to validate "actual operational execution" of the merger, including realization of the $400 million synergy target, is chronologically premature. The companies remain separate entities pending close in H1 2027; the falsification pass records the premise as invalid for now, while noting that the 17% sell-off signals market skepticism about the synergy math before the deal has even closed.

## Google and Disney: chokepoints without the clean-room posture

Google, through YouTube, is the ultimate distribution chokepoint independent of any data-withholding posture: YouTube captures more total US television viewing time than any other streaming platform, consistently surpassing Netflix. YouTube TV, scaled to approximately 8 million subscribers and anchored by NFL Sunday Ticket, functions as a tier-one virtual cable operator. The leverage was demonstrated in November 2025, when Disney pulled ESPN, ABC, and FX from YouTube TV for nearly two weeks in a carriage dispute and ultimately capitulated to Google's distribution scale: a stark inversion of the historical norm in which vertically integrated content owners dictated terms to distributors.

Disney operates as an embattled content fortress, generating gatekeeper-grade opacity through bundling rather than ad-tech: ESPN Unlimited at $29.99 per month, the Fubo acquisition, the ESPN-Fox One skinny bundle at $39.99 per month, and the NFL's 10% equity stake in ESPN together make it impossible for analysts to ascertain ESPN Unlimited's true churn or the survival rates of standalone versus bundled subscribers. The strategy carries regulatory cost: Disney agreed to a $50 million settlement in a class action antitrust suit alleging its bundling practices artificially pushed streaming subscription prices higher.

## Paramount Skydance/Warner Bros. Discovery: reclassified as prey

Initial ecosystem mapping hypothesized the combined PSKY and Warner Bros. Discovery entity as a Variant B gatekeeper on the assumption that a massive content portfolio confers structural power. The stress test rejects that classification: the entity is too leveraged and too distressed to gatekeep, and is reclassified as a vendor-layer subject, acted upon by actual gatekeepers.

The February 27, 2026 definitive agreement, engineered by David Ellison's Paramount Skydance, acquires 100% of Warner Bros. Discovery for $31.00 per share in cash, with a ticking fee of $0.25 per share per quarter (measured daily) if the deal has not closed by September 30, 2026. The transaction values WBD at $110.9 billion in enterprise value, 7.5x fully synergized 2026 EBITDA, and consolidates Harry Potter, Game of Thrones, Top Gun, Mission Impossible, and the DC Universe into what one source calls a "Sovereign of Cinema." The financing is the problem: WBD carried $17.8 billion in senior notes at the end of 2025; Paramount initially secured $54 billion in debt commitments from Bank of America, Citigroup, and Apollo, since restructured to a $49 billion senior secured bridge facility plus roughly $39.5 billion of first-lien permanent financing (per Paramount Skydance's 2026 8-K filings), alongside $47 billion in equity backed by the Ellison family, RedBird Capital Partners, and a sovereign consortium including Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, Abu Dhabi's L'imad Holding Company, the Qatar Investment Authority, and Tencent. The combined company is estimated to carry $79 billion of debt against roughly $35 billion of post-merger equity market capitalization: a debt load exceeding 200% of equity that film financier Joseph Singer has called financially unsustainable, even with management targeting 4.3x net debt to EBITDA at close.

That fragility structurally precludes gatekeeper behavior. The forced divestiture pattern has already begun: the loss of NBA broadcasting rights to Amazon is the canonical example of a true gatekeeper extracting premium assets from a distressed subject. Management's $6 billion-plus synergy target implies, per analyst estimates, termination of 20% to 40% of the combined workforce. Antitrust exposure is severe: DOJ Antitrust Division and California Attorney General scrutiny, WGA and SAG-AFTRA monopsony objections, the CBS-CNN news consolidation problem, and a highly unusual maneuver by Paramount chief legal officer Makan Delrahim to file HSR paperwork before the agreement was signed in order to compress the review window; James Cameron backed the Paramount deal publicly to avert a Netflix takeover. The entity's function in the ecosystem is now to feed the Demand/Valuation and Sports-Rights vendor layers with distressed assets that must be priced for fire-sale licensing.

## The Netflix exception, closed

Netflix historically sat outside the gatekeeper taxonomy: pure subscription revenue, superior first-party telemetry, no ads, no clean room, and a licensing-arbitrage strategy known as the "Netflix Paradox," with the NBCUniversal *Suits* deal (priced using demand data, generating $356 million in UCAN revenue) as the canonical case. All three broad passes agree the exception has now closed: Netflix is constructing the same withhold-and-monetize infrastructure as Amazon and Roku and has moved permanently into Variant A. The platform's underlying financials frame the shift: Q1 2026 revenue of $12.25 billion, up 16% year over year, at a 32.3% operating margin, with advertising revenue targeted at $3 billion for 2026, a doubling of 2025.

The headline analytical result is the resolution of the ad-tier scale discrepancy. Internal research had flagged an unresolved order-of-magnitude conflict between a 40 million global user figure and a 250 million monthly active user figure. The conflict is not structural; it is chronological growth compounded by a deliberate metric redefinition:

| Reporting period | Disclosed metric | Scale | Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| Launch (November 2022) | Ad-tier sign-ups | ~1 million | Slow initial adoption |
| Upfronts (May 2024) | Monthly active users | 40 million | Announced by Amy Reinhard, President of Advertising; up from 5 million a year prior, driven by password-sharing crackdowns and ad-free price hikes |
| May 2025 | Monthly active users | 94 million | Interim disclosure |
| November 2025 | Monthly active viewers | 190 million | Household-multiplier methodology adopted |
| Upfronts (May 2026) | Monthly active viewers | Over 250 million | Confirmed two years after the 40 million baseline |

The redefinition is the load-bearing detail. In late 2025, anticipating its retirement of quarterly subscriber reporting, Netflix switched its advertising metric from a subscriber count to "Monthly Active Viewers": any paying account streaming at least one minute of ad-supported content per month, multiplied by the estimated number of individuals in that household, with the multiplier derived from Netflix's own proprietary research rather than Nielsen or Comscore. Against roughly 325 million total global paid subscribers across all tiers in 2026, Netflix likely holds roughly 70 million to 90 million actual ad-supported accounts by mid-2026; a household multiplier of 2.5 to 3.0 individuals produces the 250 million figure. The falsification pass records the original "unresolved discrepancy" framing as contradicted: there was never an accounting conflict, only 24 months of growth (a 525% increase in the stated metric) plus a vocabulary change that aligns Netflix with broadcast-style audience currency. One genuine conflict between the passes remains open on adoption mix: the falsification pass reports approximately 45% of all new US sign-ups electing the ad tier as of May 2026, while the structural economics audit reports approximately 60% of new sign-ups choosing it across markets where the tier is available as of Q1 2026. The scopes differ (US versus all ad markets), but the spread does not fully reconcile and both figures are carried. Over 80% of ad-supported viewers stream weekly, neutralizing agency concerns about dormant accounts.

The infrastructure build-out is comprehensive. Netflix abandoned its rushed 2022 Microsoft ad-tech outsourcing for the proprietary in-house Netflix Ads Suite, rolled out in the US and Canada in 2025 and globally through 2026. Programmatic access runs through The Trade Desk, Google Display & Video 360, Yahoo DSP, Magnite, and Amazon DSP. To prove inventory quality inside a walled garden that refuses third-party pixels, Netflix built a clean-room substrate: Snowflake Data Clean Rooms (available immediately to partners) and AWS, with InfoSum and LiveRamp integrations completing by the end of 2026, allowing advertisers to match first-party purchase data against Netflix viewing telemetry via tokenized identifiers and cryptographically verify audience overlap, reach, frequency, and last-touch attribution without either side exposing raw personally identifiable information. DoubleVerify and Integral Ad Science are integrated for viewability, traffic authentication, and connected TV bot-fraud protection. The Audience Insights API and Reach Curve API expose viewing behavior to media planners; AI buying agents autonomously manage campaigns and dynamically adjust frequency caps; generative AI adapts advertiser creative into interactive pause ads and vertical video formats, with successful tests from DoorDash and Target. The company deployed its INKubator AI animation studio to scale production velocity while cutting per-title cost. Geographic expansion pushes the ad tier from 12 core markets into 15 new territories starting 2027: Austria, Belgium, Colombia, Denmark, Indonesia, Ireland, the Netherlands, New Zealand, Norway, Peru, the Philippines, Poland, Sweden, Switzerland, and Thailand, targeting APAC and LATAM volume. With live sports (NFL Christmas Day games, WWE Raw) and more Nielsen Top 10 originals in 2025 than any competitor, Netflix now runs the Amazon playbook squarely: the house controls the data, dictates the metrics, and wins the transaction.

## The reverse flow: the ecosystem taxation model

Prior research passes on this layer covered only the forward flow: what the gatekeepers withhold and the vendor demand that withholding creates. The reverse-flow audit closes that gap by mapping what moves in the opposite direction: capital, data, and intellectual property flowing from the seven vendor layers back up to Layer 0 through inventory expropriation, data licensing, programmatic API tolls, mandatory cloud compute, and algorithmic feedback agreements. The unidirectional model of gatekeepers as passive data hoarders is empirically dead; they are active toll collectors taxing the ecosystem their opacity spawned. As noted in the orientation, the falsification pass disputes only the "undocumented" label, finding the flow plainly visible in public disclosures; the mechanics below are agreed across sources.

### The distribution toll: Roku's 30% inventory expropriation

The most direct tax sits at the point of distribution. Per Roku's distribution agreements and developer documentation, any ad-supported application on the platform is forced by default into an "Inventory Split" framework: the publisher routes 30% of its total advertising inventory to Roku, which retains 100% of the revenue generated on that expropriated slice while assuming no fill obligation; the publisher controls the remaining 70%. Applications lacking their own ad-serving infrastructure can instead enter the Roku Sales Representation Program, routing 100% of inventory through Roku's demand-side platform in exchange for a 70/30 or 80/20 revenue share back to the publisher depending on the carriage agreement. The mandate strips billions in programmatic value from the open ecosystem and consolidates it inside Roku's platform segment.

### The hardware premium: OEM licensing of ACR telemetry

The Glass layer cannot function without Automatic Content Recognition data captured at the chipset level of smart televisions, and the vendors do not own the hardware. Vizio's Inscape unit, with an opt-in footprint of over 24 million devices, licenses telemetry to virtually every measurement challenger: iSpot.tv, VideoAmp, Comscore, EDO, and Nielsen. Inscape generated $121 million in non-advertising platform revenue in 2022, the vast majority from licensing telemetry to the alternative data ecosystem. VideoAmp's trajectory toward an estimated $6 billion in 2026 upfront transaction volume depends entirely on a multi-year Inscape licensing extension feeding its VALID (VideoAmp Linked Identity & Data) engine; Comscore expanded its Inscape pact in late 2024. LG Electronics, through LG Ad Solutions, executed a 4.5-year exclusive licensing deal giving iSpot measurement access to ACR data from more than 20 million opted-in LG smart TVs, roughly doubling iSpot's licensed footprint to about 40 million connected TVs when combined with its Vizio access. Samba TV, with embedded OEM ACR across Sony, Sharp, and TCL sets reaching roughly 110 million global screens, operates as a hybrid gatekeeper-vendor, self-monetizing through data-as-a-service and managed media. The exact licensing terms are trade secrets, but the structure is transparent: vendors bear the costs of panel weighting, sales operations, and Joint Industry Committee certification while the OEMs extract pure margin at the top of the supply chain.

Walmart's $2.3 billion acquisition of Vizio converts this into a dual-track strategy. Externally, Inscape continues licensing ACR at arm's length, routed through authorized intermediaries to limit Video Privacy Protection Act class-action exposure. Internally, Walmart is absorbing the footprint (the reverse-flow audit describes it as 40 million-plus smart TVs, against the 24 million-plus opted-in device figure carried by the other passes; both are stated here as an open discrepancy) to fuel Walmart Connect's closed-loop retail media flywheel: global ad revenue of $4.4 billion in FY2025 (+27%) and $6.4 billion in FY2026 (+46%), per Walmart's own SEC 8-K filings, a Luminate-powered clean room forcing closed-loop attribution inside Walmart's servers, unified account logins across Vizio OS, and shoppable content pilots with major CPG brands. If Walmart restricts external licensing to protect its retail-media advantage, the foundational telemetry under the independent measurement currencies evaporates.

### Programmatic tolls: the API fee architecture

Macro analyses by the World Federation of Advertisers and the Association of National Advertisers find that of every $1,000 invested in programmatic advertising, only 43.9% reaches consumers as viewable impressions on valuable inventory; 56.1% is consumed by the supply chain. Within that chain, the gatekeepers have hard-coded their tolls into API architecture. The Amazon Ads API carries explicit fields (`3pFeeDoubleVerify`, `3pFeeIntegralAdScience`) that apply a cost-per-mille surcharge to impressions verified by third parties, with the advertiser electing whether the fee is added on top of or deducted from the line-item budget; the fees are itemized and absorbed by the advertiser, but the vendors must negotiate underlying access agreements with Amazon to operate at all. Google's Display & Video 360 runs the identical architecture: a baseline platform fee of roughly 10% to 15% of media spend on open auction inventory, plus partner cost parameters (`PARTNER_COST_TYPE_DOUBLE_VERIFY_PREBID`, `PARTNER_COST_TYPE_INTEGRAL_AD_SCIENCE_PREBID`) that enforce CPM fees in micros of the advertiser's currency, with billable events aggregating as advertisers stack brand safety, suspicious activity, and viewability thresholds. Because Google and Amazon own both the demand-side routing and the underlying supply (YouTube, Prime Video), vendors have no alternative but to comply, converting the independent measurement industry into a captive client base of the platforms it audits.

### The infrastructure trap: clean rooms as compute sinks

Gatekeepers extract a second layer of capital by forcing measurement into proprietary cloud environments. Google banned third-party measurement pixels on YouTube and migrated Comscore, DoubleVerify, Kantar, Integral Ad Science, and Nielsen into Ads Data Hub, a clean room built on Google Cloud Platform that prohibits raw data export; all querying and joining of CRM data against Google ad exposure runs on GCP BigQuery projects, with vendors paying Google Cloud compute, processing, and storage costs. Vendors pay Google to audit Google. Amazon runs the same play through AMC on AWS: no base license fee and free standard queries, with the real cost embedded in AWS billing, tooling, and the paid signals described in the Amazon section above. Roku replicates it through the Roku Data Cloud on Snowflake's Media Data Cloud: members of the Roku Measurement Partner Program (historically over twenty certified partners, including Nielsen, Kantar, VideoAmp, and Comscore) receive matched analyses but must operate within OneView's confines, ensuring Roku controls match rates and monetizes downstream activation.

### Algorithmic feedback: the Roku-iSpot intellectual property transfer

The most consequential reverse flow is not fees but intelligence. In early 2026, following a $325 million iSpot investment round led by Goldman Sachs, Roku became the first major streaming publisher to integrate iSpot's "Outcomes at Scale" deterministic attribution product directly into its native advertising optimization engine. Rather than delivering isolated post-campaign reports, iSpot's conversion and attribution signals now feed Roku's algorithms in real time, letting the platform autonomously recalibrate bids, adjust creative strategy, and optimize for mid-funnel web activity in flight; Roku's Senior Director of Strategic Advertising Partnerships described the integration as iSpot attribution "powering Roku's optimization engine." This is a documented transfer of intellectual property from the Glass/measurement layer into Layer 0: the vendor outsources the machine-learning training of the gatekeeper's connected TV demand-side platform, and the long-term enterprise value of its data accrues to the gatekeeper's stack.

### The Wallet parallel: bank API tolls

The taxation pattern is systemic, not media-specific. Wallet-layer transaction panels (YipitData, Consumer Edge, Antenna) historically relied on aggregators like Plaid and Yodlee scraping consumer banking data at near-zero marginal cost. The 2025 JPMorgan Chase-Plaid data-access agreement replaced scraping with tokenized API connections carrying per-pull pricing, estimated to cost aggregators up to $300 million annually in fractional-cent fees; JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, and Wells Fargo have together established the fractional-cent per-pull precedent, converting data acquisition from a fixed infrastructure cost into a variable, high-margin revenue stream for the data originators. Combined with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's Section 1033 rules and California's Delete Act (DROP), which imposes $200 daily penalties for non-compliance with centralized deletion requests, the economics are forcing a capital-intensive pivot from passive aggregation to zero-party incentivized panels in which consumers are paid directly for their data.

## The Shield layer: integration or absorption, an open conflict

The passes disagree fundamentally on the Shield layer's trajectory, and the conflict is stated here as open rather than resolved.

The integration case, argued by the falsification pass: the buy side enforces a strict penalty on closed-loop ecosystems that verify their own delivery, with media agencies actively refusing to allocate premium programmatic budgets to platforms lacking independent verification. The sector's financials and deal flow support expansion, not collapse. Integral Ad Science was taken private by Novacap in late 2025 at approximately $1.9 billion, a 22% premium, folding the verification engine into a private equity platform consolidating a vertically integrated programmatic supply chain alongside Cadent and AdTheorent, with Lidiane Jones appointed CEO in July 2026 to drive an enterprise AI mandate. DoubleVerify, now the sole public proxy for the layer, reported FY2024 revenue of $656.8 million (up 15%, driven by a 95% year-over-year surge in connected TV measurement volume in Q4 2024), Q1 2026 revenue of $180.8 million (up 10%), adjusted EBITDA margins of 33% (FY2024) and 31% (Q1 2026), and net revenue retention of 112%. In June 2026 it launched DV Authentic AdVantage across Meta and TikTok, combining pre-bid protection, AI-powered bidding optimization, and independent measurement inside the walled gardens themselves, optimizing roughly 25 billion impressions monthly. Netflix's integration of both DoubleVerify and Integral Ad Science points the same direction: gatekeepers yielding to third-party verification to secure ad budgets.

The absorption case, argued by the reverse-flow audit: the 2025-2026 securities fraud class action against DoubleVerify in the Southern District of New York documents the cost of walled-garden dependence. As AI-generated invalid traffic surged on open exchanges (an Adalytics Research report exposed DoubleVerify failing to detect bot networks operating from known data center server farms, and Google was accused of mis-selling video through the Google Video Partners program in violation of its own TrueView standards, serving premium ads against AI slop), advertisers shifted budgets into Meta, Google, TikTok, and Amazon. The complaint alleges that integrating DoubleVerify's high-margin Activation Services into those closed platforms was "significantly more expensive and time-consuming than disclosed to investors." On February 27, 2025, after lackluster Q4 results acknowledging the damage, the stock fell 36% in a single day to $13.90, with management conceding a one-to-three-year runway to profitability on social activation. Inside the walled gardens, verifiers see only what platform APIs permit, leaving open the possibility that platforms cherry-pick data and reduce the vendors to rubber stamps on self-reported metrics: paid coverage vastly inferior to open-web verification. On this reading, Roku's own architecture illustrates the co-optation: its proprietary Advertising Watermark authenticates inventory inside OneView, certification rules require all measurement beacons to fire client-side through the Roku Advertising Framework, and DoubleVerify feeds Fraud Lab signals and SmokeScreen mitigation directly into Roku's ecosystem; the gatekeeper captures the CPM premium of a clean ecosystem while the verifier collects fractional-cent Measured Transaction Fees. With programmatic CTV fraud up an estimated 140% year over year into 2026, the audit reads current third-party integration as a temporary patch tolerated only until native AI safeguards reach feature parity, at which point platforms hold unilateral authority to terminate contracts and restrict API access.

Both readings rest on real evidence separated by time: the litigation captures the 2025 shock, the integration case captures the 2026 recovery and expansion. Whether DV Authentic AdVantage represents durable structural defense or a tolerated interim arrangement is the single largest unresolved question in this layer.

## The identity fracture: LiveRamp loses neutrality

The Identity/Clean Rooms layer is the connective substrate that lets Wallet transaction data match against Glass viewership data without exchanging raw personally identifiable information, and its independence has now fractured. LiveRamp, historically the neutral "Switzerland of data" whose RampID infrastructure achieved 99.5% match rates across competing networks, studios, and panels, was acquired by Publicis Groupe in May 2026; one pass records the deal at a $2.546 billion equity value and another at a $2.167 billion enterprise value (different valuation bases, both carried). Vertical integration into one agency holding company disincentivizes rival networks from routing client data through a competitor's infrastructure: WPP steers toward its InfoSum asset, Omnicom toward Acxiom, and cross-platform identity resolution splinters. The beneficiaries are the Layer 0 gatekeepers, whose deterministic identity graphs (Amazon Marketing Cloud, Roku Data Cloud, Google Ads Data Hub) become the default, taxing all multi-touch attribution executed inside them. The Trade Desk's open-source Unified ID 2.0 is left as the sole remaining open identity alternative heading into the 2027 programmatic upfronts.

## What each vendor layer answers to

The remaining layer-by-layer mapping, condensed to what is not already covered above. The Wallet layer (YipitData, Consumer Edge, Antenna, Bloomberg Second Measure, M Science) exists because Apple and Amazon bury standalone ARPU, plan-mix, and churn inside services lines and Disney obscures ESPN Unlimited economics through bundling; the vendors isolate actual billing relationships through card streams and bank APIs, and the gatekeepers benefit from the regulatory friction (Section 1033, the Delete Act) that degrades that reverse-engineering. The Glass layer's attention sub-niche (EDO, TVision) builds human-engagement verification atop the ACR infrastructure; Apple TV boxes and Fire TV sticks remain closed ecosystems limiting third-party ACR reach, and the Fox-Roku deal removes a further swath of connected TV inventory from independent measurement. The Demand/Valuation layer (Parrot Analytics, PlumResearch under Ampere Analysis, Gracenote, Whip Media) exists because Amazon's monopsony doctrine denies studios the ratings needed to price residuals; CAA and WME lean on synthetic demand indices for packaging, and the distressed PSKY-WBD entity now feeds the layer volume as it prices library assets for fire-sale licensing. The Funnel layer (JustWatch, Reelgood, Sensor Tower) reads app-store velocity and watchlist additions as leading indicators because gatekeepers guard customer acquisition costs; Fox's home-screen placement of Tubi and Fox One is a deliberate move to internalize discovery and starve the layer of intent signals. The Sports-Rights layer (Genius Sports (GENI) on NFL data, Stats Perform on FIFA data) operates mandatory tollbooths for optical tracking, betting-handle correlation, and blackout compliance, but the gatekeepers are integrating vertically around it: Disney through the NFL equity structure, Amazon through the NBA rights seizure, Apple through the closed MLS loop.

## Relative centrality and outlook

Gatekeeper leverage is not proportionate to gatekeeper dependence. For Amazon, Apple, and Google, streaming media is a strategic side effect of much larger machines (retail and cloud, hardware, search), which lets them sustain content losses indefinitely to acquire share and telemetry. For Roku and Fox, the ecosystem is existentially central: $4.14 billion of Roku's $4.74 billion in revenue is platform-derived, and the $22 billion acquisition was a structural necessity for both sides, transforming Fox from a declining linear programmer into a connected TV data monetizer. Netflix sits between: all media, but its sprint into ads, clean rooms, and identity infrastructure is an explicit acknowledgment that subscription arbitrage alone no longer sustains dominance.

The consolidated outlook across the four passes: margin compression in the vendor layers is structural rather than cyclical, driven by walled-garden integration costs and compute tolls; measurement vendors are drifting from independent arbiters toward outsourced research and development for gatekeeper optimization engines; and verification inside platforms that control the feed, the auction, the data, and the clean room risks becoming ratification rather than audit. Valuation work on this sector should therefore center on the resilience of data pipelines, the leverage required to acquire them, and each vendor's ability to remain relevant inside increasingly fortified borders. Absent regulatory intervention mandating open data standards or interoperability, the vendor layers trend toward operating as taxed subsidiaries of the walled gardens they were built to circumvent.
