# Layer 2 Focused Research: Glass (Hardware / ACR)

Layer 2, colloquially known as The Glass, is the foundational substrate of the streaming alt-data architecture: hardware manufacturers, smart television operating systems, and Automatic Content Recognition (ACR) telemetry vendors capturing deterministic, second-by-second viewing data directly from the screen's pixels, independent of whichever streaming application or set-top box is routing the content. As of mid-2026 the layer is under systemic stress on three fronts at once, and this focused pass audits all three: the Walmart-Vizio Inscape monetization split, the Video Privacy Protection Act (VPPA) circuit split now before the Supreme Court with its $2,500-per-violation exposure, and the Nielsen currency disconnect running through the August 31, 2026 Media Rating Council deadline and the emerging shareholder harm litigation vector.

## The Walmart-Vizio split: one dataset, two masters

Walmart's acquisition of Vizio for approximately $2.3 billion, completed in late 2024 and fully integrated by the 2025/2026 fiscal cycles, is the defining structural event of this layer. Before the deal, Vizio's data unit Inscape operated as a largely neutral arms dealer, licensing the ACR data thrown off by its opted-in smart television footprint to competing measurement vendors. Since the deal, Walmart runs a dual-track strategy: internally, it feeds Inscape's raw telemetry into Walmart Connect, its high-margin retail media network; externally, it keeps extracting licensing fees from the very independent alternative currencies its own advertising stack may eventually disintermediate.

### The internal track: the retail media flywheel

Walmart's strategic imperative was never hardware margin; it was the deterministic linkage of living room attention to physical and digital retail checkout. Streaming now accounts for more than half of all television viewing time according to Vizio Inscape ACR data from the fourth quarter of 2025, yet content, commerce, and measurement have historically stayed fragmented. By absorbing Inscape's telemetry (viewing patterns from over 24 million active, opted-in devices, representative of an estimated 79 million people) Walmart bridged ad exposure and consumer transaction.

The operational execution surfaced at the 2026 IAB NewFronts. Walmart and Vizio introduced a unified account login across Vizio OS televisions and Walmart's proprietary "onn" brand televisions, letting consumers use their Walmart retail accounts to access smart television features. That login constructs a deterministic identity framework linking streaming engagement to Walmart's first-party shopper base of approximately 150 million weekly United States customers shopping online and in-store, per Walmart's NewFronts announcements. The result is a closed loop in which advertisers measure actual, SKU-level sales driven by CTV impressions with no probabilistic matching and no third-party cookies.

The flywheel is already producing commercial proof points. At the same NewFronts, Walmart announced a first-to-market shoppable product placement integration with L'Oréal: powered by Walmart's first-party customer insights, L'Oréal products are placed directly within premium content across the Vizio OS, with creative extensions spanning Walmart's digital product pages, a seamless path from inspiration to checkout. Early Walmart Connect CTV performance metrics: successful advertiser campaigns delivered a median viewing completion rate of 44%, campaigns for brands such as Cafe Bustelo drove 98% incremental household reach beyond traditional linear television, and 65% of surveyed Walmart customers reported that CTV advertisements helped them discover new products.

To scale the infrastructure, Walmart opened Vizio inventory within Walmart Connect via Yahoo's demand-side platform through ad-tech provider Magnite, simplifying activation for mid-market brands and marketplace sellers, and separately partnered with Google's Display & Video 360 (DV360) so brands can use Walmart audience data to manage and measure YouTube campaigns. The DV360 tie-up signals a retail media landscape reorganizing around shared measurement infrastructure rather than isolated walled gardens.

### The external track: licensing durability and alternative currency dependence

Walmart cannot immediately cut off external syndication of Inscape data without sacrificing hundreds of millions in high-margin software and licensing revenue. The software layer of the United States ACR market delivered 62.05% of total industry revenue in 2025, driven by licensing of the video, audio, and image fingerprint engines sitting inside smart TV operating systems, and the overall US ACR market is projected to grow from $1.29 billion in 2026 to $2.24 billion by 2031 at an 11.72% compound annual growth rate, per Mordor Intelligence's US ACR market report. Walmart therefore has a vested interest, for now, in staying a data supplier.

The external track is arm's-length telemetry licensing to the major independent measurement vendors, primarily VideoAmp, iSpot.tv, and Comscore, which rely on it to operate as Joint Industry Committee certified transactional currencies. The alternative currencies fundamentally depend on the sheer scale of the 24 million device Vizio footprint to generate statistical significance for second-by-second impression forecasting. Contract durability varies materially across the four:

| Vendor | Inscape licensing status | Reliance on Inscape | Broader footprint and diversification |
|---|---|---|---|
| VideoAmp | Renewed multi-year extension, early 2025 | Critical; foundational input for the VALID big data engine | 40M homes, 65M devices; integrates SiriusXM and National CineMedia |
| iSpot.tv | Contract active through end of 2025 (per body text; see the 7.5-year citation lead above) | High; essential for initial ad cataloging and conversion analytics | 40M+ TVs; exclusive 4.5-year LG Ad Solutions deal (20M TVs) plus TiVo set-top box data |
| Comscore | Renewed and expanded, late 2024 | High; essential for local station tuning and cross-platform deduplication | Integrates Comcast, AT&T, Dish, and Samba TV data |
| Nielsen | Multi-year licenses active | Moderate; supplements traditional panels | 41,600 proprietary panel homes; large set-top box partnerships |

VideoAmp is the most deeply tethered. Its early 2025 multi-year extension with Inscape feeds VALID (VideoAmp Linked Identity & Data), the big data and tech engine that commingles ACR with set-top box feeds and proprietary clean room technology for interoperable cross-screen measurement across walled gardens and digital publishers. The expanded deal also grants VideoAmp access to Inscape's Tuner Data solution, capturing over-the-air viewership across more than 5,000 local stations, and the Commercial Feed + dataset, which deduplicates exposures and expands streaming commercial coverage.

iSpot.tv built its ad verification and cross-screen measurement business heavily on Inscape, diversifying its supply chain in late 2021 with the exclusive 4.5-year licensing deal covering 20 million smart TVs from LG Ad Solutions; its Inscape contract is described as active through the end of 2025. Combined, iSpot works from a footprint of nearly 40 million connected televisions, layering Inscape's screen-level behavioral data with TiVo's deterministic set-top box data for closed-loop attribution and attention metrics at scale.

Comscore renewed and expanded its six-year Inscape partnership in late 2024, relying on Inscape's National Representative Panel dataset and newly introduced streaming ad data for its television measurement and ratings products. It has developed a patent-pending method to deduplicate smart TV and set-top box data so viewers are not counted multiple times across fragmented devices, a capability central to its direct competition against Nielsen and VideoAmp for 2026 upfront transaction volume on the strength of its Joint Industry Committee certifications.

### The firewall: keeping the margin, exporting the liability

The tension between the two tracks is the single highest-return diligence question in this layer: if Walmart eventually restricts external licensing to maximize its own retail media advantage, the foundational ACR telemetry underpinning independent alternative currency upfront volume evaporates at once. The institutional market already recognizes the fragility; open questions about the long-term availability of Inscape data for currency use are pushing measurement providers to build systems robust to the sudden loss of a footprint partner.

Meanwhile Walmart extracts pure margin from the external relationships while insulating itself through contractual firewalls, the clearest example being the Data Authorization Agreement required for iSpot customers using Vizio's Integrated User Level Data (terms published via inscape.tv's iSpot legal page and iSpot's services agreements). Vizio explicitly states it is not a party to the overarching iSpot Customer Agreement; iSpot acts as the authorized intermediary, managing data matching, cloud hosting, and nightly file transfers to AWS buckets. The agreement mandates that any "Direct Matching" be performed internally by the client using IP addresses as the match key, explicitly excluding third-party data brokers; requires IP addresses and Mobile Device IDs to be stored at rest under AES-256 encryption; prohibits use of the data for ad targeting or retargeting when the client receives the Integrated User Level Data; and absolutely forbids assignment of the agreement or sharing of data with any entity deemed a "Vizio Competitor." Vizio caps its maximum aggregate liability at an infinitesimal amount, shifts all indemnification onto the client, and accepts no liability for any act or omission by iSpot. By routing data through a legally insulated intermediary, Walmart shields its balance sheet from the privacy class-action exposure that historically plagues direct data brokers, keeping the financial upside while pushing the compliance burden and the statutory exposure downward onto the vendors.

## VPPA: the statute that prices every tracked impression at $2,500

Those firewalls are not theoretical corporate hygiene; they are direct responses to live statutory exposure that threatens the basic mechanics of ACR data and tracking pixels. The Video Privacy Protection Act, enacted in 1988 after a Washington, D.C. newspaper published the video cassette rental history of Supreme Court nominee Robert Bork, prohibits a "video tape service provider" from knowingly disclosing personally identifiable information about any "consumer" without written, informed consent. Modestly modernized by the 2012 "Netflix Amendment," the statute has been aggressively weaponized by the plaintiff class-action bar against streaming platforms, digital publishers, and smart TV manufacturers. The primary targets are website advertising technologies, specifically the Meta Pixel and Google Analytics, which capture a user's digital video viewing history and transmit it alongside unique identifiers (a Facebook ID, an IP address, device cookies) to third-party ad networks. Because the VPPA carries liquidated damages of $2,500 per violation, every tracked video impression is a potential liability trigger for the Glass layer's entire syndication model.

### The circuit split, case by case

The crisis turns on the statutory definition of a "consumer": "any renter, purchaser, or subscriber of goods or services from a video tape service provider." The contested question is whether those goods or services must be audio-visual, or whether subscribing to anything (an email newsletter, say) from a company that also hosts video suffices to trigger liability.

The broad reading came first. In October 2024 the Second Circuit ruled in Salazar v. National Basketball Association (No. 23-1147) for plaintiff Michael Salazar, who had signed up for a free NBA email newsletter, later watched free public videos on the NBA site, and alleged the Meta Pixel transmitted his viewing history and Facebook ID to Meta without consent. The district court had dismissed, holding a text newsletter subscription did not make him a subscriber to audio-visual services. A unanimous Second Circuit panel reversed on strict textualist grounds: the "video tape service provider" definition requires audio-visual materials, but the "consumer" definition contains no such limitation and uses inclusive language like "any," so a subscriber to any good or service of the business qualifies. The court further held that exchange of value requires no money; trading an email address, IP address, and device cookies for a free newsletter establishes a statutory subscription. The Seventh Circuit reinforced this in early 2025 in Gardner v. MeTV National Ltd. Partnership, holding an email address and zip code created the requisite subscriber relationship, with no monetary exchange and no video nexus required.

The narrow reading answered in April 2025, when the Sixth Circuit ruled in Salazar v. Paramount Global (No. 23-5748), a nearly identical fact pattern involving the same serial plaintiff. Salazar alleged he became a VPPA consumer by signing up for a free daily newsletter from 247Sports.com, a college sports recruiting site owned by Paramount (now Paramount Skydance, PSKY), then watched videos while logged into Facebook, with the Meta Pixel transmitting his history and Facebook ID. The Sixth Circuit explicitly rejected the Second Circuit's logic, holding the statute's natural, contextual reading makes someone a consumer only when subscribing to goods or services "in the nature of video cassette tapes or similar audio visual materials," affirmed dismissal, and emphasized the statute's physical rental era context: a newsletter with incidental video access is not an audio-visual subscription, and treating any interaction with a video-enabled site as one ignores the specific privacy harms Congress meant to remedy. The D.C. Circuit aligned with the narrow view in Pileggi v. Washington Newspaper Publishing Co. in August 2025.

### The Supreme Court's binary

The split produced open jurisdictional arbitrage: plaintiffs forum-shopping $2,500-per-violation class actions into the Second and Seventh Circuits, defendants seeking refuge in the Sixth and D.C. Circuits. The Supreme Court granted certiorari in Salazar v. Paramount Global (Docket No. 25-459) on January 26, 2026 (coverage per Paul Weiss and Morrison Foerster client memos, among others). The parties' joint briefing schedule sets the petitioner's brief for April 17, 2026, the respondent's for June 23, 2026, and replies for July 23, 2026, with oral argument anticipated in the October 2026 term and a ruling expected in the first half of 2027.

The outcome is binary for this layer. If the Court upholds the broad Second Circuit interpretation, any company using tracking pixels to map video exposure to downstream conversions (the exact product architecture sold by attention verification vendors like EDO and by alternative currencies like VideoAmp and iSpot) faces class-action liability whenever users interact with any generic account, loyalty program, or newsletter on the host platform; a single popular website running a Meta Pixel could generate hundreds of millions of dollars in statutory exposure, effectively destroying the economics of passive ACR calibration and pixel-based attribution. If the Court adopts the Sixth Circuit's narrow reading, the recent wave of VPPA actions against video-embedded websites is severely curtailed and platforms using video as ancillary engagement get a safe harbor. Until the ruling, corporate legal departments are running emergency audits of pixel payloads, decoupling video access from non-video subscriptions, and treating VPPA exposure as a discrete, highly toxic diligence stream in mergers and acquisitions.

## Nielsen: the February 2026 Gauge suppression

The third fault line is the breakdown of the legacy currency itself. The alternative data ecosystem depends on a universally accepted measurement standard for upfront transactions, a monopoly Nielsen held effortlessly until the Media Rating Council suspended its accreditation in 2021 over severe pandemic-era undercounting. Its transition to a modernized "Big Data + Panel" currency has been plagued by methodological instability, and in early 2026 that instability went public through The Gauge, Nielsen's heavily cited monthly snapshot of US viewing time split between linear television and streaming, historically weaponized by Netflix and YouTube to narrate linear's terminal decline.

To address perceived demographic inaccuracies, and per requests from the Media Rating Council, Nielsen began incorporating an independent demographic study from the Advertising Research Foundation (ARF) to adjust its estimates of US household composition and technology adoption. When February 2026 viewing data was processed under the new methodology, the unreleased figures showed a massive reversal, amplified by an unusually strong linear month built on NBCUniversal's exclusive broadcasts of Super Bowl LX and the 2026 Winter Olympics:

| Platform / medium | January 2026 share (prior methodology) | February 2026 unreleased share (ARF methodology) |
|---|---|---|
| Linear TV (total) | 42.7% | 47.4% |
| Streaming (total) | 47.0% | 41.9% |
| YouTube | 12.5% | 11.0% |
| Netflix | 8.8% | 7.5% |
| Amazon Prime Video | 4.1% | 3.3% |
| Roku | 3.0% | 2.4% |
| Peacock (NBCU) | 1.9% | 2.7% |

Peacock was the only major platform to gain, on its live sports simulcasts, lifting NBCUniversal's combined cable and digital share to 13.1%. The streamers, facing data that undermined their growth narrative, objected aggressively to the ARF-backed methodology on seeing the preliminary figures. Nielsen first delayed the February Gauge (originally scheduled for March 17, 2026) to give streamers more evaluation time, then suppressed the ARF-backed data entirely and reverted to the prior methodology that favored streaming, citing the need to avoid a "one-time shift in viewing data." The MRC stated that its 2025 audit of Nielsen's service remains open and that Nielsen would delay implementation of the MRC-mandated modeling and weighting fixes until August 31, 2026, well past the spring upfront selling season. One source-internal tension to log: the source's citation list carries a May 2026 TheDesk.net headline, "MRC reaffirms accreditation for Nielsen's Big Data + Panels currency," which the body text never develops and which sits awkwardly beside the open-audit characterization; carried here as an unreconciled lead.

The suppression ignited open warfare. On March 26, 2026, Sean Cunningham, President and CEO of the Video Advertising Bureau (the trade organization for major linear broadcasters), issued a formal statement accusing Nielsen of "obvious interference in markets," characterizing its actions as "indefensible manipulations" designed to artificially suppress linear audience totals during the most commercially sensitive period of the year, and accusing it of "public kowtowing to Google" and "cheerleading YouTube boom / TV gloom." The VAB's argument: Nielsen's reversion systematically undercounted traditional television exactly while billions in advertising commitments were being negotiated, paralyzing buyers and planners.

### From methodology dispute to shareholder harm

Methodology fights between Nielsen and the networks are a historical constant; what changed in 2026 is the legal vector. NBCUniversal advertising chief Mark Marshall, criticizing the suppression in a Wall Street Journal interview, said pointedly: "Every media company is valued on a multiple of something, right?" That framing moves the dispute onto valuation terrain: if audience data is systematically wrong and advertising revenue consequently understated, the market values of publicly traded linear broadcasters are materially and unfairly damaged.

Media sector analyst Ian Whittaker drew the implication explicitly: this shifts Nielsen's problem from a panel disagreement with angry networks into the realm of securities regulators and class-action plaintiff firms. By letting billions in upfront capital transact on an uncorrected currency while acknowledging the required MRC fixes will not land until August 31, 2026, Nielsen has exposed itself to severe commercial liability. In Whittaker's words: "Nielsen's problem is no longer that the networks disagree with its panel. It is that the networks have chosen the terrain of shareholder harm, and that terrain is crowded with counterparties whose interest Nielsen cannot afford to invite in."

Streaming executives tried to wave it off. On Netflix's first-quarter 2026 earnings call on April 16, Co-CEO Greg Peters said of the Gauge change: "It is not a change in how people actually watch TV. It changes Nielsen's numbers, not actual viewing behaviours. Nielsen Gauge is not the currency for the video marketplace." Technically accurate (The Gauge is a share-of-viewing report, not the granular transaction currency for individual ad buys), but it sidesteps the epistemological crisis: the dispute now offers institutional investors a direct litigation pathway against the legacy provider, accelerating adoption of the ACR-backed alternative currencies, VideoAmp and iSpot above all, as indispensable infrastructure rather than challengers.

## Synthesis

The Glass layer in mid-2026 sits at the intersection of aggressive hardware monopolization, statutory privacy risk, and collapsed institutional trust in legacy measurement. Walmart-Vizio demonstrates that television attribution's future runs through closed-loop retail media: Inscape ACR powering Walmart Connect, deterministic links from living room to omnichannel checkout, and mid-market demand pulled in through Magnite and DV360. Yet the independent alternative currency market (VideoAmp, iSpot, Comscore) remains dangerously tethered to Walmart's willingness to keep syndicating, with Data Authorization Agreements underscoring how much liability the gatekeeper has already exported downward. The Supreme Court's pending Salazar v. Paramount Global review is a binary overhang on the legal architecture of ACR collection itself. And Nielsen's Gauge suppression plus its deliberate delay of MRC fixes to August 31, 2026 has converted the measurement wars from media buying optimization into matters of fiduciary duty and corporate liability. For the 2026-2027 cycle, the Glass layer is no longer a neutral measurement utility; it is a theater of corporate arbitrage, regulatory evasion, and legal warfare.
