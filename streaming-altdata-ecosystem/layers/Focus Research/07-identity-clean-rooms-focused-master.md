# Layer 7 Focused Research: Identity and Clean Rooms

Identity resolution and clean rooms sit beneath every other vendor layer in the streaming alt-data stack: the tokenized matching substrate that lets Wallet-level transaction data join Glass-level viewership telemetry without either party exchanging raw personally identifiable information. In an ad and media economy that has moved permanently from zero-interest-rate subscriber acquisition to free cash flow and unit-economic discipline, that substrate is the chokepoint everything else routes through, and this focused pass is organized around the single event that broke it: Publicis Groupe's May 2026 acquisition of LiveRamp and the neutrality fracture it triggered. The pass also closes the one gap both prior research passes on this layer shared, a dated read on the deal's regulatory and closing status; the source now supplies that, and it is flagged in its own section below.

## The stack beneath the fracture

For context, the source restates the seven vendor layers plus the gatekeeper tier the layer serves. Gatekeepers (Amazon, Apple, and Roku among them) withhold granular consumption data while monetizing their own proprietary first-party audiences; the multi-billion-dollar alternative data infrastructure below them exists to reverse-engineer those closed-loop silos, with each layer defined by its own collection methodology, buyer set, and compliance framework.

For over a decade this seventh layer was dominated by LiveRamp, which operated as the definitive independent "Switzerland of data" for the programmatic supply chain. Agencies, publishers, and rival data vendors relied on LiveRamp's RampID to execute cross-platform attribution, secure in the knowledge that they were not enriching a direct competitor. That structural neutrality fractured permanently in May 2026: by vertically integrating LiveRamp into its proprietary stack, Publicis triggered an immediate balkanization of the programmatic identity layer, forcing rival holding companies into defensive infrastructure migrations and altering the trajectory of open-source identity alternatives ahead of the 2027 media upfronts.

## The deal, deconstructed

On May 17, 2026, Publicis Groupe entered a definitive agreement to acquire LiveRamp in an all-cash transaction at $38.50 per share, a 29.8% premium to LiveRamp's closing price on May 15, 2026. The transaction established a total equity value of $2.546 billion and a total enterprise value of $2.167 billion, the difference accounted for by $379 million in acquired net cash. These figures match the previously verified values from Publicis Groupe's own May 17, 2026 press release exactly; no discrepancy to log.

The target's financial profile explains the price. In fiscal year 2026, LiveRamp reported $812.9 million in revenue, up 9% year over year, driven by a highly recurring SaaS subscription model that represented 76% of the business and carried a 107% customer revenue retention rate. LiveRamp generated record operating cash flow of $167.8 million alongside GAAP net earnings of $146 million. Publicis expects the transaction to be accretive to headline earnings per share from the first year of consolidation, projecting a 2.9% positive impact based on calendarized 2026 non-GAAP EBITDA of approximately $126 million and an anticipated $50 million in run-rate cost synergies.

Funding is cash on hand plus new financial debt, with new corporate bond issuance anticipated in the second half of 2026 and maximum net financial leverage of approximately 1.2 times entering 2027.

## Regulatory and closing status: the standing gap, now closed

Both prior passes on this layer left the deal's regulatory and closing status undated. This pass closes that gap. The acquisition had not closed as of the source's reporting: it remains subject to customary closing conditions, including a LiveRamp stockholder vote scheduled for August 17, 2026, antitrust clearance under the Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) Act, and review by the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS). The merger agreement stipulates a $32.35 million termination fee applicable to either party under specified scenarios in the event of regulatory obstruction or deal collapse. As of this consolidation date (2026-07-13), the shareholder vote sits roughly five weeks out and the source reports no clearance outcomes; status is signed and pending, not closed.

## The illusion of ongoing neutrality

Publicis has aggressively assured the market that LiveRamp will retain its structural neutrality. Per internal employee FAQs filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission on June 2, 2026, LiveRamp is slated to operate as an independent business within Publicis, with Scott Howe remaining Chief Executive Officer and reporting directly to Publicis CEO Arthur Sadoun. For external reporting, LiveRamp's financials will sit within Publicis's Technology segment as a parallel but distinct entity from Epsilon (acquired for $4.4 billion in 2019 and classified as a managed service for direct consumer engagement) and Lotame (acquired in early 2025).

The industry has rejected the premise that an identity layer owned by the world's largest agency holding company can remain an impartial utility, and the asset's scale explains why. RampID is the definitive infrastructure through which brands, publishers, retailers, and agencies share and match data securely: it connects over 25,000 publisher domains and more than 500 technology partners across 14 global markets. Combine Epsilon's identity spine (250 million consumer profiles) with Lotame's 1.6 billion global identifiers (per Publicis's own Lotame acquisition release; the 2.3 billion figure elsewhere in circulation is Publicis Groupe's own pre-Lotame profile count, not Lotame's contribution) and LiveRamp's clean room distribution networks, and Publicis is constructing what the source calls an impenetrable, closed-loop data fortress.

That fortress is the centerpiece of Publicis's "agentic business transformation": a fully integrated stack in which media planning ceases to be a discrete, billable service and becomes an automated capability executed by AI agents at near-zero marginal cost, with profit generated entirely inside the proprietary data and distribution infrastructure. For rival holding companies and independent agencies, routing data through LiveRamp now means explicitly subsidizing a direct competitor's AI advantage and exposing their clients' most sensitive predictive consumer intelligence to rival infrastructure.

## Omnicom's counter: Real ID and the accelerated exit

The most immediately quantifiable reaction came from Omnicom. Its prior roadmap already contained a sunset provision: exit the LiveRamp relationship naturally when the contract ran its course in the first quarter of 2028. The Publicis deal rewrote that timeline. Speaking at the J.P. Morgan Global Technology, Media and Communications Conference shortly after the announcement, CEO John Wren said: "I moved that drop dead date yesterday a year from now when we'll be completely separated, even if we have to invest a little money to honor our contract for the balance of the year". That puts the full separation around May 2027, roughly a year ahead of the contractual sunset. Wren dismissed Sadoun's neutrality assurances outright, arguing there is no conceivable way to extract value from keeping LiveRamp independent of the rest of Publicis's infrastructure, and noting that a functional identity resolution provider "had to be Switzerland"; a status the acquisition permanently revoked.

The mechanics of the exit are unusually frictionless because of corporate history. Acxiom and LiveRamp were the same entity until 2018, when Acxiom split itself: LiveRamp spun out as an independent public company while the Marketing Solutions division was sold to Interpublic Group for $2.3 billion. When Omnicom acquired IPG in late 2025, it inherited the foundational Acxiom data assets. The remaining financial dependency nets to zero: Acxiom pays LiveRamp approximately $50 million annually for data services, and LiveRamp pays Acxiom exactly $50 million in return for reciprocal services. Neither entity relies on the other for net positive operating margin, so Omnicom can execute a clean structural break without a revenue hit to Acxiom.

The replacement is Acxiom's Real ID, engineered over the preceding years precisely so the holding company would not remain subservient to a third-party identity layer, and heavily integrated into the broader Omni platform. Per Omnicom Chief Technology Officer Paolo Yuvienco, Real ID is cloud-native and inherently interoperable: an agnostic orchestration layer that can pull in whichever identity graph is most effective for a given client use case, whether Real ID itself, The Trade Desk's UID2, or publisher-specific identifiers. Migrating clients to Real ID insulates a brand's first-party data from Publicis's machine-learning models, converting the LiveRamp acquisition from a Publicis asset into an Omnicom client-retention and acquisition tool.

## WPP's hard pivot: InfoSum and the independence mandate

WPP had begun insulating its data supply chain well before the deal materialized; the $2.167 billion acquisition supplied the shock that turned preparation into a mandate across the global agency portfolio. At the Campaign House during the Cannes Lions International Festival of Creativity in June 2026, CEO Cindy Rose confirmed that WPP agencies had been instructed to cease using LiveRamp "as of recently". Asked directly whether the Publicis acquisition prompted the cessation, she replied: "What do you think?".

Rose framed the contrast philosophically, positioning WPP around "total independence" and "total platform elasticism": client media budgets optimized for client growth, not locked into a holding company's proprietary data infrastructure. "Ultimately, I want clients to stay with us because we're delivering superior growth, not because we've created friction or switching costs or lock-in," she stated. Client data, in her framing, is the source of a brand's "asymmetric competitive advantage," and running it through a centralized graph owned by a competitor exposes that advantage to algorithmic absorption.

The migration vehicle is InfoSum, the privacy-centric clean room provider WPP proactively acquired in April 2025 for an estimated $150 million. InfoSum's architecture uses "non-movement of data" technology: brands deterministically match datasets across decentralized nodes without ever pooling raw PII in a central repository. Embedded deeply in the WPP Open AI platform, it gives WPP clients an identity resolution framework architecturally immune to the centralization risk posed by the Publicis LiveRamp-Epsilon stack.

Rose also connected the balkanization to agency economics: she declared the traditional "time and materials" billing model "dead" in the era of AI and agentic marketing. With audience building and media planning increasingly automated by AI platforms and clean rooms, agencies can no longer charge premium margins for that manual labor, and WPP is experimenting with tying remuneration directly to client success and media outcomes. The battleground shifts from executional services to the proprietary intelligence and measurement fidelity embedded in the data layer itself.

## The Trade Desk: from embargo to truce to ascendancy

The vacuum LiveRamp leaves requires a scaled, neutral alternative for the open web, and that need has dramatically accelerated The Trade Desk's Unified ID 2.0 (UID2) heading into the 2027 programmatic upfronts. But UID2's ascendancy runs through a margin war that first had to be settled.

In March 2026, Publicis formally instructed its clients to pause all spending on The Trade Desk, alleging that a third-party audit commissioned via FirmDecisions had uncovered "fee-stacking" and improper billing: ad-tech fees stacked on top of other charges in violation of contractual agreements, auto-enrollment of clients in unauthorized tools, and resistance to transparency on at-cost media pricing. The market reaction was severe, driving The Trade Desk's stock down roughly 13% to 16% in the immediate aftermath. The dispute was fundamentally a proxy war over margins and structural control: as DSPs push direct-to-advertiser relationships and disintermediate the supply chain through products like OpenPath (which bypasses SSPs to plug directly into publisher inventory), legacy agencies see their indirect revenue streams and strategic relevance threatened.

The dispute resolved quickly and quietly by June 12, 2026. In a terse joint statement, the companies announced they had "addressed previous differences as identified in the audit" and that Publicis would immediately resume recommending The Trade Desk to clients; neither party disclosed the financial adjustments or contractual concessions involved. The speed of the reconciliation (under 90 days from embargo to truce) reflects inescapable codependency: with billions in media spend on the table, neither side could afford a protracted standoff. The timing also tracked the LiveRamp fallout precisely. Publicis, having just alienated WPP and Omnicom by buying LiveRamp, could not simultaneously war with the industry's largest independent DSP; The Trade Desk, facing share pressure from the Google and Meta walled gardens, needed Publicis's aggregated ad budgets to sustain its growth trajectory.

With LiveRamp's neutrality gone, UID2 is the primary beneficiary of the industry's need for an open identity framework. Developed initially by The Trade Desk and subsequently handed to independent governance via Prebid, UID2 is a cryptographic, email-based identifier designed to replace third-party cookies without a centralized corporate tollbooth. Because it is interoperable and not owned by a competing holding company, WPP and Omnicom can integrate it into their own orchestration layers (WPP Open, Real ID) without fear of data leakage or competitive enrichment. Per the source, UID2 has achieved critical mass with adoption across hundreds of premium publishers, cementing its position as the de facto connective tissue of the open internet into the 2027 upfronts.

## The agentic execution layer: Koa Agents, Open Agentic Kit, Claude

The Trade Desk is not content to be a passive identifier vendor; it is moving up the value chain by automating the planning and execution work agencies historically billed for. In early 2026 it launched the Open Agentic Kit, an interoperability framework that lets AI agents from various foundation models interact with its programmatic infrastructure, and introduced Koa Agents, an agentic layer built on the existing Koa AI platform and embedded in the Kokai campaign management dashboard, shifting campaign optimization from manual levers toward AI-driven recommendations.

At the Marketecture Live conference in March 2026, CEO Jeff Green confirmed a closed beta in which advertisers create full programmatic campaigns using Anthropic's Claude large language model: a Claude-built workflow ingests a natural language media plan, reformats it into a template compatible with The Trade Desk's OpenTTD API portal, and automatically configures the campaign inside the platform. The operational stakes are considerable. The Trade Desk processes over 20 million ad impression opportunities per second, evaluating thousands of variables (site lists, bid factors, frequency caps, supply path quality) within milliseconds, and encoding a human trader's nuanced expertise into a natural language prompt introduces real transparency challenges about how the AI implicitly configures the campaign.

Kokai manages that tension through two distinct execution states. In Control Mode, the Koa Agent acts as an advisory co-pilot: it analyzes audience strategy, inventory quality, and budget constraints, and surfaces recommendations the human trader must evaluate and explicitly activate. In Performance Mode, the AI assumes total execution authority, dynamically optimizing bids and capital allocation within guardrails set at campaign inception. Koa Agents currently focus on campaign setup, audience segmentation, and troubleshooting rather than autonomous real-time bidding, but the stated long-term vision is complete end-to-end automation of the buying process. The structural friction with agencies is direct: as The Trade Desk simplifies its interface to enable direct-to-brand relationships (a 55% jump in joint business deals with direct advertisers in March 2026), it systematically undermines the specialized trading expertise agencies rely on to justify their historical margins.

## Walled gardens harvest the displacement

The balkanization of open-web identity disproportionately benefits the structural walled gardens. As legacy identity graphs fragment and agencies embargo competitor pipes, the Layer 0 gatekeepers are standing up internal clean room environments to capture displaced media spend ahead of the 2027 upfronts.

Netflix is the fastest-moving case. Having abandoned ad-free purity entirely, it scaled its ad-supported tier to 250 million monthly active viewers globally by May 2026, with public expectations of doubling advertising revenue to $3 billion. To monetize that inventory without third-party identity brokers, Netflix built clean room integrations with Snowflake and Amazon Web Services, and confirmed the addition of WPP-owned InfoSum to its ecosystem by the end of 2026, a direct appeal to WPP's post-LiveRamp infrastructure that lets WPP clients match audience data against Netflix's viewership logs without exposure to the open web. Netflix is also testing its own AI agents to manage and purchase ads, using generative AI to dynamically adapt advertiser creative to vertical video and pause-ad formats.

Amazon runs its familiar dual posture: withholding granular Prime Video viewership from the studios that supply the content while monetizing that same audience through Amazon Marketing Cloud (AMC). With over $70 billion in trailing-twelve-month advertising revenue as of Q1 2026, Amazon uses AMC as a closed-loop attribution engine; because AMC forces all data matching and measurement to occur inside Amazon's proprietary server environment, it neatly circumvents the identity wars on the open web. The full quantification of this posture lives in the gatekeeper layer document.

The gatekeeper tier consolidated further in June 2026 when Fox Corporation (FOXA/FOXB) announced its acquisition of Roku in a cash-and-stock deal at a $22 billion enterprise value, fusing Fox's premium live sports and news rights with Roku's 100-million-household hardware reach and the Roku Data Cloud. Fox thereby moves from pure-play content provider to hybrid gatekeeper dominating both distribution chokepoints and hardware-level data monetization, insulated from the LiveRamp fracture by owning the glass on which the media is consumed. Full deal mechanics ($160.00 per share, announced June 15, 2026) are carried in the gatekeeper layer document.

## Third-order effect: the Shield layer's disintermediation risk

An emerging third-order effect of gatekeeper consolidation threatens the independent verification vendors of the Shield layer, DoubleVerify and Integral Ad Science. Advertisers historically relied on these third parties to verify viewability, brand safety, and the absence of bot fraud, which surged 140% in CTV environments in early 2026. But as Amazon and Netflix absorb larger shares of ad budgets inside their clean rooms, they are building and enforcing their own media-quality and fraud-detection tooling. Amazon vets open internet supply upstream, before it enters the bidding stage, removing invalid traffic (IVT) and made-for-advertising (MFA) sites via internal telemetry. Netflix applies multi-layered up-front filtration to block non-human impressions and requires users to be authenticated and logged in to view content, inherently reducing scraper-bot risk without a third-party Shield vendor. If the walled gardens convince advertisers to accept self-graded verification metrics backed by closed-loop performance data, the independent Shield layer faces severe disintermediation: third-party verification becomes a legacy patch rather than a permanent fixture of the 2027 digital advertising ecosystem.

## Synthesis

The $2.167 billion Publicis acquisition of LiveRamp is not a routine consolidation event; it is the structural fracture of the foundational identity layer. Absorbing the industry's primary neutral data conduit forced a rapid, defensive balkanization of the programmatic supply chain, and the countermeasures are concrete and irreversible: Omnicom accelerated its exit to roughly May 2027 behind Acxiom's Real ID, and WPP mandated total independence, routing clean room workflows entirely through InfoSum. That coordinated exodus mathematically guarantees severe client attrition for LiveRamp, effectively erasing the independent data collaboration market and turning identity resolution into a zero-sum weapon between rival agencies. In the vacuum, UID2 has achieved primacy as the open-source alternative for the open web, while The Trade Desk deploys agentic AI (Claude plus the Open Agentic Kit) against the manual execution margins agencies survive on. Heading into the 2027 upfronts, the destruction of open-web identity neutrality is driving spend into the walled-garden clean rooms of Amazon, Roku, and a rapidly scaling Netflix. The era of shared, neutral intelligence is over; owning the data pipeline is the remaining moat.
