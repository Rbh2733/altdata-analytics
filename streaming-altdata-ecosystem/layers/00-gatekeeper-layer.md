# Layer 0: Ecosystem Gatekeepers

Eight platforms sit above the streaming alt-data ecosystem as gatekeepers: Amazon, Apple, Roku, Netflix, Disney, Google, Fox, and Paramount Skydance/Warner Bros. Discovery. Each withholds some piece of viewership, subscriber, or engagement telemetry that the seven vendor layers below exist to reconstruct, and several now run their own clean rooms monetizing the very data they withhold from studios and licensors. The research underneath this layer draws on three independent Gemini Pro-tier Deep Research passes run within about 45 minutes of each other on 2026-07-07 (17:06, 17:48, and 17:50 UTC), referred to below only where their figures diverge; where they agree, the finding is simply stated as fact.

## Amazon's dual posture, quantified

Amazon operates the most heavily monetized dual posture in the ecosystem: it withholds granular viewership telemetry from the studios and licensors supplying Prime Video, while selling deterministic audience targeting built on that same viewing behavior to advertisers.

On the withholding side, Amazon's FY2025 consolidated net sales reached $716.9 billion, up 12% from $638.0 billion in 2024, with operating income of $80.0 billion (up from $68.6 billion), per Amazon's Q4 2025 earnings release and 2025 annual report filed with the SEC. Prime Video's standalone economics are never broken out. They sit inside the "Subscription Services" line ($49.62 billion in FY2025, up 11.8% from $44.37 billion), which bundles Prime memberships, digital video, audiobooks, digital music, and e-books. Because a Prime membership is accounted for as a single stand-ready obligation, studios licensing content to Prime Video cannot isolate the subscriber-acquisition or retention value of their own intellectual property from public disclosures. AWS, for scale context, did $128.7 billion in FY2025 (up 19.6%) with $45.6 billion of operating income, underscoring that streaming is a small piece of a much larger machine.

On the monetization side, Advertising Services is Amazon's fastest-growing major segment: $56.0 billion in FY2024, crossing a $70 billion trailing-twelve-month run rate by Q1 2026 (Q1 2026 advertising services revenue of $17.24 billion, up 24% year over year, per Amazon's Q1 2026 earnings release, aboutamazon.com, April 29, 2026, where Andy Jassy stated on the record that "advertising grew to over $70 billion in TTM revenue"). Analyst estimates put advertising gross margin above 90% with operating margins near 50%, because the ad stack layers directly onto existing retail, traffic, and customer data.

Amazon Marketing Cloud (AMC) is the operational instrument of that posture: a privacy-safe clean room built natively on AWS, in which advertisers run aggregate SQL queries across Amazon's deterministic first-party signals (Prime Video ad exposures, search clicks, retail purchases) joined against their own first-party data (CRM logs, loyalty metrics), with outputs delivered only as aggregated metrics and no row-level PII exposed. AMC lets advertisers compute exact incremental return on ad spend and run multi-touch attribution linking a top-funnel Prime Video impression to a bottom-funnel marketplace purchase. The clean room itself is free for eligible advertisers, but access requires executing media spend through the Amazon DSP, so withheld viewership data functions as a toll booth into Amazon's ad stack. Recent expansions deepen the asymmetry, and it is worth being precise about what actually happened, since two figures that read like a conflict are in fact two separate events: Amazon extended the ad-traffic lookback window from 13 to 25 months following unBoxed 2025, and separately unlocked up to 5 years of historical data for retail purchase and video viewership signals (per Amazon's own advertising.amazon.com announcement). As of June 2, 2026, the Amazon Retail Purchases and Flexible Shopping Insights datasets are available to all AMC users at no additional cost, letting brands track customer lifetime value and organic shopping behavior independent of ad exposure. A commissioned Forrester Consulting Total Economic Impact study published February 2026 credits AMC plus the Amazon DSP with 240% ROI, up to 65% reduction in wasted ad spend, and 30% to 50% higher return on ad spend.

The asymmetry is formally contested. The Writers Guild of America has filed unfair-labor-practice charges with the National Labor Relations Board against Amazon, explicitly citing the withholding of Prime Video subscriber numbers as a tactic to underpay writers whose residual compensation is tied to subscriber tiers (the current labor agreements set a viewership-based residual threshold of 20% of a platform's domestic subscriber base, a figure creators cannot verify without third-party data). This NLRB charge is a stronger, more current citation than the earlier WGA and Strategic Organizing Center letters to the FTC about the MGM acquisition, which made the same anticompetitive-withholding argument. Amazon's Prime Video legal agreements meanwhile explicitly codify its right to withhold data and offset payments, structurally institutionalizing supplier blindness. This dynamic connects directly to the Wallet, Glass, and Identity/Clean Rooms layers elsewhere in this framework.

## Apple's posture, resolved

Where Amazon runs a dual posture, Apple runs an isolationist one, and the question of whether Apple operates an AMC-equivalent clean room or pure ecosystem bait resolves cleanly to the latter.

Apple's opacity exceeds Amazon's. FY2025 (ended September 27, 2025) total net sales were $416.16 billion, up 6%, per Apple's 10-K. The entire software, subscription, and streaming ecosystem is condensed into one "Services" segment: $109.15 billion in FY2025, up 13% from $96.16 billion, at a 75.4% gross margin (versus 36.8% gross margin for Products). There is zero breakout of Apple TV+ subscribers, churn, or ARPU, which is precisely why the Wallet layer (Antenna, Consumer Edge panels) exists as the only source of synthetic sign-up and cancellation proxies for the service.

Apple operates no programmatic data-monetization clean room comparable to Amazon Marketing Cloud or Roku Data Cloud. Its advertising revenue comes primarily from App Store search ads, not from selling streaming audience segments. In November 2025, Apple's head of Services, Eddy Cue, confirmed on the record that the company has "no plans at this time" to introduce an ad-supported tier for Apple TV+. Apple TV+ is a high-budget, non-monetized loss leader whose job is hardware retention (iPhone net sales were $209.58 billion in FY2025) and Apple One bundle survival. Because Apple has no second-order data business to protect, its incentives run entirely toward hardware-level privacy enforcement, and it wields the App Tracking Transparency framework as a weapon that deprecates third-party identifiers across the broader ad-tech ecosystem while building no replacement infrastructure for external buyers. Apple is structurally different from the other gatekeepers in this posture, not a smaller version of the same thing.

## Roku's centrality, and its absorption into Fox

Before the June 2026 Fox acquisition, Roku was the purest gatekeeper of the group: unlike Amazon and Apple, whose streaming postures are side effects of much larger businesses, data withholding and monetization was Roku's central, existential business model.

Roku's FY2025 total net revenue was $4.74 billion, up 15%, with a return to net income of $88.4 million, per Roku's Q4/FY2025 shareholder letter. In early 2026 Roku recast its reporting from a single "Platform" line into three segments: Advertising ($2.33 billion, up 13%, driven by 68% higher video ad impressions despite pricing pressure), Subscriptions ($1.82 billion, up 25%, supported by owned-and-operated services Frndly TV and Howdy), and Devices ($592 million, flat, managed near break-even). Advertising plus Subscriptions generated $4.14 billion, over 87% of total revenue; hardware is effectively a loss leader run to acquire viewership telemetry, generating 145.6 billion streaming hours across roughly 90 million global streaming households in 2025. The Q1 2026 quarterly cut tells the same story: total revenue $1.25 billion with platform revenue up 28% to $1.13 billion (Advertising $613 million, up 27%; Subscriptions $519 million, up 30%), net income of $86 million, and record trailing-twelve-month free cash flow of $538.8 million.

To monetize the telemetry, Roku retired its legacy OneView ad-stack strategy, which functioned more as an exclusive ad-tech mandate than an open ecosystem, in favor of the Roku Data Cloud, a clean-room environment letting partners analyze Roku's proprietary TV data for programmatic measurement, plus the self-serve Roku Ads Manager aimed at small and medium businesses. The Roku Channel gives Roku a large owned-and-operated FAST inventory position that it monetizes without paying upfront content licensing costs. What looked like an unreconciled fault line here (one pass putting The Roku Channel alone at 6.3% of US TV streaming time, another putting Tubi plus The Roku Channel combined, pre-merger, at 5.2%) was a mislabeled figure rather than a genuine two-source disagreement: Nielsen's own March 2026 Gauge data (corroborated via advertising.roku.com's own Gauge writeup) puts The Roku Channel alone at 3.0% and Tubi at 2.2%, with 5.2%-6.3% being the combined, projected post-merger range, not a standalone figure. No disclosure isolates Roku Data Cloud or OneView revenue specifically; it stays bundled inside Platform/Advertising.

Roku's absolute reliance on ad monetization also made it uniquely exposed to consolidation pressure and competition for ad dollars from Amazon and Netflix, which is the vulnerability the Fox deal resolved. On June 15, 2026, Fox Corporation announced a definitive agreement to acquire Roku outright: $160.00 per share in cash and stock, roughly $22 billion enterprise value, with Roku holders receiving $96.00 cash plus 0.9693 shares of FOXA per Roku share, backed by $12.0 billion in fully committed bridge financing from Morgan Stanley as senior unsecured term loans subject to a 4.5-to-1.0 operating income leverage covenant. Existing Fox shareholders will own approximately 73% of the combined company at close, expected in H1 2027, with approximately $400 million in annual run-rate cost synergies and pro forma net leverage of approximately 2.8x at closing.

Strategically, the deal transforms Fox from a legacy, ad-dependent appointment-viewing broadcaster (NFL, MLB, NASCAR, Big Ten, Fox News, with no hardware telemetry) into a hybrid gatekeeper spanning both rights control and data infrastructure. Tubi plus The Roku Channel combine into a FAST ecosystem trailing only YouTube and Netflix, riding Roku's CTV operating system, first-party data, and direct billing relationships with over 100 million global streaming households. Roku Data Cloud and Roku Ads Manager give Fox deterministic monetization of live sports viewership, insulating the combined entity from App Tracking Transparency and cookie deprecation. Lachlan Murdoch framed the acquisition as a deliberate pivot into high-growth digital verticals. Roku's role within this framework changes accordingly: no longer a standalone pure-play, it is now the data engine inside a rights-chokepoint controller.

## The Netflix exception, closing

Netflix has historically been the acknowledged exception in this layer: superior first-party telemetry and algorithmic distribution let it operate outside the third-party ad-tech ecosystem, funding itself on subscriptions and licensing arbitrage, the NBCUniversal *Suits* acquisition being the canonical case (its headline value is discussed under the Demand/Valuation layer, where the two figures in circulation, $356 million in measured US/Canada subscriber revenue and over $800 million in global licensing value, describe different scopes rather than competing claims).

That exception is closing. Netflix's ad tier is scaling fast, though the scale figures in circulation diverge by an order of magnitude and the gap does not fully resolve: one pass puts the tier at over 40 million global users by 2025, while another puts monthly active users over 250 million by the May 2026 upfronts (up from 190 million in late 2025), defining an active user as anyone in a household watching more than one minute of ads per month, an individuals-based reach metric rather than a subscriber count. The definitional difference plausibly explains part of the gap, but both figures were presented as the tier's scale in the same window without reconciling each other, so both are carried here rather than averaged. What every pass agrees on is the target: roughly $3 billion in advertising revenue in 2026, with the ad business expected to double. Sixty percent of new customers now choose the ad-supported plan, and the tier is slated to roll out to 15 additional international markets, including Austria, Belgium, Denmark, and the Philippines, through 2027. To capture this, Netflix abandoned its initial exclusive reliance on Microsoft's ad stack and built its own in-house ad server, the Netflix Ads Suite, with global rollout across 2025 and 2026, providing AI-driven audience segmentation and interactive ad formats.

The structural tell is the clean-room buildout: Netflix has announced data clean room partnerships with Snowflake, InfoSum, and LiveRamp, letting marketers compute audience overlap, post-campaign reach, and last-touch attribution without Netflix ever exposing raw subscriber viewing histories, alongside private one-to-one marketplace deals. And the walled gardens are starting to merge: in Q1 2026, Amazon Audiences became available to advertisers buying Netflix inventory, letting brands target Netflix viewers using Amazon's proprietary shopping, streaming, and browsing signals, a direct bridge between two previously isolated gatekeepers. Combined with aggressive live-sports acquisition (NFL Christmas Day games, the $5 billion, ten-year WWE Raw deal), Netflix has structurally converged with Amazon and Roku, extracting programmatic ad spend on its proprietary viewership data while keeping row-level data locked behind a clean-room wall. The old framing of Netflix as operating outside the ecosystem is now historical.

## What each gatekeeper forces the vendor layers to build

The demand for every vendor layer in this framework traces back to a specific thing a gatekeeper withholds or controls, and the flow is predominantly one-way in the underlying research; what flows back from vendors to gatekeepers (payments, data licenses, measurement contracts) is comparatively undocumented and remains an open question.

Wallet-layer demand (subscriber transaction intelligence) comes from Apple and Amazon obscuring standalone streaming health inside their $100 billion-plus Services and Subscription Services lines, Disney obscuring the churn-reduction and ARPU mechanics of its bundles, and Fox's new standalone platform architecture requiring independent validation. Antenna and YipitData respond by modeling gross adds, cohort survival, plan-mix migration, and true customer acquisition cost from consumer panels, card transaction feeds, and digital invoice scraping; Netflix's ad-tier shift forces a recalibration to separate premium subscription revenue from ad-subsidized ARPU, and Google's Play-store billing is itself scraped to estimate app-store subscription health.

Glass-layer demand (hardware and ACR measurement) comes from Amazon, Apple, and Netflix enforcing strict viewership embargoes against the studios licensing them content, which pushes ACR chipsets embedded in smart TVs, Samba TV and Vizio/Inscape among them, to measure viewing at the screen-pixel level, circumventing software-level blockades. Post-acquisition, Fox-Roku controls Glass-layer data for 100 million households, and the Roku OS itself gates third-party ACR vendors trying to measure Roku-branded TVs.

Shield-layer demand (media quality and fraud verification) comes from programmatic CTV growth on Roku, Amazon, and Netflix driving CTV bot fraud up 140% year over year in early 2026, concentrated in unprotected direct-deal campaigns. The layer's independent verifiers (DoubleVerify, Integral Ad Science) face a genuine disintermediation threat here: Amazon and Netflix are building internal fraud-detection and media-quality tooling, and if gatekeepers succeed in grading their own homework, advertisers risk being locked into gatekeeper-reported metrics.

Demand/Valuation-layer demand comes from Paramount Skydance/WBD's roughly $79 billion debt burden forcing continuous rights divestitures and library licensing that require external IP valuation, from gatekeepers weaponizing release windowing (NBCUniversal withheld the final season of *Suits* from Netflix to create a halo effect driving users to Peacock), and from Disney and Fox sports bundling obscuring standalone demand for specific IP. Parrot Analytics and PlumResearch (acquired by Ampere Analysis in 2026) respond by synthesizing social, search, and piracy signals into cost-per-demand-point metrics that talent agencies use to set independent baselines against data-hoarding platforms.

Funnel-layer demand comes from the ad-supported shift at Netflix and Amazon requiring constant user acquisition, with platforms bidding up mobile ad inventory around tentpole launches, and Apple gating App Store download-velocity data. JustWatch, Reelgood, and Sensor Tower respond by proxying customer acquisition cost efficiency from search navigation, watchlist additions, and app-store velocity, though conversational AI and LLM assistants, dominated by Google, now threaten to become the new discovery gatekeeper, bypassing aggregators entirely.

Sports-Rights-layer demand comes from Fox-Roku consolidating tier-one NFL and college football rights with hardware distribution, Google holding NFL Sunday Ticket inside YouTube TV, Amazon's NBA investment rebuilding TNT's legacy broadcast operation, and Disney countering with ESPN Unlimited. That fragmentation is the layer's demand engine: Genius Sports, Stats Perform, and Sportradar operate as a mandatory toll layer because live sports require real-time latency, blackout compliance, and sportsbook integration that every rights-winning gatekeeper must buy rather than build, effectively taxing gatekeeper expansion.

Identity and Clean Rooms-layer demand comes from third-party cookie deprecation, accelerated by Apple's App Tracking Transparency, plus AMC and Roku Data Cloud forcing brands into walled-garden measurement environments, and Netflix building its own clean-room walls with Snowflake and InfoSum. LiveRamp and The Trade Desk act as neutral connective tissue matching Wallet transaction data to Glass viewership data without raw PII exchange, though that neutrality is now structurally threatened: Publicis Groupe's acquisition of LiveRamp (May 2026, $2.167 billion enterprise value, $38.50 per share, $2.546 billion equity value, per Publicis Groupe's press release of 2026-05-17) removes LiveRamp's independence, and if competing holding companies such as WPP and Omnicom abandon RampID, the proprietary gatekeeper clean rooms are positioned to monopolize the identity layer.

## Paramount Skydance/WBD: reclassified as vendor subject, not gatekeeper

The stress test this section of the research was built to run, whether Paramount Skydance/WBD still belongs in the gatekeeper set, resolves clearly against gatekeeper status. Paramount Skydance entered a definitive agreement to acquire WBD on February 27, 2026, for $110.9 billion ($31 per share, all cash), outbidding Netflix. WBD stockholders approved on April 23, 2026; the DOJ Antitrust Division cleared the deal June 12, 2026; closing targets Q3 2026. The financing is heavy: $39.5 billion in first-lien secured debt, bridge commitments recently reduced from $54 billion to $49 billion, and tender/exchange offers for up to $2.4 billion of legacy WBD notes with repeatedly extended deadlines. The combined entity carries roughly $79 billion in debt under consolidated net leverage covenants capped at 5.25x and stepping down to 4.50x, and is chasing $6 billion in run-rate cost synergies, 30% expected in year one.

The NBA outcome captures the entity's actual position. In a sweeping 11-year settlement resolving WBD's breach-of-contract lawsuit, WBD conceded US domestic NBA broadcast rights to Disney/ESPN, Amazon, and NBCUniversal. WBD retained international NBA rights in the Nordics, Poland, and parts of Latin America, plus a global license to produce and distribute NBA content through Bleacher Report and House of Highlights; TNT Sports continues producing *Inside the NBA*, but the show is now licensed and distributed by ESPN and ABC, and in exchange ESPN sublicenses a package of Big 12 college football and basketball games to WBD.

Paramount Skydance/WBD lacks Apple's hardware monopoly, Amazon's clean-room dominance, and Google's distribution chokepoint. Its opacity is not intentional data hoarding, it is the byproduct of forced divestitures, studio restructuring, and debt management. It therefore functions as a primary subject of the vendor layers, feeding the Demand/Valuation and Sports-Rights layers as analysts value its surviving IP, rather than as a Layer 0 engine in its own right. It stands as the cautionary case of a legacy media company that never built an owned data clean room and lost its distribution chokepoints. No additional platform emerged from the research as a required ninth gatekeeper, and none of the original eight was made redundant, though this reclassification means the active gatekeeper set is effectively seven, with Fox and Roku merging into a single combined entity by H1 2027.

## How central this ecosystem actually is to each gatekeeper

The research converges on a three-tier ranking of how much each gatekeeper's fortunes actually depend on this economy, and it is not proportionate across the eight.

Roku and Fox (combining), Disney, and increasingly Netflix are structurally central engines. Roku's entire $4.74 billion revenue model is the exchange of subsidized hardware for platform advertising telemetry; Fox's existence rests on live sports rights and linear yield; together they form an entity wholly dependent on the streaming data economy. Disney's future depends on resolving the opacity of its DTC bundles and sports rights as legacy cable declines, with Fox One priced at $39.99 per month for the ESPN Unlimited bundle, ESPN Unlimited standalone at $29.99 per month, and the Disney+/Hulu/Max bundle serving as a churn suppressant, none of which discloses cross-sell discounts or twelve-month cohort survival rates, which is exactly what keeps the Wallet layer in business. Netflix, the historical exception, is now centrally reliant on ad-tier scaling and proprietary ad tech.

Amazon, Apple, and Google are, by contrast, side effects of unrelated behemoths. Amazon's streaming economics are subsumed into a $716.9 billion retail and cloud operation, and AMC, however powerful, is ultimately a tool to drive high-margin DSP spend rather than a core media endeavor. Apple's streaming posture is pure ecosystem bait supporting a $416 billion hardware and services empire. Google's YouTube TV leverage is an extension of its search and video dominance: YouTube captures more total US television time than any platform including Netflix, and the November 2025 carriage dispute demonstrated the leverage in practice, when Disney pulled ESPN, ABC, and FX from YouTube TV for nearly two weeks, Google held firm, and the blackout cost ESPN an estimated $110 million and forced Disney back to the table, against YouTube TV's roughly 8 million subscribers and exclusive NFL Sunday Ticket integration.

Paramount Skydance/WBD sits alone as a distressed subject: crushed under roughly $79 billion of debt, structurally irrelevant as a gatekeeper, functioning as a distressed supplier of IP to the actual market makers. The practical implication for reading the rest of this framework is that Roku/Fox and Disney decisions propagate through the vendor layers because those companies live or die by this economy, Amazon, Apple, and Google reshape the ecosystem only as a side effect of decisions made for much larger businesses, and Paramount Skydance/WBD generates vendor-layer demand passively, by being restructured in public.
