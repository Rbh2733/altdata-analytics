# Layer 1: Wallet, Subscriber Transaction Intelligence

The Wallet layer is the ground-truth stratum of the streaming alt-data ecosystem. Where the Glass layer measures attention and the Demand layer measures intent, the Wallet layer isolates the billing relationship itself, built from aggregated and anonymized credit and debit card streams, direct bank API feeds, and digital invoice tracking. Four derived metrics carry the layer's institutional value: cohort survival curves that track a signup-month cohort's attrition over multiple years to model customer lifetime value; plan-mix migration, or visibility into premium-to-ad-supported tier downgrades as platforms universally roll out ad tiers, and whether those transitions are revenue-accretive or dilutive; cross-platform wallet mapping, which shows where an entertainment budget gets reallocated when a consumer cancels one service, distinguishing isolated churn from broader discretionary contraction; and serial-churner tracking, the highly transient consumers who repeatedly cancel and resubscribe over twelve-month windows, a behavioral segment sized at an estimated $9 billion market opportunity. These metrics feed cross-layer triangulation workflows, where a title-level demand spike from attention-layer providers gets time-lagged against Wallet-layer retention or resubscription curves over defined lag windows, controlling for confounders such as concurrent price increases or a competitor's simultaneous tentpole release. Mid-2026 projections size the total alternative data market between $17.78 billion and $30 billion by year end, the variance reflecting definitional strictness (the narrower investment-management-spend model projected $15.4 billion for 2025). The Wallet layer's distinguishing legal exposure within that market is financial-transaction consent, not device consent, which places it directly in the path of CFPB Section 1033 and California's Delete Act.

The findings below draw on four Gemini Deep Research passes across four model tiers, Pro (2026-07-06, the base document), Finance, Flash, and FlashLite (all three 2026-07-07 midday, roughly ninety seconds to two minutes apart), consolidated with pass-level attribution kept only where a claim is single-sourced or genuinely disputed.

## Ownership and capital structure across the five incumbents

YipitData is the dominant multi-sector incumbent. Founded 2010 as a daily-deal aggregator on a roughly $1.3 million seed round, it pivoted to institutional research in 2013. Its capital history is corroborated across all four passes: a $6 million Series A led by RRE Ventures (2011); $175 million in growth equity from Norwest Venture Partners (2019), valuing the company in the mid-hundreds of millions; a $475 million Series E led by The Carlyle Group (December 2021, out of a $587.71 million total round), pushing valuation past $1 billion; the acquisition of 1010data (2023), financed with a mix of cash and equity that brought former 1010data backers onto the cap table while early angel stakes were bought out in secondaries (broker projections had the integration lifting annual recurring revenue growth 8 to 12 percent annually); and a conventional debt facility from CIBC Innovation Banking (June 2024) taken in place of further dilutive equity. By late 2025, institutional stakes represented an estimated 60 to 75 percent of total voting power, with founders Vinicius Vacanti and James Moran retaining a roughly 20 to 30 percent aggregate stake and day-to-day control while preferred holders carry liquidation preferences, merger and dilution vetoes, and drag-along rights. Board seats include Jon Korngold of Carlyle Growth Equity and Parker Barrile of Norwest, with one pass, citing SEC proxy-adjacent filings, also naming Carlyle executives Scott Hughes and Patrick McCarter on compensation and nominating committees. Carlyle's balance sheet, $477 billion in assets under management exiting 2025 and $300 million of fee-related earnings in Q1 2026 against a $132.2 million GAAP loss, insulates the position from short-term volatility.

The 2024-2026 posture shifted from hypergrowth to margin optimization ahead of a liquidity event: targeted workforce reductions in late 2025, an ROI-before-spend mandate, and hires of public-market-experienced CFOs. The rumored 2026-2027 IPO remains genuinely in the preparation phase, with no S-1 filed. On sourcing quality this reads as settled rather than contested: the Pro and Flash passes both report no S-1 filed, tracing to aggregator and marketplace company profiles (PitchBook, Built In, EquityZen) that are at least the right kind of source for a company-status claim, while the Finance pass's claim of a confidential S-1 submission with the IPO "stalled" pending market conditions traces to a single citation, a Hiive pre-IPO share-listing page, a private secondary marketplace with no visibility into confidential SEC filings and no plausible way of knowing what it asserts. The citation cannot support the claim attached to it, so the no-S1 reading governs here; an SEC EDGAR confidential-draft disclosure check would be warranted if IPO timing ever becomes decision-relevant. Two facts postdate everything else in the record: on April 13, 2026, YipitData appointed Henry Schuck, founder and CEO of ZoomInfo (NASDAQ: GTM), to its board of directors, and the company has partnered with ANSR to establish a Global Capability Center in India for engineering and data-processing scale. Shares trade on restricted secondary marketplaces (Hiive, EquityZen, Notice) subject to company approval and rights of first refusal.

Consumer Edge, the primary horizontal challenger, is backed by Treville Capital Group and took over $60 million in equity financing from CoVenture in January 2023 to fund its M&A strategy. That capital culminated in the April 2025 acquisition of Earnest Analytics, founded by Kevin Carson in 2012, bringing the Orion and Vela consumer spend panels, Phoenix Pharmacy Claims, and Leo Medical Claims databases under one parent, discussed further below.

Antenna is the streaming-specific vertical specialist. Founded 2019 and seeded by Raine Ventures, it raised a $10 million Series A in February 2022 led by Bertelsmann Digital Media Investments alongside Foundry Group, with UTA Ventures participating; total raised is approximately $13.8 million across two rounds, with a wider cap table including Grit Capital, Hyper, Imagination Capital, SK Ventures, and Waverley Capital. Antenna deliberately runs a leaner capitalization optimized for enterprise B2B data-as-a-service licensing to media corporate development teams, and pioneered the M+1 retention metrics used to track serial churners and bundle efficacy.

Bloomberg Second Measure is a wholly owned Bloomberg LP subsidiary, acquired in late 2020 (one pass specifies December 24, 2020, facilitated by Moelis & Company and SRS Acquiom; the exact date is single-sourced but plausible). It has no independent funding track: its feeds are integrated into the Bloomberg Terminal, via ALTD and ECAN, and distributed through Bloomberg Data License, drawing on a US panel of over 20 million consumers with five-plus years of spend history. A database artifact reporting a "new" $20 million Series A in March 2026 traces to a re-parsed record of the actual February 12, 2019 Series A, co-led by Bessemer Venture Partners and Goldman Sachs with Citi Ventures participating, and is a database error rather than a real funding event.

M Science is a quant-focused, wholly owned subsidiary of Jefferies Financial Group (NYSE: JEF), which reported Q2 fiscal 2026 revenue of roughly $2.21 billion, up 35 percent year over year, and net income up 157 percent to $226.2 million. M Science has modernized delivery for the agentic era with a Unified Data Model plus a Model Context Protocol server that lets autonomous AI systems programmatically query its analyst-curated datasets, converting a passive dataset into an API-callable knowledge graph.

## Margin compression and the JPMorgan/Plaid precedent

The Wallet layer's economics historically rested on near-zero marginal-cost data acquisition through aggregators such as Plaid, Yodlee, MX, and Finicity, much of it via screen-scraping. That paradigm has collapsed. In July 2025 JPMorgan Chase sent commercial pricing sheets to major aggregators, and in September 2025 JPMorgan and Plaid signed a renewed data-access agreement under which Plaid pays for consumer account data the bank had historically transmitted free.

JPMorgan's justification rests on volume: it disclosed 1.89 billion data requests from aggregators in June 2025 alone, of which only about 13 percent were tied to an immediate customer-initiated action, with the remaining 87 percent automated background pulls refreshing dashboards and transaction panels (a second framing puts the volume at roughly two billion monthly requests, up from one billion in 2023, a different measurement rather than a contradiction of the same figure). Contracted pricing is undisclosed but described by industry sources as fractions of a cent per data pull, with the steepest fees on payments-related transfers, the exact data class the Wallet layer runs on. Pre-deal Forbes estimates put Plaid's potential exposure at up to $300 million in new annual fees, an amount equal to roughly 75 percent of Plaid's 2024 revenue. Plaid has publicly committed to absorbing the costs rather than repricing its roughly 7,000 clients, a position the Financial Technology Association and American Fintech Council argue cannot hold indefinitely. JPMorgan's finalized agreements reportedly cover aggregators representing over 95 percent of third-party data requests, including Plaid, Yodlee, Morningstar, and Akoya.

The regulatory backstop that would have prohibited these tolls is gone for now. The CFPB finalized the Personal Financial Data Rights rule under Section 1033 in October 2024, with compliance phasing in from April 1, 2026. In August 2025 the CFPB issued an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking reopening the fee question, and on October 29, 2025, Judge Danny Reeves of the Eastern District of Kentucky issued a preliminary injunction blocking enforcement, ruling the CFPB likely exceeded its statutory authority in extending data rights to commercial third parties and calling the compliance deadlines "arbitrary and capricious." The Financial Technology Association filed a notice of appeal in December 2025, but the April 1, 2026 trigger passed without effect. The vacuum gives tier-one banks legal cover to monetize API endpoints.

Spread beyond JPMorgan remains directional rather than confirmed: no source confirms Bank of America, Wells Fargo, or Citi have activated identical per-pull fee schedules. The furthest documented positions are PNC CEO Bill Demchak publicly applauding the JPMorgan move, retail banking head Alex Overstrom arguing against "free and unlimited" third-party access, and both PNC and Wells Fargo actively directing clients to Akoya, the bank-owned data network that bypasses independent aggregators. Bank of America has scheduled its API migration off credential-based scraping for early 2026, applying a strict twelve-month consent expiration to connected accounts, and a data-access network of twelve of the largest North American banks has signed agreements with Pentadata. Industry consensus expects the toll model to become the de facto standard absent a CFPB line against it.

Downstream, no Wallet-layer vendor, whether YipitData, Consumer Edge, or Antenna, has publicly disclosed client price increases attributable to API tolls. The structural mechanism is margin compression by another route: data ingestion converts from fixed-cost infrastructure to variable-cost tolling that scales with query frequency. To protect gross margin without repricing clients, vendors are incentivized to throttle polling cadence from daily toward weekly or biweekly pulls, which degrades the temporal resolution of cohort survival curves and weakens the ability to time-lag a content release against a subscription surge. Aggregators are simultaneously tiering their packages, pricing basic balance checks low and marking up the real-time longitudinal transaction histories institutional analysts require, and vendors are shifting client agreements from flat-rate platform subscriptions toward usage-restricted, ticker-specific, or API-metered pricing. No source has confirmed a dollar figure that any Wallet-layer vendor, as opposed to Plaid itself, is actually paying banks for API access, which remains the single largest open question in this section.

## DROP compliance and the August 1, 2026 trigger

California's Delete Act (SB 362, signed October 2023) is the layer's live operational exposure. The Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform, known as DROP, launched for consumer registrations January 1, 2026; by mid-2026 over 215,000 California consumers had registered deletion requests and the broker registry tracked over 500 entities (some secondary reporting cites over 600; unresolved against CPPA's own registry). Registration runs annually by January 31 with a $6,000 fee. The hard operational trigger is August 1, 2026: from that date registered brokers must query DROP at least once every 45 days, retrieve matching consumer records, and process verified deletions (with a 90-day completion window from retrieval in some framings), including derived inferences, with deletion orders cascaded to all downstream service providers and contractors and perpetual suppression so deleted consumers are not re-acquired in subsequent feed updates. Unverifiable requests must be processed as opt-outs of sale or sharing. Independent third-party compliance audits begin January 1, 2028, on a three-year cycle, with first reports due to the CPPA by January 1, 2029.

Penalties compound at $200 per deletion request per day of non-compliance, with a missed 45-day query check constituting an independent violation; a 50,000-request backlog would accrue $10 million per day, and a systemic API failure missing 5,000 requests would accrue $1 million per day, $30 million over a 30-day window. The penalty math makes manual compliance infeasible and forces automated data-subject-access-request workflows, with a typical Wallet-layer vendor's 45-day cycle operations estimated at $150,000 to $350,000 annually for database administration, audits, API maintenance, and legal review, and connected-system integrations separately estimated at $20,000 to $50,000 each. Legal analysts advise querying DROP weekly or biweekly rather than at the 45-day statutory minimum to avoid backlog-driven compounding.

As of every source pass's research date in early July 2026, the August 1 deadline had not yet passed, so no Wallet-layer vendor had been named in a DROP deletion-failure action; the question is chronologically premature rather than answered in the negative. The enforcement infrastructure, however, is demonstrably active against non-Wallet brokers on registration failures. On January 8, 2026, days after DROP went live, the CPPA's Data Broker Enforcement Strike Force announced its first decisions: S&P Global was fined for failing to register, an acknowledged administrative oversight penalized anyway at the statutory $200-per-day rate, and Rickenbacher Data LLC, doing business as Datamasters, was fined for failing to register while buying and reselling sensitive demographic, medical, and health-related lists. A separate $46,000 fine against Jerico Pictures, Inc. is reported for an earlier registration failure. The exact dollar figures for the two January 8 fines diverge across sources, one reporting Rickenbacher/Datamasters at $42,000 and S&P Global at $62,000, another reporting $45,000 and $62,600 respectively, a discrepancy that remains unresolved pending a direct check against the CPPA's own announcements. These actions pushed alternative-data providers to register, pay fees, and build matching algorithms ahead of the August deadline regardless of the exact figures.

The deeper threat is statistical rather than punitive. Transaction panels derive value from longitudinal continuity, and DROP deleters are a non-random population skewing tech-savvy, high-earning, and privacy-focused. Scrubbing their historical footprints from 2024-2025 panels distorts the baseline denominators behind churn rates, plan-mix migration, and bundle retention, injecting survivorship bias and shrinking active panel size, which mechanically widens the variance on estimated churn rates. Vendors must continuously reweight remaining panels against macro-population demographics, converting data retention from passive storage into an active liability. The burden will scale nationally: Connecticut's Public Act No. 26-64 mandates a comparable accessible deletion mechanism by July 1, 2028.

## Antenna's Subscriber Views: the deepest cross-layer fusion product

Subscriber Views is Antenna's ACR-plus-transaction fusion product and the most sophisticated cross-layer fusion currently operating in the media vertical (one source dates its unveiling to October 2025, uncorroborated elsewhere). It stitches licensed Automatic Content Recognition viewership telemetry from smart TVs against Antenna's proprietary transaction panel using identity-resolution substrates, LiveRamp's RampID and The Trade Desk's UID2 among them, linking the viewing of a specific title to a downstream financial event up to 30 days later. Standard ACR alone identifies what is on screen but cannot resolve which distribution path is in use, a standalone app versus Amazon Channels, or map the viewer to a billing account; the fusion closes both gaps. Attribution runs on three axes: acquisition, meaning which title triggered a signup; retention, meaning which content prevents churn; and engagement or plan migration, meaning viewing behavior around tier changes and cancellations.

Published case studies carry the hard numbers. On acquisition, the series *Stick* drove an estimated 33 percent of all new Apple TV+ premium signups in June 2025, roughly 700,000 gross additions. On retention, Apple TV+ subscribers who watched *The Buccaneers* in June 2025 showed a 98 percent retention rate by late July against a 92 percent platform baseline. On lifetime value, analyzing Max's promotional annual plan launched around the *House of the Dragon* premiere, a 40 percent effective monthly discount, Antenna calculated an 18-month customer lifetime value of $186.71 for promotional annual-plan subscribers versus a $122.48 benchmark for monthly-plan cohorts, 52 percent higher, evidence that deeper upfront discounts can structurally raise long-term cohort value by locking in annual plans. That CLTV pair is the most granular data point on the product across all four source passes.

Pricing and clients remain undisclosed, and the two sources that venture pricing estimates disagree materially: one puts enterprise annual platform commitments in the mid-six-to-seven-figure range scaling with tracked categories, while another describes customized annual contracts under NDA typically starting in the low-to-mid six figures. Neither is sourced to a named client contract, so both figures are held open rather than reconciled. One source, citing Antenna's Sports & Streaming 2025 report and Subscription Insider coverage, asserts adoption by sports-focused platforms tracking signups and retention around ESPN Unlimited and Fox One events; this is a single-source claim that no other pass corroborates and falls short of a confirmed client name.

Competitively, Subscriber Views faces no direct fused equivalent at YipitData or Consumer Edge. Both run larger aggregate transaction panels, but their schemas are built for merchant-category-level spend, total Netflix versus Disney+ spend, not row-level identity resolution against title-level ACR logs within SVOD release windows. The identity-resolution engineering required to match television glass data to card panels constitutes the moat, and it is currently a temporary monopoly on this fusion vector.

## Consumer Edge and Earnest Analytics: unified sales, unfused data

More than a year after the April 2025 acquisition, all four source passes converge on the same conclusion: partial integration, not a fused product. The strategic rationale was marrying Consumer Edge's transaction intelligence, direct bank feeds, credit panels, and receipt scraping spanning global panels covering over 40,000 brands across five continents and roughly 180 million accounts, with Earnest's healthcare telemetry: Phoenix Pharmacy Claims, covering longitudinal patient journeys across approximately 90 million domestic patients, Leo Medical Claims, covering medical encounters since 2016, and the Orion and Vela spend panels, alongside Consumer Edge's own Hydrus Prime Enriched medical claims asset.

The operational evidence shows sales architecture and governance unified, but data architecture is not. Neudata and other procurement platforms continue to list the legacy Earnest products as distinct offerings supported through the Consumer Edge brand, and clients access the feeds through separate S3 buckets, Snowflake shares, and Tableau Server dashboards. Consumer Edge proved the fusion capability in March 2025 with "Click," a product fusing web-traffic intent with downstream transactions, and internal communications reference "integration-led stretch assignments" through late 2025 and 2026, but institutional buyers still procure the transaction panels and the claims panels as complementary modules rather than a single mathematically fused feed. Consumer Edge also continues to market standalone signals such as Basketview Signal CPG, used to track brands including Kellanova and Mars.

The mechanistic explanation for why fusion has not shipped is twofold. First, HIPAA de-identification: claims data must be tokenized through protocols such as Datavant's to be legally licensable, and those tokens strip the temporal and geographic granularity needed to resolve a patient back to a specific card swipe without violating federal privacy law. Second, schema incompatibility: card data is organized by timestamp, merchant, and amount, while claims data is organized by ICD-10, NDC, and CPT clinical taxonomies. Mapping across them is expensive, and the resulting row-level correlations are often too sparse for reliable predictive modeling. Keeping the panels separate also contains compliance risk while both assets are cross-sold.

One development complicates a flat "still siloed" verdict: in June 2026 Consumer Edge published a combined forecasting report in which the Phoenix pharmacy claims and Hydrus medical claims datasets jointly predicted Q1 2026 pharmaceutical and medical technology revenues. Commercial-layer synthesis is running ahead of row-level data fusion.

## New entrants, exits, and the zero-party retrofit

This was the weakest-answered question across the four broader source passes; a subsequent focused-research pass closed it, identifying Coverd, an a16z-backed gamified cash-back card that launched publicly in June 2026, as a genuine new Wallet-layer entrant built on zero-party architecture (verified via a16z's portfolio listing and PR Newswire/Morningstar coverage of the June 18, 2026 launch). No incumbent has exited or shut down within strict layer scope; the layer's consolidation events remain the historical 1010data and Earnest acquisitions.

What the passes document instead is the incumbents retrofitting toward the zero-party model. The structural logic follows California's data-broker definition, which covers businesses selling personal information of consumers with whom they have no direct relationship: a vendor that compensates consumers directly, in cash, gift cards, or premium access, for voluntarily sharing transaction data establishes a direct relationship, exits the DROP deletion regime, and simultaneously mitigates Video Privacy Protection Act exposure, a statute carrying $2,500 per violation and currently being weaponized against streaming pixel tracking. The trade-off is high customer acquisition cost against a data stream immune to bank API tolls and aggregator throttling. YipitData structures acquisition around 100 percent owned-and-operated B2C and B2B panels plus more than 40 always-on direct feeds, and Antenna's core SVOD intelligence already rests on an opt-in panel and direct digital invoice tracking.

The nearest thing to zero-party challengers are the established incentivized-panel platforms rather than new corporate entrants: Fetch Rewards (17 million-plus users, SKU-level receipts), Ibotta (40 million-plus, cashback), Rakuten (15 million-plus), Shopkick (5 million-plus), and Receipt Hog (3 million-plus), with gamified financial-linking apps like Bridge and Drop maintaining high-frequency access to connected accounts. Their consent is explicit, compensated, and continually reaffirmed, and deleting a profile severs the user's own revenue stream, which suppresses churn and legal exposure by design.

Two adjacent threats round out the picture. The first is AI-native disruption: foundation-model operators with direct consumer ecosystem access, Google via Gemini's integration with Gmail receipts and Google Pay histories, OpenAI via agentic shopping frameworks parsing checkout flows, hold the latent capability to approximate high-fidelity transaction panels at near-zero marginal cost, without aggregators, making the ultimate Wallet-layer disruptors potentially the model providers themselves rather than rival data firms. The second is consolidation in adjacent measurement layers, explicitly outside strict Wallet scope but part of the same wave: Novacap's take-private of Integral Ad Science at roughly $1.9 billion (December 2025), Mediaocean's acquisition of Innovid at $3.15 per share, roughly $500 million enterprise value (November 2024), Comscore's sale of its Movies division to Advaya Capital for $70 million on May 27, 2026, with $40.1 million of proceeds retiring its Blue Torch credit facility, and Ampere Analysis acquiring PlumResearch (May 2026).
