# Layer 4: Demand/Valuation (Audience Demand & IP Valuation)

Demand/Valuation vendors do not hold authorized platform telemetry or hardware-level ACR. They ingest screen-level consumption signals, global social media activity and sentiment, search engine query velocity, peer-to-peer piracy downloads, and fan wiki engagement, then harmonize those touchpoints into synthetic demand indices that proxy actual viewership. Parrot Analytics claims a correlation above 0.9 between its global demand-expression index and reported subscriber growth across major streaming platforms, which is what lets analysts build synthetic proxies for locked ecosystems such as Apple TV+ and Amazon Prime Video. As SVOD platforms withhold precise viewership, engagement, and subscriber metrics, these indices function as the primary quantitative baseline for carriage licensing, contract renewals, and catalog asset valuation for independent studios, talent agencies, and financial institutions.

The core financial metric is the implied cost-per-demand-point: an estimated, often leaked or reported, production budget for a premium original series divided by its registered global demand-expression footprint. If the resulting figure runs above historical benchmarks, the platform is structurally overpaying for audience acquisition relative to the market; if a client's show delivers an efficient cost-per-demand-point, an agency uses that same figure to demand richer backend participation, higher renewal fees, or co-exclusive licensing terms at renewal. Institutional users, hedge funds, private equity analysts, studio corporate development teams, run a standard cross-layer triangulation workflow: an analyst first identifies a large unseasonal spike in demand expressions for a title or franchise in this layer, then, because demand expressions tend to precede subscription by days or weeks, cross-references that time-lagged signal against bottom-of-funnel transactional data from the Wallet layer. After econometric controls for confounders such as localized price increases, promotional discounts, and simultaneous competitor releases, the analyst can establish a causal link between a specific piece of IP and platform revenue, reverse-engineering subscriber acquisition costs and marginal title-level revenue contribution without any cooperation from the platform.

The layer as of mid-2026 is being reshaped by two simultaneous forces: private-equity-driven consolidation ending the era of the independent data boutique, and an existential methodological threat from LLM-mediated discovery, which silos the top-of-funnel intent signal these vendors scrape. The findings below draw on two Gemini passes, a FlashLite pass and a Pro pass conducted within hours of each other on 2026-07-06 and 2026-07-07; FlashLite serves as the factual base for M&A mechanics, case-study currency, and Gracenote's AI-discovery pivot, with Pro contributing the WWE and NFL churn-retention figures, the Previsible and Profound GEO vendor coverage, and the agentic-commerce framing. Neither pass reads as uniformly superior: FlashLite is surprisingly competitive and in places stronger, especially on dated, sourced case studies and deal mechanics, while Pro reads more narratively and covers the LLM-disruption thesis with more analytical framing.

## Ampere Analysis absorbs PlumResearch, backed by its first institutional capital

On May 21, 2026, UK-based private equity firm Goldenpeak announced a majority investment in Ampere Analysis, the London-based film, TV, gaming, and sports research firm founded in 2015, marking Ampere's first institutional backing since founding. Goldenpeak, led by Mark Williams, deployed the capital from its second fund, launched in 2025. Because the transaction constituted a fundamental change of business for the seller under AIM Rule 15, it required formal shareholder approval: at a General Meeting on June 8, 2026, shareholders representing approximately 44.3% of issued share capital committed support, and the transaction closed June 10, 2026.

Simultaneously, on May 26, 2026, Ampere announced the acquisition of Warsaw-based streaming audience analytics firm PlumResearch for an undisclosed fee, funded by the newly injected Goldenpeak capital. PlumResearch, founded 2015, had scaled its panel-based, device-agnostic tracking across more than 75 international markets, with Tar Heel Capital Pathfinder confirmed as its primary first investor (one source additionally names Montis Capital and PFR Ventures as early backers, a detail the other source does not carry, so it is held as a probable but not fully corroborated addition). Plum's 20-person core technical team, out of 42 total employees, was integrated into Ampere's corporate structure, with leadership of both firms retained for continuity: Richard Broughton continues as Executive Director of Ampere Analysis, Dan Stevenson as Managing Director of Ampere Analysis, Lukasz Jeziorski (PlumResearch's founder) remains CEO of PlumResearch, and Jonathan Broughton remains PlumResearch's Research Director.

The standalone-versus-absorbed question resolves clearly: Showlabs, Plum's proprietary viewing-log telemetry platform, survived the integration as an active, distinct product line operated by "PlumResearch, an Ampere Analysis company," rather than being sunset or folded into Ampere's backend forecasting suite. On June 16, 2026, NIQ, NielsenIQ powered by GfK intelligence, commercially launched Showlabs in Japan, delivering cross-platform analysis of viewing trends across Netflix, Prime Video, Disney+, and Crunchyroll, built on anonymized viewing log data modeled against local demographics and platform-share dynamics, targeting production companies, distributors, broadcasters, and advertisers needing empirical data for overseas expansion and distribution negotiations.

Client-facing positioning has shifted under the new ownership, though the two source passes characterize the shift differently and are carried here as a single account with that caveat attached: one frames the June 16, 2026 NIQ launch as a Japan-specific standalone deployment, while the fuller account, used above, holds that Showlabs is no longer sold in single-market, country-specific configurations, with commercial data contracts now structured as bundled regional subscriptions integrating viewing telemetry from all four supported streaming platforms across seven core markets, Japan, the United States, France, Brazil, South Korea, Taiwan, and India, a move designed to prevent geographic data fragmentation and capture enterprise-scale license fees. The strategic mandate of the acquisition is to merge Plum's real-world viewing logs with Ampere's market sizing, consumer behavior modeling, and title-level licensing forecasts into connected, end-to-end audience intelligence across the content production and media rights value chains.

## Parrot Analytics moves past *Suits* into a run of dated case studies

The standing proof-of-concept remains NBCUniversal's licensing of *Suits* to Netflix, $356 million in measured US and Canada subscriber revenue between Q1 2020 and Q4 2024 per Parrot's Streaming Economics model, over $800 million globally since 2020, a case that validated NBCU's strategic withholding of the final season until July 2024 to create a halo effect migrating engaged viewers back to Peacock. That example is now dated, and Parrot has since published a series of more current, dated case studies. A reality-competition talent-synergy study, finalized March 30, 2025 and highlighted in a July 17, 2025 global entertainment report, modeled the regional demand footprints of prospective international cast members for a major unscripted series' third season and projected a 144% lift in global audience demand from a specific casting combination, with incremental revenue projected above $120 million per season at peak global attention, convincing the partner platform to approve a larger production budget; note that as published this case is itself pre-mid-2026 and does not fully satisfy a "since mid-2026" currency bar on its own. An independent-film packaging study, published April 15, 2025, ran what-if casting scenarios against domestic opening-weekend box office and twenty-year streaming revenue projections and secured a premium licensing deal before principal photography. *The Gilded Age*, published June 29, 2025, was estimated to have delivered just over $70 million in global streaming revenue for Max as of Q1 2025, a baseline platform executives used to judge whether Julian Fellowes' brand equity justified further seasons against the *Downton Abbey* benchmark.

A mid-2025 comparative analysis of *The Last of Us* against *The White Lotus* reframes what a "hit" means. From premiere through end-2024, season one of *The Last of Us* generated just over $90 million in US and Canada streaming revenue for Max, narrowly edging the roughly $109.05 million generated by the first two seasons of *The White Lotus* over a longer window (Q3 2021 through Q3 2024, before year-end adjustments). But *The Last of Us* cost over $10 million per episode, approaching $100 million for the nine-episode season, making its domestic revenue essentially a breakeven mechanism, while *The White Lotus* season one was produced under pandemic lockdown for under $4 million per episode, with the expanded second season around $36 million, and by end-2024 had far surpassed total production cost on domestic revenue alone, rendering international revenue pure margin. The title-level attribution demonstrates that platforms cannot scale to profitability on expensive high-velocity tentpoles; the economics rest on capital-efficient assets generating outsized retention value relative to cost.

Two further studies extend the geographic and category range: Spain-originated content generated $5.1 billion in global streaming revenue over the four years preceding 2026, with the Spanish content market expected to deliver $1.4 billion in global revenue in 2024 alone (June 2026), and Japanese anime, in joint research with the Association of Japanese Animations published June 24, 2026 for the Anime Industry Report 2025, delivered $21.9 billion in combined global streaming and merchandising revenue in 2024, up 12.5% year over year, streaming growing 23.4% to $6.42 billion and merchandising generating $15.49 billion, with Netflix holding 42.6% of global anime streaming revenue, a $2.7 billion direct contribution to Netflix globally in 2024. A January 15, 2026 award (the 8th Annual Global Demand Awards) named Netflix's Argentinian sci-fi epic *The Eternaut* Most In-Demand Latin American Original of 2025, and beyond subscriber metrics documented an estimated 41 billion Argentine pesos, approximately $34 million, injected into the local Argentine economy, linking streaming demand to physical macroeconomic impact.

The most consequential recent study, published April 30, 2026, calculated that major global streaming platforms lost a combined $6.3 billion to subscriber churn in 2025, and used that loss to rewrite the sports-rights acquisition playbook. Live sports, the industry's favored retention answer, is a decaying asset in Parrot's demand tracking, delivering maximum attention for a three-hour window with retention value collapsing toward zero once the event ends; the model instead validates a 365-day retention loop built on continuous shoulder content. Netflix's blueprint is the case study: after acquiring NFL Christmas Day broadcast rights, it deployed unscripted shoulder programming, *Quarterback*, *Receiver*, *America's Sweethearts: Dallas Cowboys Cheerleaders*, that Parrot calculates actively retains approximately 500,000 global subscribers per quarter and has generated over $100 million in global streaming revenue on its own. The larger case is Netflix's ten-year, $5 billion acquisition of WWE Raw: professional wrestling runs a 52-week continuous narrative loop with no off-season, and Parrot's model calculates WWE franchise content retains 1.25 million Netflix subscribers globally every quarter, before counting international top-of-funnel growth or ad-tier monetization, a demonstration of this layer actively rewriting the sports-rights playbook rather than merely measuring it after the fact.

## Gracenote stays metadata-only, but gets aggressive about discovery friction

Gracenote, the Nielsen subsidiary, does not compete on predictive demand-side analytics or synthetic viewership footprints, and it has not been repositioned to mimic Parrot's social scraping or cost-per-demand-point metrics; that metadata-only characterization holds. What has changed is aggression at the metadata level: Nielsen has repositioned Gracenote from static descriptor supplier into an outcomes-based discovery, search optimization, and ad-tech analytics vendor attacking the same macro problems, churn and discoverability, from the infrastructure side, with explicit targeting of the FAST and CTV advertising markets. Its core dataset spans normalized program metadata for more than 50 million titles across 260-plus catalogs in 70-plus languages.

The competitive thesis rests on quantified discovery friction. Gracenote's 2025 State of Play report, published November 5, 2025 and surveying consumers across the US, UK, Germany, France, Brazil, and Mexico, found consumers globally spend 14 minutes on average searching for something to watch, with US search time rising to 12 minutes in late 2025 from 10.5 minutes in mid-2023, and French viewers posting the worst friction at 26 minutes, the length of a program episode. Forty-five percent of users find the content volume overwhelming, roughly a third of global streaming users (40% among ages 25-34) say fragmentation degrades their viewing experience, 19% of global consumers (29% of ages 18-24) abandon a viewing session entirely if the initial search fails, and 49% of surveyed streamers expressed willingness to cancel a subscription over recurring catalog-navigation difficulty.

Product moves against that friction span several fronts. By Q1 2025, Gracenote's Video Data tracked a record 1,600 individual FAST channels, up 42% since mid-2023, collectively offering more than 178,000 shows and movies; because FAST programming is non-exclusive and redundant across platforms, Gracenote argues accurate metadata, search architecture, and discoverability are the only remaining competitive moats for FAST distributors. On Sports enhancements, launched December 17, 2025, unify sports hubs covering more than 160 leagues across 50-plus countries, linking live game broadcasts to related shoulder content; US streaming sports documentary consumption grew 260%, from 4.7 million minutes in 2021 to 16.9 million in 2024, and this metadata plumbing is the connective tissue for exactly the sports retention loops Parrot's economics validate, with Gracenote enabling the habit loop rather than valuing the IP itself. On February 25, 2026, Nielsen launched over 200 new advanced audience segments in Nielsen ONE, pairing Scarborough's national consumer dataset with Gracenote catalog metadata so media buyers can transact addressable advertising on consumption habits and lifestyle categories, and late 2025 into 2026 brought programmatic and contextual CTV integrations, a PubMatic partnership powering content-informed CTV ad-buying by linking Gracenote content IDs to programmatic bidding engines, plus Xumo and Iris TV integrations supplying "Contextual Video Data" for advanced contextual targeting in place of blunt demographic targeting.

Gracenote has also produced the layer's most direct measurement of AI-mediated discovery behavior, the April 8, 2026 report "TV Search and Discovery in the AI Era," detailed below alongside the layer's foundation-model-native threat.

## Talent agencies weaponize demand data, though the newest deal remains undocumented

CAA, WME, and UTA systematically ingest IP valuation data from Parrot Analytics and Ampere Analysis during talent packaging and compensation negotiation (UTA's own analytics arm, UTA IQ, aggregates Parrot Analytics viewership data via its MediaHound acquisition for use live in negotiations). The structural driver is that streaming's cost-plus buyout model consolidated global rights and decimated the traditional backend syndication residuals that built creator wealth in linear TV, so agencies now use synthetic demand data to prove a showrunner's or actor's contribution to platform retention and acquisition, and to force negotiated synthetic backend pools. The mechanics run several tracks: title-level splits of acquisition share versus retention share (a drama like *Grey's Anatomy*, with a 7% acquisition share but a 93% retention share on Hulu, is a retention shield, and demonstrating that a creator's library prevents churn supports structured "retention bonuses" in lieu of syndication residuals); concept testing and what-if modeling through Parrot's "From Script to Stream" framework, applying historical talent-demand synergy modeling to comparative casting scenarios across specific platforms; the implied cost-per-demand-point proxy, where agency business intelligence teams divide reported or leaked production budgets by global demand-expression footprints to benchmark platform pricing efficiency, then leverage the result in either direction; exclusive-versus-co-exclusive modeling quantifying the financial risk to a platform of library titles leaving its exclusive catalog; and data-driven packaging, with WME's FY2025/2026 operating model structuring agreements around regional demand cycles and combining upfront fees with scalable backend participation and IP ownership stakes rather than flat buyouts. One documented outcome without named parties: an independent comedy series renegotiated a substantially richer renewal after demand analytics showed its global appeal was undervalued by the distributor's internal metrics.

The frontier extension is digital likeness licensing. By early 2026, WME, a subsidiary of Endeavor Group and TKO Group Holdings, was brokering deals for AI-generated "digital twins" of top clients, hyper-realistic avatars and localized voice replicas deployable in global brand campaigns and immersive events without the talent's physical presence, and WME executives project digital likeness licensing will capture between $120 million and $160 million in incremental brand and studio revenues by 2027. Because a digital twin is a synthetic asset, its licensing fees, backend payouts, and residual structures are negotiated strictly off the tracked global demand for the original human talent's IP, so a large demand multiplier in Parrot or Ampere data scales the replica's fee proportionally.

On a specific, named, post-2025 deal or dispute where demand data directly moved a backend compensation outcome, both source passes fall back on historical precedent only, the arbitration Tommy Lee Jones v. William Morris Agency (DLSE TAC 16396), in which the agency deployed structured financial analyses and demand valuations to support a $15 million backend compensation claim against Paramount. Contemporary agency negotiations remain closely guarded, and this remains the one genuinely open question in this layer, flagged for a targeted follow-up query rather than answered.

## Foundation models are quietly draining this layer's raw material

The methodological foundation of this layer, scraping open-web consumer intent, is being undermined by the migration of discovery into closed conversational AI ecosystems. A traditional Google search leaves measurable data exhaust; the same intent expressed to ChatGPT ("what sci-fi thriller should I watch tonight based on my love for *The Last of Us*?") is siloed inside OpenAI's servers, and the top-of-funnel demand signal goes dark to outside observers. By late 2025 approximately 13% of all digital searches included AI-generated answer summaries, and generative search is probabilistic rather than deterministic, with content visibility varying dynamically across models, prompts, topics, and dayparts, which is precisely why stable third-party measurement platforms are emerging to fill the gap.

Gracenote's April 8, 2026 report, surveying 4,003 US chatbot users, found 66% of all consumers (80% of Gen Alpha) reported increased use of conversational chatbots for entertainment recommendations over the preceding 18 months, and 57% said chatbots had become or were poised to become their primary mechanism for deciding why, where, and when to consume content. Previsible's 2026 State of AI Discovery Report, authored by Chief Product Officer David Bell and drawing on a 19-month benchmark across 6.77 million AI-driven sessions on 166 enterprise websites, found Google's integrated AI Overviews remain the largest AI-discovery surface, but the standalone LLM market is effectively monopolized: ChatGPT commands 92.4% of trackable standalone LLM referral traffic, Anthropic's Claude recorded 64x growth over the tracking period and displaced Perplexity in March 2026 for the developer, technical-buyer, and professional-services demographic, Gemini grew 3.2x on the strength of Google integration, and Perplexity holds niche dominance while losing aggregate share. The implication is direct: if a studio's tentpole is not cited by ChatGPT or Claude, it effectively does not exist in the conversational discovery funnel.

A new tier of measurement vendors is building specifically on foundation-model-native signals, and it is worth noting that two separate source passes named largely non-overlapping vendor sets, Previsible and Profound in one, Diesel Labs and IQRush in the other, which together map the emerging landscape more completely than either pass alone. Diesel Labs, owned by Forma Group and built to support institutional M&A transactions, launched Diesel AI ("Daisy") on July 31, 2024, fusing ChatGPT with its proprietary multi-platform audience engagement datasets for natural-language querying of consumption trends; its PanelAI platform goes further, using generative models to construct synthetic focus groups that simulate target demographics and predict consumer reactions, content preferences, and audience flows in real time, replacing slow human panels. IQRush was named a Representative Vendor in the 2026 Gartner Market Guide for Answer Engine Visibility Tools on May 12, 2026, measuring how brands, content, and products surface across ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity through citation prevalence, brand mention frequency, real-time content valuation, and e-commerce discovery tracking. Profound, a GEO and AI search discoverability platform founded by former OpenAI, Microsoft, and Uber alumni, closed a $35 million Series B led by Sequoia Capital in August 2025, bringing total institutional funding to $58.5 million, and rather than scraping consumer sentiment it reverse-engineers the LLMs themselves, processing over 100 million AI search queries monthly to deliver "Share of Voice" analytics on how often, and in what contextual sentiment, a brand's IP is recommended, operating as an agentic workbench whose AI agents automatically write localized content, publish articles, and update backend site code so a brand's data gets ingested, trusted, and cited by external LLMs, a shift from passive analytics to automated workflow execution that mirrors Whip Media's own pivot in the Funnel layer. Previsible itself is the AI visibility agency behind the State of AI Discovery benchmark above.

The next phase compounds the threat. In Previsible's framing, "AI stops recommending and starts buying, with the user never leaving the conversation." Open architectural layers, the Model Context Protocol for tool integration, the Universal Commerce Protocol for agentic discovery, and AP2 for payments, are enabling AI agents to query inventory, negotiate features, and execute transactions autonomously, at rates up to 50 per second. When agents book movie tickets, buy merchandise, or subscribe to streaming platforms inside a chat interface, traditional attribution models and open-web demand scraping break entirely, and the marketing funnel collapses into a single conversational prompt. Vendors reliant on legacy social and wiki scraping, Parrot, PlumResearch, and Diesel Labs' legacy pipelines among them, will have to re-architect ingestion for agent-mediated discovery or lose visibility into the demand signal altogether.

On one genuinely settled question underneath all this: Luminate, the PME TopCo subsidiary (the joint venture between Penske Media Corporation and Eldridge Industries) led by CEO Rob Jonas, was checked directly against three independent company-intelligence databases, PitchBook, GetLatka, and Built In, after one source reported 750 to 860 employees and $100 million to $250 million in revenue and another reported roughly 187. All three databases converge on 187 employees, with LeadIQ separately estimating roughly 193 as of June 2026, consistent within normal estimate variance, and actual reported revenue around $7.5 million ARR; the 750-860 figure and its associated revenue range match no database and most likely got pulled from an unrelated entity during generation, so they are dropped here rather than presented as a range. Luminate's data scale is described as on the order of 30 trillion data points, though the two sources diverge between "30 trillion" and "23 to 30 trillion" and that narrower figure stays open, compiled from more than 500 verified global data partners serving more than 1,400 institutional clients across major and independent music labels, film studios, and talent agencies. Luminate has powered the Billboard music charts for over 30 years, tracked delivery of approximately 106,000 unique ISRCs to digital service providers daily in 2025 (up 7% year over year), and its "Transmedia Integration" research treats music, film, television, and gaming consumption as inextricably intertwined attention flows. Luminate Film & TV, the October 2020 transition of the Variety Insight database, functions as the industry's production and scheduling registry, tracking over 100,000 executives, producers, projects, and companies from development through post-production, and powers Variety's Streaming Originals charts and the Golden Globes' verified data source. Luminate also powers the walled-garden clean room environment for Walmart Connect, the retail media network whose ad revenue, per Walmart's own SEC 8-K filings, was $4.4 billion globally in FY2025 (up 27% for the full year) and $6.4 billion in FY2026 (up 46% year over year, with Q4 FY2026 US growth of 41%), positioning Luminate as much in the Identity Resolution and Clean Rooms layer as in this one.

### Case study: Whip Media chose the AI product over the audience that built it

Whip Media is the sharpest instance of this thesis playing out on the supply side, not just the demand side. TV Time was not out-competed by a chatbot for its users' attention; its owner made a direct capital-allocation choice to shut down a data-collection product with real behavioral scale, 26 million lifetime installs of mostly unpaid, opted-in show-tracking activity, precisely the kind of voluntary consumer-intent signal this layer is built on, in favor of selling AI-native automation tooling to the studios and platforms that would otherwise have been Whip Media's data customers. Blue Torch Capital, a restructuring-focused direct lender rather than a venture investor optimizing for audience growth, acquired Whip Media for its enterprise contracts and technical talent; the consumer community was the part of the balance sheet that did not carry forward. The pattern generalizes beyond one company: as foundation models absorb the discovery layer described above, the same economic logic increasingly favors selling AI infrastructure to a small number of institutional buyers over maintaining a free consumer product that only ever monetized indirectly, through the data it generated as a byproduct. The data moat and the AI-tooling business built on top of it are now competing for the same capital inside the same company, and at Whip Media, the tooling business won outright.

A handful of smaller discrepancies round out the record without changing any conclusion: Whip Media's capital raised is cited as either "over $115 million" or a more precise $115.5 million through its January 2020 Series D, a trivial spread; Whip Media itself, acquired February 19, 2025 by Blue Torch Capital, a restructuring-focused direct lender, ended its venture era, with Welby Chen appointed CEO in February 2025 and Richard Rosenblatt as Executive Chair, and by September 2025 held an estimated $11.4 million ARR against a $34.3 million valuation with roughly 104 employees; under Blue Torch it executed a hard pivot out of consumer-facing data, announcing on July 2, 2026 that TV Time, its tracking app with over 26 million lifetime installs, would shut down July 15, 2026, with all technical resources moving to Helix, an AI-native enterprise B2B SaaS platform automating content supply chains and streaming analytics for named clients including Cineverse and MPI Media Group. TV Time's original 2013 founder, Antonio Pinto, no longer affiliated with Whip Media, separately launched a community successor app called Bingers around the shutdown date, rebuilt independently and importing users' GDPR-exported TV Time archives; as of this writing Bingers has no institutional data product and sits outside this layer's scope, worth revisiting only if that changes.
