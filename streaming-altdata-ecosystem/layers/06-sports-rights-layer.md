# Layer 6: Sports-Rights Sub-Vertical

The sports-rights layer sits inside the eight-layer alt-data architecture as the sub-vertical handling what standard SVOD transaction panels cannot: synchronous low-latency delivery, betting-handle correlation, and regional blackout compliance at scale. Demand for the whole ecosystem is generated structurally by the gatekeeper layer (Amazon, Apple, Roku, Google/YouTube, Disney, Fox) withholding downstream metrics and monetizing withheld first-party data through Roku Data Cloud and Amazon Marketing Cloud, which forces studios and investors to build synthetic proxies through the Wallet, Glass, Shield, Demand/Valuation, Funnel, and Identity layers elsewhere in this framework. Cross-layer triangulation is the operating method throughout: a Parrot demand spike checked against Antenna transaction cohorts, controlled for price changes, resolved through clean-room identity matching. This layer is structurally unique within that stack because it runs on exclusive, multi-year league data licensing rather than consumer transaction consent, which is why its vendors, Stats Perform, Sportradar, and Genius Sports, hold pricing power no panel vendor has.

The operative thesis here, carried over from the project's broader "Great Collision" framing and validated across all three source passes, is that exclusive league data rights function as a permission-layer toll, structurally parallel to semiconductor lithography access: official data must be licensed by every participant in the downstream ecosystem, competing networks, regulated sportsbooks, programmatic advertisers, and now prediction markets, regardless of which bidder wins the video rights. As broadcast rights costs escalate, the data layer is consolidating into fewer, deeper, longer deals, increasingly laced with direct league equity in the vendors, not fragmenting.

The findings below draw on three Gemini Pro-tier Deep Research passes, all conducted 2026-07-07. One pass, run first that morning, supplies the technical-infrastructure background (Paris 2024, NFL Next Gen Stats, Netflix's Open Connect rebuild) and a betting-economics range; a second, run later that afternoon, supplies the eight-layer taxonomy framing and the NCAA-2032 detail; a third, run one minute after the second, supplies the Kalshi deal, the xGOT and FIFA Integrity Task Force material, the confirmed Fox One standalone price, the MLB.TV mechanism, the Barstool deal terms, the Savannah Bananas case, and the Fox-Roku acquisition, and serves as this layer's base document.

## Stats Perform becomes FIFA's first official betting-data partner, and inherits its integrity problem

In January 2026 FIFA appointed Stats Perform as its first-ever official worldwide betting-data and betting-streaming rights distributor. The multi-year agreement grants exclusive distribution of live data and video for the expanded 48-team, 104-match 2026 Men's World Cup across the United States, Canada, and Mexico, the 2027 Women's World Cup in Brazil, the 2028 Futsal World Cup, the 2026 and 2028 U-20 Women's World Cups, the 2027 and 2029 U-20 World Cups, and the FIFA Intercontinental Cup through 2029, plus exclusive betting rights to thousands of member-association matches per season powered by the FIFA+ platform.

Execution rests on a dual-engine architecture. RunningBall handles ultra-low latency in-play data, with trained scouts inside stadiums on dedicated secure Wi-Fi delivering deterministic pitch-to-trading-floor data in under one second, the requirement for automated market making, longer live betting windows, avoidance of unnecessary market suspensions, and immediate settlement. Opta handles the semantic and predictive tier, running proprietary foundation models trained on 7.2 petabytes of historical sports data with direct broadcast-video ingestion; its Expected Goals on Target (xGOT) model is the flagship for 2026, an unusual post-shot model evaluating goal likelihood from the actual shot trajectory and end location in the goalmouth rather than pre-shot probability, isolating finishing skill and goalkeeper performance and letting sportsbooks price highly specific in-play markets. The Bet LiveStreams service additionally distributes live match video directly into licensed sportsbook applications in selected territories, bundling data with video so the sportsbook becomes a sticky media surface rather than a spreadsheet, with distribution integration extending to TikTok and DAZN.

The expanded 104-match format has also radically widened the corruption surface. The core structural risk is micro-bet spot-fixing, since RunningBall enables wagers on isolated events, the exact minute of a throw-in, specific player cards, corner counts, that a single player can manipulate without throwing the match, disguised as ordinary error; critics frame this as a paradox, since commercializing ultra-fast data through Stats Perform deepens the sport's reliance on the betting ecosystem and multiplies the markets requiring surveillance. FIFA's response was an Integrity Task Force established in May 2026, integrating Stats Perform's integrity division alongside the FBI, INTERPOL, the UN Office on Drugs and Crime, and the integrity units of competitors Sportradar and Genius Sports, using market monitoring and reporting tools embedded in the official FIFA Player app. As of this document's build date, mid-tournament, Stats Perform retained exclusivity with no reported commercial breaches or competitive challenges to its position.

## Sports Innovation Lab and Zoomph, a year on, ends in acquisition

The April 2025 partnership between Sports Innovation Lab (SIL) and Zoomph fused SIL's deterministic Fluid Fan Graph, built on billions of indexed purchases, ticket sales, merchandise, and subscription transactions across millions of households, with Zoomph's real-time AI-driven social audience intelligence, targeting sponsorship measurement across social, broadcast, and digital. What actually shipped, with named clients: fan-segmentation work for the Washington Wizards identifying high-value audiences to optimize ad spend and lift ticket sales, market-level data analysis across all 12 WNBA teams powering the league's five-year growth and fan-acquisition strategy, and quantification of the impact of Ally Financial's 50/50 pledge on gender-equity sports spending; broader named users of the SIL stack include Legends, Kellanova, NASCAR, and Monumental Sports and Entertainment. Reported aggregate results for brands using SIL-targeted audiences run 4% higher engagement, 16% lower cost per acquisition, and a 0.2x return-on-ad-spend lift. SIL's underlying research claim is that Fluid Fans are roughly 10% of the sports fan base but drive more than 50% of total revenue, with women's-sports fans the pinnacle archetype as early adopters of streaming and direct commerce out of necessity, rewarding early sponsors such as Budweiser (NWSL), Visa (USWNT), and Nike (WNBA, NWSL, USWNT).

The partnership's endgame was acquisition: Genius Sports bought SIL in September 2025 for an undisclosed sum. The strategic logic is closed-loop attribution: Genius's FanHub ID already tracked media consumption across digital, social, mobile, and CTV but lacked the link to actual commerce, and the Fluid Fan Graph supplied the transactional half. Productized through Genius's FANHub programmatic offering and its Moment Engine advertising product, the combination lets brands connect campaign exposure during live games to measurable real-world transactions, and the financial impact was immediate: Genius's media division reported an 88.9% year-over-year revenue jump to $41.8 million in Q3 2025, credited to the SIL acquisition and the FANHub rollout.

## Streaming platforms build viewer-facing AI in-house, but stay dependent on the vendor triopoly for certified data

The answer to whether streaming platforms are building sports-data infrastructure in-house is a strategic bifurcation rather than a binary build-versus-buy choice. For advertising monetization, audience targeting, personalization, and delivery infrastructure, the platforms are absolutely building in-house, with individual technology budgets of $1 billion to over $5 billion annually and hundreds of dedicated AI scientists. For certified live match integrity, betting settlement, and official optical tracking, they remain wholly dependent on the vendor triopoly, because replicating sub-second certified collection across hundreds of federations plus regulated sportsbook API settlement is prohibitive even at Big Tech scale.

The in-house build is real and extensive. Amazon's Thursday Night Football architecture on AWS guarantees stadium-to-screen latency of 10 seconds or less, and its NFL Next Gen Stats partnership processes millions of tracking points per game from RFID chips in all 22 players' shoulder pads plus ultrawideband sensors inside the ball, through 75 concurrent machine-learning models executing in under one second; heading into the 2025 season the Next Gen Stats team adopted transformer architectures interpreting every player's spatial-temporal dynamics at one-tenth-second intervals, yielding new metrics such as Coverage Responsibility and Pressure Probability. Amazon monetizes this telemetry directly through the Prime Vision with Next Gen Stats alternate broadcast, feeding engagement data back into its retail ad stack. Netflix, to serve its three-season NFL Christmas Day deal, had to rebuild its Open Connect CDN for live: the standard predictive-fill model, pre-positioning encoded assets on Open Connect Appliances inside ISP networks, is useless for live events, so Netflix engineered a real-time push ingest path to edge-optimized OCAs with DPDK kernel-bypass networking targeting sub-four-second glass-to-glass latency, with Apache Kafka managing broadcast telemetry, Apache Chukwa collecting it, and Hystrix isolating microservice failures. Its inaugural NFL broadcast averaged 26.5 million US viewers per game with ad inventory sold out weeks in advance, monetized through an in-house ad platform plus EverPass Media for commercial venues. The Paris 2024 Olympics stands as the cloud-broadcast blueprint the platforms are converging on: OBS Cloud 3.0 with Alibaba Cloud carried 379 video and 100 audio feeds to worldwide broadcasters, replacing satellite and leased circuits, Alibaba's multi-camera AI replay systems covered 14 venues and 21 sports with live spatial reconstruction, Intel Xeon processors with AI Deep Learning Boost encoded 8K live streams, and Peacock layered on multiview and AI-generated Al Michaels voice narration for personalized highlights.

The dependency side is just as concrete. Amazon's NBA operation is the clean illustration: it rebuilt the TNT broadcast wholesale, yet underneath that bespoke production wrapper the official NBA optical tracking data, live odds, and embedded betting widgets remain exclusively controlled and distributed by Sportradar under its NBA deal running through the 2030-31 season. On the tracking-technology frontier the vendors are ahead of the platforms, not behind them: Genius's computer-vision mesh tracking captures surface data points at up to 10,000 times per second across the English Premier League and FIBA with millimeter accuracy and no wearables, processed through GeniusIQ into a live digital twin of the game, and the Premier League's Microsoft Azure and Copilot integration shows the same pattern of league-side AI layered on vendor-collected telemetry. The conclusion holds across all three passes: platforms internalize viewer-facing analytics and delivery, and keep paying the toll for certified betting-grade data.

## Betting-handle correlation now runs directly into prediction markets

The named case for how this layer's data reaches sportsbooks and, increasingly, prediction markets is Sportradar's June 2026 partnership with Kalshi, a first-of-its-kind multi-year global data and infrastructure agreement with the largest CFTC-regulated prediction market. Sportradar supplies official data, live odds, and AI-powered integrity monitoring across MLB, the NHL, MLS, the UFC, and the NBA, and, the structurally important term, retains the explicit right to sublicense its low-latency feeds directly to the third-party brokers and automated market makers providing liquidity inside Kalshi's exchange. Feeding the exchange and its liquidity providers the same sub-second certified feed eliminates latency arbitrage, the failure mode where traders in the stadium or on faster feeds pick off stale exchange prices; a 10-second broadcast delay on Amazon's optimized NFL feed, or 40 seconds on standard OTT, is an eternity against algorithmic trading. By mid-2026 sports contracts were roughly 80% of Kalshi's entire trading volume, and Polymarket runs the same playbook on the decentralized side, sourcing official Serie A data through Genius Sports.

The economics behind that correlation matter as much as the mechanism. The leading vendors do not charge flat SaaS fees for betting services; they take a revenue share tied to operator Gross Gaming Revenue, with top-tier providers charging between 12% and 25% of GGR for risk management, trading tools, and live odds platforms. Sportradar generates roughly 80% of revenue from its betting segment on this model, with fixed SaaS data feeds a stable floor around 15% of revenue, plus Managed Trading Services for smaller books and the ad:s performance-marketing product capturing operator acquisition budgets. As handle shifts from pre-match to in-play and micro-betting, dependence on sub-second data deepens and vendor pricing power compounds. No source discloses actual dollar terms for any single sportsbook or prediction-market data deal, which remains the one hard number missing from this section.

## The triopoly consolidates further, and the leagues start taking equity in it

The layer has consolidated into a triopoly: Stats Perform, private and holding FIFA, and two public names, Sportradar Group AG (Nasdaq: SRAD) and Genius Sports Limited (NYSE: GENI). The consolidation-not-fragmentation thesis verifies league by league, with a structural kicker worth stating plainly: the leagues are increasingly taking equity in their own data vendors, binding rights holder to toll collector.

Sportradar's rights portfolio runs deep: NBA exclusive ultra-low latency data and audiovisual rights through the 2030-31 season, with the NBA an existing Sportradar shareholder; MLB extended in February 2025 through the 2032 season, with MLB issued up to 1,855,724 Class A ordinary shares over the contract, covering exclusive distribution of Statcast optical-tracking data and audiovisual content to a network of over 800 sportsbook clients and 900 media companies, plus Synergy Sports coaching-and-scouting deployment across all 30 MLB clubs processing over 20,000 amateur games; NHL under the 2021 ten-year extension through 2031-32; UEFA extended through 2027 across all club and national-team competitions including the Champions League; Bundesliga renewed early through 2031-32; and NASCAR, expanded in August 2023 to include official betting data rights, breaking Genius's prior exclusivity on the property, with full telemetry (position, speed, pit stops) across 50-plus drivers packaged into head-to-head and stage-winner markets. In November 2025 Sportradar acquired IMG ARENA in a uniquely structured deal, being paid $225 million to take over the business and absorb its tennis, golf, and soccer rights portfolio. Q1 2026 financials: revenue €346.5 million, up 11% year over year, led by Betting Technology & Solutions at €287.6 million, up 15%; adjusted EBITDA €66.0 million at a 19.0% margin; net loss €6.3 million, driven by a €9.3 million foreign-currency loss on US-dollar rights obligations plus IMG ARENA amortization; free cash flow €44.0 million; €542 million total liquidity, zero debt; a new $250 million enhanced open-market repurchase inside a $1.0 billion authorization, with 12.5 million shares already repurchased for $228 million; full-year 2026 guidance of €1.557 to €1.582 billion revenue with 200 to 225 basis points of adjusted EBITDA margin expansion; trailing-twelve-month revenue roughly $1.55 billion; and Sameer Deen appointed Chief Operating Officer effective May 18, 2026. One source adds an unverified market-structure estimate of roughly 40% share of the global third-party sports data market.

Genius Sports' portfolio centers on the NFL exclusive data distribution contract through 2029, its foundational moat, with the league holding approximately 8.7% equity in the vendor, its largest shareholder position, deliberately calibrated below the 10% threshold that would trigger added SEC disclosure obligations (confirmed via Sportico's reporting on the June 2025 partnership extension); also English Premier League optical tracking, and the NCAA, extended in April 2025 through 2032, which for the first time lets licensed sportsbooks use official NCAA data, marks, and logos for all postseason championships including the men's and women's Division I basketball tournaments, conditioned on integrity terms including a first-of-its-kind ban on high-risk proposition bets (player underperformance wagers, negative-outcome bets, wagers on injuries or officiating decisions). Genius has run NCAA LiveStats since 2018 under a ten-year exclusive, powering over 70,000 games annually across all three divisions, now layered with the GeniusIQ AI platform. Q1 2026 financials: group revenue $188.0 million, up 31% (Betting Technology, Content & Services $146.2 million, up 33%; Media Technology, Content & Services $41.7 million, up 22%); adjusted EBITDA $24.0 million, up 21%, a 12.8% margin; net loss widened to $55.5 million from $8.2 million a year earlier on $85.6 million of data and streaming rights costs, $30.9 million of stock-based compensation, and $7.5 million of one-time Legend transaction expenses; cash $197.4 million after $66.4 million of operating outflows.

The Legend acquisition is worth resolving precisely, since the underlying source material contained an internal inconsistency rather than a genuine cross-source conflict: two different financing figures, an $850 million Term Loan B and an $825 million term loan plus $220 million revolver, appeared inside a single source document in two paragraphs that never reconciled, evidently because signing-stage coverage and closing coverage were pulled without noticing they described the same fact at two points in time. Checked against Genius Sports' own 6-K and credit-agreement disclosure filed with the SEC, the settled record is that at close (April 30, 2026), consideration ran $800 million cash plus 10 million-plus new shares, funded by a new credit agreement comprising an $825 million senior secured Term Loan A, not B, at SOFR plus 350 basis points and a $220 million revolving facility, both maturing April 30, 2031; the $850 million Term Loan B figure was stale signing-stage coverage and is not carried forward. The transaction, valued at up to $1.2 billion, brought the Covers.com, Casino.org, and Casino Guru affiliate network, 320 million annual visits from 118 million unique visitors in 2025, converting Genius from B2B data infrastructure into an integrated sports and gaming media network that collects on the front end (cost-per-acquisition affiliate fees routing bettors to operators) and the back end (GGR share when the bet settles on its data). Post-close, management raised full-year 2026 guidance to $990 million to $1.01 billion of revenue and $270 to $280 million of adjusted EBITDA, lifting the margin target from 23% to 28%, targets pulled forward two years, with a 35% adjusted EBITDA margin target by 2028 and at least 60% free cash flow conversion.

The toll-layer thesis, in short, is confirmed and stronger than the framing that originated it: fewer, deeper, longer deals; league equity stakes, MLB and the NBA in Sportradar, the NFL in Genius, that align rights holders with vendor economics; and monetization expanding horizontally from sportsbooks into programmatic advertising (SIL), affiliate media (Legend), and financial exchanges (Kalshi). A commonly cited "400-plus leagues" aggregate for the toll-layer thesis was never independently tallied by any of the three source passes and should be treated as directional rather than verified.

## League breadth: the NHL runs the standard model, the College Football Playoff carves out an exception, and the Savannah Bananas opt out entirely

The NHL operates entirely inside the incumbent vendor model: the 2021 ten-year Sportradar extension runs through the 2031-32 season and designates Sportradar the official betting-data, betting-streaming, and media-data rights partner, including distribution of the NHL's puck-and-player tracking system output for both performance analytics and betting correlation, with Sportradar's Universal Fraud Detection System monitoring global betting activity on NHL games, the same integrity net used for MLB and the NBA.

The College Football Playoff runs a bifurcated posture worth noting because it demonstrates that ultra-premium properties retain leverage to ring-fence their data and negotiate standalone. The CFP sits outside the NCAA's commercial jurisdiction and is explicitly excluded from the Genius-NCAA 2032 agreement: SportSource Analytics is the certified official data and statistics provider to the CFP selection committee, the historical and situational repository behind team evaluation and bracket determination, while the live collection plumbing across collegiate athletics remains Genius's NCAA LiveStats. The CFP therefore runs a layered vendor approach, Genius for real-time collection and commercial distribution, SportSource as the committee's certified analytical engine.

The Savannah Bananas are the genuine opt-out case, real but bounded. Banana Ball operates completely outside the Sportradar and Genius ecosystem: no licensed low-latency feed, no betting-data deal, self-distribution via YouTube and owned channels, with monetization running through an ad-tech partnership with Transmit inserting automated contextual video ads into livestreams, which in the 2025 season lifted monetizable in-stream impressions 300% at a near-100% video completion rate. The franchise reached an estimated $500 million valuation and generates outsized event economics, including $17.1 million of economic impact in a single weekend at UNC's Kenan Stadium. The structural limit is that this model works precisely because Banana Ball has no betting-market relevance; scripted entertainment elements, spontaneous rule changes, and non-standard competition cannot legally or mathematically support regulated wagering, so there is no toll to pay and no toll revenue to collect. Opting out of the vendor layer is therefore a viable and lucrative pattern for creator-led exhibition properties, and structurally impossible for competitive leagues dependent on gaming revenue.

## Sports-media journalism as a rights-adjacent track that survives platform turnover

Netflix's deal with Barstool, announced December 17, 2025 and now verified with terms, is a multi-year partnership valued at an estimated $60 million over three years with a $15 million guaranteed annual payment, securing exclusive video distribution rights to Barstool's three flagship podcasts, *Pardon My Take*, *Spittin' Chiclets*, and *The Ryen Russillo Show*. Active since early 2026, the video versions left YouTube and went exclusively behind Netflix, while audio remains open on Apple Podcasts and Spotify, preserving Barstool's existing podcast ad revenue, a deliberate split that keeps the deal cheap for Netflix and safe for Barstool: platforms are buying mass sports attention through creator-led commentary at a small fraction of live-rights cost.

NBA Countdown continues operating as an independent studio and journalism property through the NBA rights fracturing across ESPN/ABC, NBC, and Amazon, and Amazon's own NBA broadcast and studio operation is built entirely on separately sourced broadcast talent and production, a production and journalism play with no commercial relationship to the rights-data vendors for the wrapper itself. The vendor relationship sits underneath it: the official data, odds, and betting widgets inside the product remain Sportradar's under the NBA exclusivity. That division, talent above, toll below, is the general pattern for this track, and studio journalism infrastructure survives rights turnover as the narrative wrapper that retains audience loyalty across platform transitions.

## ESPN Unlimited: the MLB.TV aggregation mechanism, and the carriage-dispute sequence it actually caused

ESPN Unlimited launched in August 2025 at $29.99 per month carrying the entirety of ESPN's linear feeds (ESPN, ESPN2, ESPNU, SEC Network, ACC Network) plus digital-exclusive content. Its MLB.TV integration is a real structural mechanism, not marketing language: subscribers who purchase MLB.TV, $47.99 for the 2026 season or $29.99 per month, activate it through the ESPN account-management portal, which links the MLB account to the MyDisney account architecture, and once that handshake completes, live out-of-market MLB broadcasts play natively inside the ESPN App and ESPN.com with no switch to the MLB app. This is a TV Everywhere-style authentication pass-through executed inside a DTC environment under a revenue-share arrangement, aggregating a league-owned property under ESPN's unified interface and keeping the user inside Disney's walled garden. No comparable NBA League Pass integration is documented in this research, and whether one exists remains unconfirmed.

The carriage-dispute sequence is settled, and it runs opposite to how this framework's own `espn-retention-analysis.md` originally carried it. ESPN Unlimited launched August 21, 2025. The Google/YouTube TV blackout followed roughly three months later, in November 2025: Disney pulled ESPN, ABC, and FX for nearly two weeks against YouTube TV's roughly 8 million subscribers, at a cost to ESPN of roughly $110 million in affiliate and advertising revenue. The blackout did not precede or cause the launch; it was a distributor reaction to it. The mechanism, per a sourced primary-citation timeline built independently of the three research passes: ESPN launched Unlimited without the leverage to dictate carriage terms, then built that leverage afterward through the NFL's 10% ESPN equity stake plus the NFL Network and RedZone acquisition, the $325 million per year WWE rights deal, and a CW sublicensing pact covering ACC, Mountain West, and Pac-12 games. Every other major distributor, Charter Spectrum, DirecTV, Fubo, Cox, folded and granted authenticated app access to their pay-TV subscribers at no extra charge; Google alone held out, producing the two-week blackout, which resolved with YouTube TV agreeing to fully integrate ESPN Unlimited into its base-plan subscriber interface. The economic logic is that a comprehensive unbundled DTC product triggers distributor cannibalization anxiety, so the carriage fight is a symptom of the DTC pivot, not its genesis, and having Unlimited already live in market gave Disney a functional off-ramp for stranded YouTube TV subscribers during the standoff. Warner Bros. Discovery (Max) and Paramount Skydance (Paramount+) are named generically as routinely encountering identical distributor pushback and blackout threats after DTC investments, but no source supplies a dated, concrete parallel case for either.

## Fox One: priced to protect the linear bundle, and merged into a distribution rail through Roku

Fox One launched August 21, 2025, the same day as ESPN Unlimited. Its settled pricing, confirmed directly against Fox's own pricing page, is $19.99 per month or $199.99 per year standalone, $24.99 per month for Fox One plus Fox Nation, $39.99 per month for the ESPN Unlimited plus Fox One bundle, and $12.99 per month or $89.99 per year for the B1G+ (Big Ten Plus) add-on on any plan; one source had reported a flat $39.99 for the standalone service, conflating it with the ESPN-bundled tier, an error the pricing-page check resolves cleanly. Content mix is near-entirely live sports, news, and local feeds, Fox Sports, Fox News, Fox Business, Fox Weather, FS1, FS2, the Big Ten Network, and local affiliate broadcasts, with no entertainment SVOD library. The pricing rationale the research confirms is that the standalone price was set deliberately high enough to create friction for casual cord-cutters, protecting Fox's high-margin traditional pay-TV carriage and linear advertising (FOXA's revenue model is ad-dependent, not subscription-dependent, with roughly $2.2 billion per year committed to NFL rights as a survival cost), while still satisfying the mandate to stream marquee events, the Super Bowl and the 2026 FIFA World Cup, and serving superfans who have fully left the bundle.

The Fox-Roku acquisition completes the structural picture. In June 2026 Fox entered a definitive agreement to acquire Roku for $22 billion at $160.00 per Roku share, $96.00 cash plus 0.9693 FOXA shares, leaving existing FOXA/FOXB holders roughly 73% of the combined company. Rather than building a Disney+-equivalent entertainment platform, Fox bought the operating system of the digital living room: the acquisition merges Tubi with The Roku Channel, combined 5.2% of US streaming viewing time, trailing only YouTube, Netflix, and Disney's combined assets, pairs Fox's live sports and news with Roku's first-party data across roughly 100 million global streaming households, and guarantees friction-free distribution for Fox One and Tubi, structurally bypassing the carriage-dispute dynamic that produced Disney's YouTube TV blackout. The characterization that "Fox is the ESPN arm of Disney, without a Disney+-equivalent entertainment platform to bundle into" holds up as an accurate structural read, and the Roku deal is Fox leaning into that identity, owning the distribution rail and the ad yield, rather than correcting it.
