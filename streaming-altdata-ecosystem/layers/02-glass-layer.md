# Layer 2: Glass (Hardware / ACR)

The Glass layer is the hardware and telemetry substrate of streaming measurement: chipset-level Automatic Content Recognition (ACR) embedded in smart TVs and set-top boxes, capturing pixel-level and audio-fingerprint data off the screen itself, agnostic to whether the source is linear broadcast, a CTV app, a DVR, or a direct-to-consumer streamer. Primary operators are the OEMs, Vizio, Samsung, and LG, plus the specialized data and measurement firms riding on their telemetry: Samba TV, VideoAmp, iSpot.tv, Comscore, and the incumbent Nielsen. Buyers skew toward agency holding companies, brand advertisers, and network ad-sales teams; the data is the raw material of transactable TV-advertising currency, a national measurement-spend market estimated at $1.5 billion to $2 billion annually.

Two structural constraints govern the layer. The first is methodology: ACR data is deterministic but not representative, since OEM installed bases carry socio-economic and demographic skews, so currency-grade use requires synthetic panel weighting, proprietary calibration such as a National Representative Panel approach, to align raw device telemetry with Census benchmarks. The second is law: the layer's consent regime is device-level opt-in, distinct from the Wallet layer's financial-transaction consent, and its dominant statutory exposure is the Video Privacy Protection Act, which allows liquidated damages of up to $2,500 per individual violation. Sony and Vizio both carry a class-action history over ACR collection without meaningful informed consent, and that exposure currently sits inside a live federal circuit split: the Second Circuit's broad reading in *Salazar v. National Basketball Ass'n* holds that sharing a user's video consumption data with even a single third party, such as transmitting viewing history to Meta via embedded pixels, is sufficient for strict liability, while the Sixth Circuit's *Paramount* decision reads the statute's "consumer" definition narrowly, requiring plaintiffs to be subscribers to goods or services in the nature of audiovisual material specifically, not merely any subscriber of the company, which shields platforms from suits by incidental site visitors. The split is unsettled; the Supreme Court granted certiorari in *Salazar v. Paramount Global* (Docket No. 25-459) on January 26, 2026, with a ruling expected in the first half of 2027 (confirmed against the Court's own docket), and the uncertainty has pushed Glass-layer participants into differential privacy, anonymization, and clean-room architectures before viewership data can be commercialized. GDPR enforcement has structurally prevented replication of United States-style granular ACR methodologies in the European Union, confining the layer's most advanced products to the US market. In institutional practice the layer is rarely used alone: measurement architects match licensed ACR tuning data against deterministic transaction panels from the Wallet layer inside clean rooms, and Antenna's Subscriber Views product is the premier example, stitching ACR consumption to financial transaction panels to prove exposure-to-purchase without exchanging raw PII.

## Nielsen's 2025-2026 credibility crisis, and why it is really two stories

Nielsen holds an estimated 80% to 90% of national TV advertising currency transactions. Its MRC accreditation was suspended in 2021 over pandemic-era undercounting and restored in April 2023. The 2025-2026 episode is best understood as two distinct but interlocking controversies, one regulatory-methodological and one commercial, that are the same event chain viewed from different ends rather than competing narratives.

On the MRC audit track, Nielsen's National Big Data + Panel service, a hybrid combining its roughly 42,000-household panel with return-path and ACR data from 45 million smart TVs and set-top boxes, was granted full MRC accreditation on January 22, 2025, the first hybrid measurement service accredited as a national TV currency. During first-half 2025 audit work the MRC identified "seemingly unusual changes" in results, most glaringly an unexplained average decline of more than 10% in total day impressions for adults 25-54 versus the same period a year earlier, plus representation problems in household viewing technology and demographics and severe variances between panel-only and hybrid estimates. In September 2025 the MRC mandated four priority fixes as a condition of continued accreditation: an independent media-related universe estimate source, demographic assignment modeling changes, weighting simplification to reduce standard error, and remediation of underrepresented segments, specifically Hispanic and Spanish-dominant households, all detailed publicly in a highly unusual marketplace update on March 3, 2026.

Nielsen implemented the Advertising Research Foundation's DASH study as its independent universe-estimate source in February 2026, with immediate improvement in demographic representation, and scheduled the remaining modeling and weighting fixes for April 2026. On May 27, 2026, the MRC released a status update confirming that Nielsen had requested, and the MRC had granted, a delay of those remaining fixes to August 31, 2026, framed by the MRC's own materials as driven by Nielsen's customers, who wanted parallel impact data before billions in audience guarantees repriced under new math, a request the MRC judged reasonable. As of mid-2026, National Big Data + Panel remains fully MRC accredited, the 2025 audit remains open specifically to review the delayed August 31, 2026 implementations, and the mandated fixes have not yet shipped. Separately, Nielsen placed its Audio Diary service on a six-month MRC accreditation hiatus, June 23 through December 23, 2026, to overhaul digital data collection and combat survey fatigue, which has no direct bearing on national TV standing but signals methodological strain across the enterprise.

On the VAB Gauge suppression track, the Gauge, Nielsen's monthly linear-versus-streaming share-of-viewing scorecard, is not a transactional currency but heavily shapes upfront narrative and channel allocation. The February 2026 edition, originally scheduled for release March 17, 2026, showed a stark reversal: per unreleased data leaked to the Wall Street Journal on March 19, 2026, linear TV captured 47.4% of US viewing time against streaming's 41.9%, versus January's streaming 47% and linear 42.7%, with YouTube's share of TV-set viewing dropping from 12.5% to 11% in the same leak. February contained both the Super Bowl and the Winter Olympics, and broadcasters read the print as proof of linear's live-event reach heading into spring negotiations.

Nielsen delayed the report after inquiries from major streaming platforms, then announced it would revert the February calculation to the older methodology, arguing the new ARF-based approach, the very methodology the MRC mandated, would produce a "one-time shift" needing time to smooth, and pushed the new math to fall 2026, past the upfront window. On March 26, 2026, VAB President and CEO Sean Cunningham formally accused Nielsen of "indefensible manipulations" and "obvious interference in markets," charging that the reversion systematically undercounted linear and out-of-home audiences precisely when roughly 70% of national TV budgets get committed. The dispute has escalated from methodology to liability, with broadcasting executives, notably NBCUniversal's Mark Marshall, introducing "shareholder harm" framing, the argument that systematically flawed measurement inputs artificially under-compensated linear broadcasters and damaged equity valuations, implicitly inviting securities regulators and plaintiff firms into the measurement ecosystem.

Read together, the two tracks tell one story: the MRC-mandated ARF universe estimates produced data favorable to linear, streaming platforms objected, and Nielsen suppressed the favorable print, reverted methodology, and delayed the mandated fixes until after 2026 upfront capital deployed, accepting an open audit status in exchange. The industry is transacting billions on a currency whose regulator has said the weighting needs fundamental fixes that the vendor has deferred past the selling season.

## VideoAmp and iSpot.tv scale into the 2026 upfronts

VideoAmp's currency volume scaled from roughly $20 million in 2022 to approximately $3 billion in the 2024 upfronts, with the company projecting roughly $6 billion in currency and measurement transactions for the 2026 broadcast year, a figure that remains a projection rather than a confirmed actual. Capitalization stands at a $1.4 billion valuation in 2021 and a $150 million Series G from Vista Credit Partners in late 2023. Enterprise proof points include a multi-year measurement renewal positioning VideoAmp as primary measurement partner across Warner Bros. Discovery's digital, linear, and cross-platform campaigns, advanced-currency deals with independent broadcasters Family Entertainment Television and Weigel Broadcasting Co. (nine national multicast networks including MeTV) in April and May 2026, and, during a four-month contract dispute in early 2025, Paramount explicitly transitioning advertisers' campaigns onto VideoAmp data, demonstrating viability as a primary Nielsen replacement. Its VXP cross-platform planner expanded in early 2026 with SiriusXM and National CineMedia first-party data plus an AI-powered reporting semantic layer. Regulatory standing: the JIC granted VideoAmp full, unconditional national currency certification in early 2024 after evaluations spanning nearly 700 technical tests, but VideoAmp is not MRC accredited; it formally initiated the MRC audit in May 2025, submitting its VALID big-data engine against the MRC Minimum Standards, and its metrics remain designated "under review" throughout 2026.

iSpot.tv reported $128 million total revenue for 2025, up 22% year over year, shifting toward multi-year enterprise contracts, capitalized by a $325 million Series D from Goldman Sachs Asset Management in 2022. Its data foundation is preferred ACR licensing from Vizio and Samsung covering more than 40 million US smart TVs, underpinning joint currency agreements with NBCUniversal, Paramount, and AMC Networks that influence an estimated $18 billion to $22 billion of annual upfront spend, roughly 40% of the upfront inventory across those conglomerates. Strategically it leans into outcomes, anchoring Paramount's "Outcomes at Scale" initiative and AMC Networks upfront guarantees, with its own 2026 upfront research finding nearly one-third of marketers planning to cut upfront budgets, making outcome guarantees the retention lever. Regulatory standing: full JIC national currency certification in August 2024, and on the MRC side, iSpot holds the first-ever MRC accreditation for National TV Ad Occurrence Reporting (2023), validating its ad catalog, over 58 trillion linear and streaming spots tracked at 98.6% reported accuracy, for verifying that an ad aired, when, and where. That accreditation covers ad occurrence only, not audience currency; person-level audience measurement remains unaccredited and rides on JIC certification while iSpot pursues broader MRC validation.

Comscore, for comparison, is fully JIC certified and has held MRC accreditation for Total Household Rating and Average Audience estimates across both national and local TV since March 2024, expanded to demographic "households with" metrics in April 2025 (confirmed against Comscore's own MRC accreditation releases), making it the only challenger accredited in both national and local markets while VideoAmp and iSpot pursue national accreditation. Neither VideoAmp nor iSpot has been granted full MRC audience-currency accreditation as of mid-2026, so the market structure is effectively one where JIC certification functions as the transactability threshold that has already unlocked billions in volume, with MRC accreditation still pending as the methodological gold standard for every challenger.

## Samba TV and Vizio Inscape: neutral identity layer versus retail-media flywheel

Samba TV is a hardware-agnostic ACR provider deployed across multiple TV brands, which structurally insulates it from single-OEM device bias. It filed for a $100 million IPO in November 2021, canceled it in 2022, and pivoted to SaaS and licensing revenue. Ownership is concentrated in venture firms August Capital and Union Grove, with strategic equity stakes held by Disney, IPG, and Warner Bros. Discovery; analyst estimates put valuation above $750 million by mid-2025, with roughly 350 employees and 30% year-over-year CTV advertising growth, followed by a $60 million venture raise in December 2025 and a June 2025 Acxiom partnership supporting a 40-market international rollout. Its 2026 strategic pivot is Project Gravity, launched May 21, 2026: an independent data onboarding and activation platform built for the agentic era, in which advertisers upload first-party lists, the platform matches them against Samba's household Identity Graph at vector-database speed using data embeddings rather than rigid exact matches, and pushes standardized segments into more than 40 ad platforms, The Trade Desk, Amazon, Meta, Google DV360, and TikTok among them, in real time, enabling autonomous AI buying agents to optimize targeting, suppression, and frequency capping independent of media inventory. Samba deliberately owns no inventory, positioning itself as a neutral identity layer against the walled gardens, supported by the Semasio contextual-targeting acquisition in October 2024 and the Bestever AI acquisition in June 2026.

Vizio Inscape sits on the other end of the spectrum. Walmart acquired Vizio in 2024 for approximately $2.3 billion, closing in December 2024, and the post-acquisition ground truth is a dual-track strategy, not absorption or independence alone. Internally, Inscape has been integrated into Walmart Connect's closed-loop attribution flywheel: at the 2026 IAB NewFronts, Walmart and Vizio delivered their first unified presentation, a content-to-commerce pipeline linking Vizio's 92 million household reach with Walmart's 150 million weekly shoppers, enabling attribution from a CTV exposure on a Vizio screen to a physical Walmart transaction. Walmart is rolling out a unified account login across new Vizio OS televisions and private-label "onn" TVs, creating a persistent identity spine from living room to register, and debuted the stack with a first-to-market L'Oréal shoppable product-placement pilot driven by Walmart first-party retail data, with a Cafe Bustelo campaign from the same presentation driving 98% incremental household reach beyond linear TV.

Externally, Inscape continues to operate as a data licensor to the measurement market that depends on Vizio's footprint: active licensing to VideoAmp and iSpot for national currency models, a November 2024 multiyear licensing pact with EDO covering telemetry from more than 24 million opted-in TVs, and a January 2025 extension with TVision integrating passive panel data for personified cross-platform and co-viewing analysis. Walmart Connect also announced in May 2026 that it was opening Vizio inventory to external programmatic platforms Yahoo DSP and Magnite. The legal architecture underneath the licensing, per Vizio's own published data-authorization terms, routes data from "VIZIO Services, LLC" solely through iSpot.tv as authorized intermediary, with iSpot hashing TV identifiers into IN#TVIDs mapped to client UUIDs, Vizio not a party to end-customer agreements, and end-users bearing responsibility for third-party service providers, a structure built to insulate Vizio from direct VPPA class-action exposure. The net read is that Walmart is simultaneously building a closed-loop retail-media walled garden on Inscape data and extracting licensing revenue as the indispensable telemetry supplier to the open measurement ecosystem.

## Attention verification: the Nielsen v. TVision litigation, the Viant acquisition, and EDO

The attention-verification niche exists because ACR proves a set was on and tuned, not that a human was watching. Two firms dominate with opposite methods: TVision runs a demographically weighted opt-in panel of roughly 5,000 households instrumented with infrared sensors, computer vision, and ACR for second-by-second eyes-on-screen, co-viewing, and in-room presence, while EDO infers attention from downstream behavioral outcomes, linking ad exposure to search spikes, site visitation, and sales.

The defining litigation, on Delaware docket 1:25-cv-00575, is Nielsen v. TVision, verified against CourtListener and Cohen Milstein's own case-study page. Nielsen sued TVision five separate times for patent infringement beginning in 2021, centered on US Patent No. 11,798,030 B1 and US Patent No. 7,783,889, covering wireless communication and automatic content recognition claims against TVision's household panels. On November 10, 2025, TVision, represented by Cohen Milstein and Quinn Emanuel, filed an antitrust counterclaim alleging Nielsen weaponized fraudulently obtained patents to drain emerging competitors and used staggered multi-year contracts that barred broadcasters from using already-paid-for Nielsen data if they declined to renew. In June 2026, a Delaware federal jury deliberated roughly two hours before returning a verdict of non-infringement in TVision's favor on the '889 patent trial. Docket summaries additionally report a stipulated voluntary dismissal of the broader patent case with prejudice dated July 22, 2026, each party bearing its own costs; that date sits after this document's build date and is carried as reported, unverified.

Viant Technology announced its acquisition of TVision Insights on April 15, 2026, for $40 million, comprising $22.5 million cash and $17.5 million stock, per Viant's own 8-K, with close expected in Q2 2026 (trade coverage reports an early May 2026 close, inside that window). The strategic effect is that attention data moves from post-campaign reporting into the programmatic bidstream, creating an "attention-adjusted CPM" that prices inventory on the probability of human engagement, bidding up attentive environments and discounting rooms the viewer has likely left, and bypassing walled-garden self-attribution.

EDO, meanwhile, has scaled its behavioral-outcome engine to over 133 trillion ad impressions, 2.5 million creatives, and 35,000 brands, adding over 1 terabyte of outcome data daily. Ahead of the 2026 upfronts it launched ChatEDO, an agentic LLM interface to its TV outcome database, offered free to all US ad agencies during the upfront season, and in June 2026, ahead of the FIFA World Cup in North America, it released Sponsored Ad Outcomes, an AI tool measuring in-stadium signage, scorebug overlays, and studio integrations, bringing deterministic ROI measurement to sports sponsorship. EDO also expanded into gaming-environment benchmarking, on Roblox, in mid-2026, and extended its data architecture outside media entirely via an early-2026 EnergyHub partnership integrating commercial building telemetry into virtual power plant ecosystems. Publisher-side adoption is broadening as well: Fubo uses both TVision and EDO data to optimize interactive CTV formats, and TVision data powered the first CTV pause-ad benchmarks in 2026, showing pause formats roughly doubling measured attention versus conventional spots.

On the litigation itself, one earlier source had reported the docket as "EDO v. TVision" rather than Nielsen v. TVision, an error traced to inheriting the framing from secondary summaries rather than pulling the primary docket; the settled record above, checked directly against CourtListener and Cohen Milstein, resolves it in Nielsen's favor as plaintiff.

## The 2027 upfront currency war

GroupM, WPP's investment arm, has formally designated VideoAmp a preferred currency for a significant portion of its national commitments, the first named, dated holding-company commitment of its kind. Omnicom Media Group, IPG Mediabrands, Publicis Media, and Dentsu have deeply integrated both VideoAmp and iSpot into cross-channel planning, bidding, and reconciliation workflows for the 2027 upfronts. Agency guidance now treats a "Nielsen-only" publisher measurement posture as a structural pricing disadvantage inviting immediate buyer price pressure, with multi-currency deal-making demanded as a standard operating condition and publishers required to demonstrate clean-room infrastructure for validating delivery against the agency's preferred currency. The buy-side has effectively forced the sell-side to absorb the operational cost of permanent measurement fragmentation.

Supporting signals reinforce the shift: Go Addressable research from May 2026, whose founding members include DIRECTV Advertising, AMC Networks, and Comcast Advertising, found 78% of US advertisers plan to factor addressable TV into 2026-2027 upfront negotiations, up from 67% a year earlier, in a market where streaming holds 66.7% of ad-supported TV time among adults 18-49. In March 2026, Meta abandoned Nielsen's Designated Market Area targeting framework in favor of Comscore Markets for automotive model advertising, a direct repudiation of Nielsen's geographic infrastructure by a major platform. And Publicis's $2.5 billion acquisition of LiveRamp vertically integrates the identity-resolution substrate that lets its clients match alternative currencies against first-party transaction data while bypassing Nielsen panels entirely, a deal covered in full in the Identity and Clean Rooms layer of this framework.

No named 2027 holding-company preferred-currency commitment currently exists for iSpot specifically; its holdco presence remains integration into planning and reconciliation workflows, a weaker form of commitment than VideoAmp's named GroupM designation, and this stays open pending any comparable announcement.
