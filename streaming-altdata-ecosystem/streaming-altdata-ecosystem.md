## 0. What this document is, and is not

This is a **map** of the vendor and data-layer architecture that has grown up around one structural fact: streaming platforms and Big Tech gatekeepers (Amazon, Apple, Roku) increasingly withhold granular subscriber, viewership, and engagement data, forcing studios, advertisers, and investors to reverse-engineer it. It records **where each layer sits, what it does, who buys it, and what is currently threatening it.** It does not issue investment verdicts and is not a portfolio artifact.

Eight per-layer master findings files live in `layers/` (see Section 8), each synthesizing every Deep Research pass run against that layer's prompt, with fault lines recorded per Section 6's convention and gaps flagged rather than papered over. The original prompt files that drove those passes now live in `supporting/prompts/`. This map deliberately holds enough to orient a reader; the `layers/` files hold the deeper detail.

**Provenance discipline:** this map reconciles two independent Deep Research passes run against the same source brief (one via Gemini, one via Google Finance Deep Research), plus one verified addition surfaced from a lower-quality third pass. Where the two primary passes gave non-overlapping specifics on the same event, this map records **both readings as a fault line** rather than silently picking one (Section 6). Two ChatGPT-run passes against a condensed version of the same prompt were reviewed and discarded as substance sources; nothing from them appears here.

## 1. Why this ecosystem exists

The direct-to-consumer streaming business has moved from subscriber land-grab to free-cash-flow discipline. That pivot made ARPU, churn, and ad-tier mix the metrics that matter, at the exact moment platforms have the least incentive to disclose them. Apple and Amazon blend standalone streaming economics into "Services" and "Subscription Services" reporting lines with no disclosed ARPU or churn. Roku and Amazon simultaneously withhold first-party consumption data from the studios licensing them content while selling that same audience data to advertisers through their own clean rooms. This asymmetry is the entire commercial reason the alt-data vendor layer exists: it is reverse-engineering infrastructure, not a nice-to-have.

## 2. The layer taxonomy

Seven vendor layers, each with its own collection method, legal exposure, and buyer set, plus **Layer 0, the Ecosystem Gatekeepers**, which is not a peer sitting alongside the seven but the boundary condition that surrounds and generates demand for all of them. Layer 0 is where withheld data and controlled distribution/rights architecture originate, in different forms, on both ends, Amazon/Apple/Roku by withholding granular economics and monetizing the data themselves, Fox/Google-YouTube/Disney/Paramount Skydance-WBD by controlling the rights and carriage architecture that creates the opacity the other seven layers exist to reverse-engineer. Netflix is the acknowledged exception to both variants. Detailed master findings files exist per layer (Section 8); this table is the orientation, not the full record.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Layer 0: Ecosystem Gatekeepers** | Withholds granular subscriber/viewership/rights economics and/or controls distribution and rights-bundling architecture; the reason the seven vendor layers below exist at all, not a vendor with its own buyers | Varies by company; antitrust/carriage disputes (e.g., Disney v. It is folded into the Glass layer file as a sub-section rather than broken out standalone; both docs describe it as a narrow niche (two vendors, one active patent dispute between them) riding directly on ACR infrastructure, not a structurally distinct layer. Layer 0 is a separate addition, formalized 2026-07-07, and should not be confused with that discarded eighth-vendor candidate; it sits outside the vendor taxonomy entirely rather than inside it.

## 3. Cross-cutting mechanics

**The Big Tech dual posture.** Roku (Roku Data Cloud/OneView) and Amazon (Amazon Marketing Cloud) are not simply defensive. Both withhold first-party consumption data from the studios that license them content while actively selling targeted access to that same audience to advertisers. This is not internal contradiction; it's the same leverage extended into a second, more lucrative market.

**Synthetic proxy construction.** Because Apple and Amazon won't break out standalone streaming metrics, analysts build proxies: implied Prime Video ARPU from Antenna/Consumer Edge-observed Amazon Channels subscription volume set against disclosed Prime membership; Apple TV+ tracked via Antenna panel data (roughly 2.1 million monthly US sign-ups, 4.8% monthly cancellation rate heading into early 2026) run against Apple's blended Services growth; implied cost-per-demand-point from leaked content budgets against Parrot Analytics' demand-expression index.

**The Netflix Paradox.** Netflix runs largely independent of this vendor ecosystem for internal product decisions — its first-party telemetry is superior to any external panel. That scale advantage is what funds its licensing arbitrage: buying undervalued legacy IP and using algorithmic distribution to extract more value from it than the original owner could. *Suits* is the standing case study (see Section 4).

**Institutional buyer segmentation.** Four distinct buyer profiles with different velocity requirements: multi-strategy hedge funds (Citadel, Point72, Millennium) buying raw unaggregated feeds to trade ahead of earnings; Big Tech corporate development licensing macro datasets for competitive intelligence while their own product teams ignore the same data in favor of first-party telemetry; legacy studio corp dev using demand/retention data as carriage-negotiation leverage; talent agencies (CAA, WME) using demand-expression data to quantify a showrunner's or actor's retention contribution as backend-negotiation leverage.

**Cross-layer triangulation is the actual product.** The thing hedge funds and studios pay for isn't a single data feed, it's the triangulation: a demand spike in Parrot Analytics (Demand layer) time-lagged against an Antenna or YipitData panel (Wallet layer) to find the corresponding subscriber surge, controlling for concurrent price hikes or competing tentpole releases before treating the correlation as causal. Antenna's "Subscriber Views" product formalizes this by stitching licensed ACR tuning data to its own transaction panel, using the Identity/Clean Rooms layer to match anonymously.

## 4. The vendor layer as its own consolidating niche

**Public proxies are scarce.** Nielsen was taken private in October 2022 (Elliott Management/Evergreen Coast Capital + Brookfield, ~$16 billion); its retail-measurement arm had already been sold to Advent International in 2021 and now runs independently as NielsenIQ. Comscore (Nasdaq: SCOR) is the sole remaining pure-play public equity in the entire taxonomy, and DoubleVerify (NYSE: DV) is the sole public proxy left in the Shield layer following Integral Ad Science's take-private.

**Comscore, 2025-2026 — two separate events, don't conflate them.** (1) A preferred-stockholder recapitalization, announced September 26, 2025 and completed December 29, 2025, with Charter Communications, Liberty Broadband, and an affiliate of Cerberus Capital Management exchanging Series B preferred shares for common stock plus new Series C preferred, eliminating a roughly $18 million annual dividend obligation. (2) A separate deleverage-via-divestiture, May 27, 2026: Comscore sold its legacy Movies (box office tracking) division to private equity firm Advaya Capital for $70 million, reverting the unit to its legacy "Rentrak" brand, and used $40.1 million of proceeds to retire its Blue Torch Finance credit facility. The next day, May 28, 2026, Comscore named Matt McLaughlin CEO (succeeding Jon Carpenter); McLaughlin was DoubleVerify's COO from 2011–2022, and his 303,030 performance RSUs vest only if the stock hits $14.50–$22.50 by 2029. Stuart Frankel (former CEO of Narrative Science) joined the board the same cycle, signaling an AI-measurement pivot.

**Buyout valuation baselines.** Mediaocean acquired Innovid (formerly NYSE: CTV), announced November 2024 and closed early 2025, at $3.15/share (~$500 million enterprise value), merging it with Flashtalking. Novacap (Canadian PE) acquired Integral Ad Science for roughly $1.9 billion ($10.30/share, a 22% premium) in December 2025, taking it private and delisting it from Nasdaq, ending its public-market duopoly with DoubleVerify. Ampere Analysis acquired PlumResearch in May 2026, backed by a majority investment from Goldenpeak, folding Plum's device-agnostic tracking into Ampere's European-based demand-forecasting business.

**The LiveRamp/Publicis acquisition — the identity-layer's headline consolidation event, and it is real.** Publicis Groupe agreed to acquire LiveRamp in an all-cash deal, $38.50/share, a 29.8% premium to the undisturbed price, announced May 17-18, 2026: $2.167 billion enterprise value, $2.546 billion total equity value (LiveRamp carried $379 million net cash). Expected to close by year-end 2026, pending shareholder and regulatory approval. Publicis frames this as a data-co-creation and agentic-advertising play, and is raising its 2027-2028 growth targets on the back of it. This is confirmed via primary-source web verification (SEC filings, both companies' own press releases); it is the single most important identity-layer event in this entire map, and it did not appear in either of the two primary research passes. Both were run before or without picking this up; treat any future re-run of the per-layer deep research prompts as needing to independently re-confirm it's still current.

## 5. Structural, technical, and regulatory vulnerabilities

**CFPB Section 1033 collapsed, then got replaced by something more dangerous.** The federal "open banking" rule was finalized October 2024 with an April 1, 2026 compliance deadline. In October 2025, Judge Danny Reeves (Eastern District of Kentucky, *Forcht Bank, N.A. v. CFPB*) enjoined enforcement, ruling the CFPB likely exceeded its statutory authority; the CFPB opened an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking in August 2025 to rewrite the rule, mooting the April 2026 deadline. But the vacuum got filled commercially, not legally: on September 16, 2025, JPMorgan Chase and Plaid struck a renewed data-access agreement that, for the first time, charges the aggregator per data pull. JPMorgan received 1.89 billion aggregator requests in June 2025 alone; the fee arrangement could cost Plaid up to $300 million annually. This converts Wallet-layer data acquisition from a fixed to a variable cost, compressing margin industry-wide regardless of the rule's ultimate legal fate.

**California's Delete Act (DROP) is a live, moving compliance deadline.** The centralized Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform went live January 1, 2026. Over 600 data brokers were registered by mid-2026 (registrant counts diverge slightly across sources: one puts consumer registrants over 215,000, another says over 300,000 within months — both are plausible readings of a fast-growing count taken at different points, not a contradiction worth resolving). The hard enforcement deadline is August 1, 2026: brokers must query DROP at least every 45 days and delete matched data within 90 days, with $200-per-request-per-day penalties that compound (a 50,000-request backlog implies $10 million/day in theoretical exposure). Annual registration is $6,000; triennial independent compliance audits begin January 1, 2028.

**VPPA litigation is an active, unsettled front, distinct from the financial-privacy fights above.** Settlement wave: Dapper Labs ($5 million, early 2025), GameStop ($4.5 million, April 2025), Willow TV ($850,000), DirectToU. The doctrinal fight is a live circuit split: the Second Circuit (*Salazar v. National Basketball Ass'n*) reads the statute broadly (sharing with a single third party like Meta is enough to create liability); the Sixth Circuit (*Paramount*) reads "consumer" narrowly, protecting platforms from incidental-visitor suits. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve this in January 2026. Separately, smart-TV manufacturers (Sony, Vizio) face class actions over ACR data collection without meaningful consent, at $2,500/violation in liquidated damages.

**Adaptation mechanics.** Vendors are migrating from passive scraping toward zero-party, incentivized first-party panels (paying consumers directly to link accounts/APIs), which is capital-intensive but VPPA/DROP-immune by design. Internationally, GDPR's opt-in regime structurally advantages Ampere Analysis and other Europe-native, deterministic-telemetry vendors over US scraping-based models; one source cites €1.2 billion in aggregate 2025 GDPR fines as the enforcement backdrop.

## 6. Fault lines the map records but does not resolve

Consistent with how `unified-toll-ecosystem.md` handles contested calls: record the disagreement, don't silently pick a side.

**Fault line 1 — Nielsen's 2025-2026 crisis is told two different ways.** One reading (via the Gemini-run pass) centers a Video Advertising Bureau report alleging Nielsen's "Big Data + Panel" currency is unstable (45-58% of analyzed hours showing >20% variance vs. legacy panels, concentrated in live sports), and a March 2026 controversy where Nielsen delayed its February 2026 "Gauge" report after unreleased data reportedly showed streaming falling to 41.9% share against a linear rebound to 47.4%, with the VAB publicly accusing Nielsen of "indefensible manipulation" ahead of the 2026 upfronts. The other reading (via the Google Finance-run pass) centers a more procedural narrative: MRC identified four priority fixes in May 2026, Nielsen integrated the ARF DASH Study for universe estimates in February 2026, but delayed the core weighting/modeling fixes to August 31, 2026 at industry request, with MRC reaffirming accreditation in July 2026 while leaving the 2025 audit formally open. Both cite the same underlying fact (adults 25-54 impressions down roughly 10% year over year) but build genuinely different stories around it — one adversarial and scandal-framed, one procedural. Treat as two partial views of the same ongoing saga until a primary-source pass (MRC's own releases, VAB's own report) reconciles them; do not cite either framing as settled fact in a thesis document without that check.

**Fault line 2 — the Disney+/Hulu/Max bundle case study uses non-overlapping numbers.** One reading frames the bundle (launched July 2024, $16.99/month ad-supported, $29.99/month ad-free) around its pricing architecture and a "38% cross-sell discount" versus buying the services separately. The other reading gives a specific retention outcome instead: a 59% twelve-month survival rate for 2024 sign-ups, roughly 28 percentage points above the standalone component services. Neither source gives both numbers. Both are plausible and not contradictory (one is a pricing fact, one is a retention outcome), but they haven't been cross-verified against each other or a primary source, and should be treated as two separate claims pending verification, not a single reconciled data point.

## 7. Adjacent-sector read-through

The retention vocabulary this ecosystem pioneered — cohort survival curves, plan-mix migration, serial-churner lifecycle tracking — is the same toolkit now used to evaluate consumer AI subscription businesses (ChatGPT Plus, Anthropic Pro, Midjourney) and B2B SaaS, where headline MAU counts are distorted by free tiers and promotional trials the same way headline subscriber counts once were for streaming. This is directly relevant to existing framework work: see `their-capex-your-revenue.md` and the S-APP hybrid-pricing mechanics in `unified-toll-ecosystem.md` Section 2 — the same underlying question (is this usage durable revenue or high-churn curiosity traffic) shows up in both trees.
