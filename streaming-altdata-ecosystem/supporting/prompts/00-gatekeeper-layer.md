# Deep Research Prompt — Layer 0: Ecosystem Gatekeepers

> Feed this file directly into Gemini Deep Research or Google Finance Deep Research. It is a prompt, not a report. Do not pre-read `streaming-altdata-ecosystem.md` into the research run beyond the context below, the point is to go past what that map already holds.

## Why this layer is numbered 0, not 8

The other seven layers (`01-wallet-layer.md` through `07-identity-clean-rooms-layer.md`) are vendors: companies selling reverse-engineered data to buyers who need it. This layer is not a peer sitting alongside them, it is the boundary condition that surrounds all seven and is the reason any of them exist. The map's own Section 1 states the mechanism directly: streaming platforms and Big Tech gatekeepers increasingly withhold granular subscriber, viewership, and engagement data, forcing studios, advertisers, and investors to reverse-engineer it. Layer 0 is where that withheld data and the money it generates actually originate and terminate, in different forms, on both ends. Every one of the seven vendor layers is, structurally, a response to a decision made at this layer. Treat this file as the one that should get re-run last, after the other seven have been researched, because its job is to explain what all of them are downstream of, not to introduce a new independent vendor category.

## Context (orientation only, do not treat as exhaustive)

The original four-name list (Amazon, Apple, Roku, Netflix) was too narrow, it only captured gatekeepers of one type: platforms that withhold granular data and monetize it themselves via a clean room. Reid's own read (2026-07-07): even companies whose core business looks nothing like a data-withholding platform can still be "the engine running this ecosystem," if their distribution and rights-bundling decisions are what force the other seven layers to exist in the first place. This layer now tracks two variants, not one:

**Variant A — data-withholding monetizers.** Companies that decline to disclose granular subscriber/viewership economics and separately monetize the underlying behavioral data themselves.

- **Amazon** — blends standalone streaming economics into the "Subscription Services" reporting line (no disclosed standalone ARPU or churn). Runs a dual posture: withholds granular viewership data from studios that license it content, while selling audience targeting built on that same data to advertisers through Amazon Marketing Cloud (an Identity/Clean-Rooms-layer walled garden in its own right, see `layers/07-identity-clean-rooms-layer.md`).
- **Apple** — blends Apple TV+ into "Services" with no disclosed standalone ARPU or churn at all, thinner disclosure than Amazon. No confirmed equivalent to AMC has been established in local research; whether Apple monetizes withheld data the way Amazon does, or simply withholds it as pure ecosystem bait (content as an iPhone/services retention tool, not a data business), is an open question this layer should resolve.
- **Roku** — runs the same withhold-and-monetize pattern as Amazon via Roku Data Cloud/OneView, but unlike Amazon and Apple, this monetization is closer to Roku's core business rather than a side effect of a much larger non-media company. Roku also has a reported investment or distribution tie-in with Fox (see below); the two should be researched as a linked pair, not independently, wherever that relationship touches this layer.

**Variant B — distribution and rights-chokepoint controllers.** Companies that don't necessarily withhold data for their own clean-room monetization, but whose rights, bundling, and carriage decisions are themselves the reason analysts, studios, and investors need the other seven layers to reverse-engineer what's actually happening.

- **Fox** — ad-dependent Linear Yield Company (per Reid's own portfolio taxonomy, "Live Event Focus" tag), not a subscription business. Fox One's premium pricing and the reported Fox-Roku investment/distribution tie-in (both still open items in `layers/06-sports-rights-layer.md` directive 10) make Fox a gatekeeper by controlling appointment-viewing rights supply and a distribution partnership, not by withholding subscriber telemetry the way Amazon or Roku do. Research this pairing (Fox/Roku) together.
- **Google/YouTube** — already documented locally as exercising real gatekeeper leverage, not vendor behavior: the November 2025 carriage dispute (Disney pulled ESPN, ABC, and FX from YouTube TV for nearly two weeks; Google held firm) per the Great Collision Substack post and `espn-retention-analysis.md`. YouTube TV's ~8 million subscribers and Sunday Ticket integration, plus YouTube capturing more total US television time than any other platform including Netflix, make Google a distribution chokepoint independent of any data-withholding posture.
- **Disney** — a Content Fortress attempting to vertically integrate sports distribution (ESPN Unlimited, the Fubo acquisition, the Fox One bundle, the NFL's 10% ESPN equity stake). Disney is not withholding data to sell to advertisers the way Amazon or Roku do, it is a gatekeeper because its bundling and rights-equity decisions are exactly the kind of opaque, non-disclosed economics (ESPN Unlimited churn, bundle-versus-standalone survival rates) that the Wallet and Glass layers exist to reverse-engineer.
- **Paramount Skydance/WBD** — the weakest fit of the four new names, flag it as such rather than asserting it confidently. The combined entity carries roughly $79 billion in debt and lost NBA rights to Amazon, which looks more like a company being squeezed by other gatekeepers than one exercising gatekeeper leverage itself. Its inclusion rests on the idea that a distressed rights-holder's forced divestiture and restructuring decisions still generate the opacity (what rights survive, at what valuation, under what debt covenant) that the vendor layers must reverse-engineer. This is the one entry this layer should actively stress-test rather than assume.

Netflix remains the acknowledged exception across both variants (see directive 4), and whether its ad-tier buildout is pulling it toward Variant A is still an open question.

## Research objective

1. **Amazon's dual posture, quantified.** What is Amazon Marketing Cloud's actual disclosed or credibly estimated revenue, and how does it compare in scale to what Amazon withholds from studio content partners? Is there any on-the-record studio or licensor complaint about this asymmetry (being denied viewership data on content they licensed to Prime Video, while Amazon monetizes viewing behavior on the ad side)?
2. **Apple's posture, resolved.** Does Apple operate any clean-room or audience-data-monetization product comparable to Amazon Marketing Cloud or Roku Data Cloud, or is Apple TV+ purely non-monetized ecosystem bait with no second-order data business at all? If the latter, Apple is structurally different from the other three gatekeepers, not just a smaller version of the same thing, confirm which is accurate.
3. **Roku's centrality.** Quantify Roku Data Cloud/OneView's actual revenue contribution and growth rate. Unlike Amazon and Apple, is this monetization strategically central to Roku's business (i.e., is Roku closer to a data company that happens to sell streaming devices) rather than a side effect of a much larger core business? What does this imply about Roku's incentives relative to Amazon's and Apple's when negotiating with the vendor layers above (Wallet, Glass, Identity/Clean Rooms specifically)?
4. **Is the Netflix exception closing?** As Netflix's ad tier scales, is there evidence it is building or acquiring clean-room, identity, or audience-data-monetization infrastructure comparable to AMC or Roku Data Cloud, i.e., converging toward gatekeeper behavior rather than remaining the "operates outside the ecosystem" exception the map currently records? Any named product, acquisition, or partnership (e.g., with an ad-tech or identity vendor from the Identity/Clean-Rooms layer) that would signal this shift?
5. **Layer-by-layer flow mapping.** This is the connective-tissue task the other seven layer files can't do individually. For each of the seven vendor layers (Wallet, Glass, Shield, Demand/Valuation, Funnel, Sports-Rights, Identity/Clean Rooms), identify specifically what flows between it and each of the eight gatekeepers now in scope (Amazon, Apple, Roku, Fox, Netflix, Google/YouTube, Disney, Paramount Skydance/WBD), in both directions: what data or leverage does the gatekeeper withhold or control that forces that layer's vendors to exist (the reverse-engineering demand side), and where does the gatekeeper itself show up as a customer, competitor, or infrastructure owner inside that layer (e.g., Amazon Marketing Cloud and Roku Data Cloud are themselves Identity/Clean-Rooms-layer participants; Fox and Genius Sports/Stats Perform have a direct commercial relationship worth checking against the Sports-Rights layer). Flag any layer where a gatekeeper's role is currently absent from the local layer file but probably shouldn't be.
6. **Stress-test the Paramount Skydance/WBD inclusion, and confirm the Fox/Roku pairing.** Paramount Skydance/WBD is the weakest-fit gatekeeper on this list (see Context), a distressed, debt-laden entity that looks more acted-upon than acting. Determine whether it belongs in this layer at all, or whether it's better modeled purely as a vendor-layer subject (its rights divestitures feeding the Sports-Rights and Demand/Valuation layers) rather than a gatekeeper in its own right. Separately, confirm the actual structure of the reported Fox-Roku investment or distribution tie-in (still unconfirmed per `layers/06-sports-rights-layer.md` directive 10), what does Roku actually get from or give to Fox, and does it change how Roku should be characterized in this layer versus Amazon and Apple. Beyond these two, is there any other platform (a traditional media DTC service, or a distributor not yet named) that should be added or that these eight collectively make redundant?
7. **Relative financial and structural centrality.** For each of the eight gatekeepers, characterize how central this ecosystem is to its business: quantify data-monetization revenue as a share of total revenue where that's the relevant lens (Amazon/AMC, Apple, Roku/Roku Data Cloud), and characterize rights/distribution centrality where that's the more relevant lens instead (Fox, Disney, Google/YouTube, Paramount Skydance/WBD). This tests whether "the engine running this ecosystem" is proportionate across all eight, or whether some are structurally central (Roku, Fox) and others are a side effect of a much larger, largely unrelated business (Amazon, Apple, Google), which should change how much weight each entry carries in any future reconciliation into the overview map.
