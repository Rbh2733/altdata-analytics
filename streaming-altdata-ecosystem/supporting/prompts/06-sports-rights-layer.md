## Context (orientation only, do not treat as exhaustive)

Live sports requires a data architecture standard SVOD transaction panels can't serve: real-time latency, betting-handle correlation, and blackout compliance. Sports Innovation Lab and Zoomph announced an AI-driven partnership in April 2025 fusing Sports Innovation Lab's transactional "Fluid Fan Graph" data with Zoomph's real-time social audience intelligence. Stats Perform secured a landmark January 2026 agreement to serve as FIFA's official betting-data and live-video-stream distributor for the 2026 Men's World Cup and 2027 Women's World Cup, processing optical tracking via its Opta and RunningBall infrastructure. Netflix's acquisition of NFL Christmas Day games is cited as the validating case for why this specialized infrastructure matters, since it fractured traditional domestic tracking models.

This layer sits downstream of a rights-and-distribution war already covered in depth by prior research: the NFL's 10% ESPN equity stake, the Fox One bundle ($39.99/mo, ESPN as senior partner), the Venu Sports antitrust collapse, the YouTube TV carriage blackout (late 2025, $110M cost to ESPN), and ESPN Unlimited's DTC pricing tiers; Genius Sports' NFL data exclusivity through 2029; and Netflix's Barstool/Ringer podcast acquisitions as fandom infrastructure, not content licensing. Treat all three as known ground, not research targets, the point of this run is what that prior research does not already answer.

A fourth local source, `Career/substack/substack_post3_great_collision.md` ("The Great Collision," Reid's own published Substack Post 3), already establishes the category-collision framing this whole layer sits inside and names facts this prompt should treat as settled rather than re-ask: ESPN Unlimited launched August 2025 at $29.99/month; the ESPN-Fox One skinny bundle runs $39.99/month; the YouTube TV carriage dispute (Disney pulled ESPN/ABC/FX for nearly two weeks) happened in November 2025 and Google held firm; Fox pays ~$2.2B/year for NFL rights and is classified in Reid's own taxonomy as a Linear Yield Company ("Live Event Focus" tag) for whom those rights are a survival cost, not a growth investment; Amazon rebuilt TNT's NBA broadcast operation on acquiring the rights (Ian Eagle, Kevin Harlan, Stan Van Gundy on the call; studio panel of Dirk Nowitzki, Dwyane Wade, Steve Nash, Blake Griffin, Candace Parker); MLB national games are split across seven platforms (ESPN, NBC, Peacock, Netflix, Apple TV+, Fox, TBS); college conference realignment (SEC to ESPN/ABC, Big Ten to Fox/CBS/NBC) already runs on media-rights logic. The post also articulates this layer's core thesis directly: exclusive league data rights function as a permission-layer toll, structurally parallel to semiconductor lithography access, that collects from every bidder in the rights war regardless of who wins the content itself. That framing, not the map's original wording, is the operative one going forward.

## Research objective

1. **Stats Perform's FIFA execution.** Confirm current status of the Stats Perform/FIFA arrangement heading into or through the actual 2026 Men's World Cup. Any reported issues, expansions, or competitive challenges to Stats Perform's exclusivity?
2. **Sports Innovation Lab/Zoomph, one year on.** What has actually shipped from this partnership since April 2025? Any named brand or rights-holder client using the fused Fluid Fan Graph-plus-social product, and any reported results?
3. **Streaming platforms' own sports-data ambitions.** Is Netflix, Amazon, or YouTube building or acquiring in-house sports-analytics capability (rather than relying on Stats Perform/Opta-style third parties) given the scale of their rights commitments (NFL Christmas Day, Thursday Night Football, NFL Sunday Ticket)?
4. **Betting-handle correlation, concretely.** Prior research asserts this data "feeds automated market makers and sportsbooks" without a specific example. Is there a named sportsbook or prediction-market operator publicly using Stats Perform or a competitor's live data feed, and any disclosed commercial terms?
5. **Competitive landscape beyond Stats Perform.** Genius Sports and Sportradar are the two most obvious public/near-public competitors in this space that neither prior pass mentioned at all. Establish their current scale, ownership (Genius Sports is Nasdaq-listed; confirm ticker and recent financials), and how they compete with or complement Stats Perform for streaming-platform rights-data contracts. Separately verify the "toll layer" claim from the Great Collision post directly: that exclusive data-rights partnerships (cited there as covering the NBA through 2032, plus MLB, UEFA, FIFA, NHL, NASCAR, and 400+ other leagues/federations) are consolidating into fewer, deeper deals rather than fragmenting, and that this pricing power compounds as rights costs escalate. Get the actual named holder(s) of each partnership and a primary source, the post does not attribute vendor names to every league.
6. **League breadth beyond the NFL.** MLB's national-game fragmentation across seven platforms (ESPN, NBC, Peacock, Netflix, Apple TV+, Fox, TBS) and the SEC-to-ESPN/ABC, Big Ten-to-Fox/CBS/NBC conference realignment are already documented locally (Great Collision post); do not re-confirm those. Push past them: what is the data/rights-distribution posture of the NHL specifically (thinnest-covered league in local material), and of the College Football Playoff's own rights-distribution structure, against the same vendor set (Stats Perform, Genius Sports, Sportradar)? Separately, identify how niche independent leagues, The Savannah Bananas' Banana Ball is the named example, are handling data and streaming distribution outside the incumbent-vendor model entirely (self-distributed via YouTube/owned channels rather than licensed to a rights-data vendor). Is "opt out of the vendor layer entirely" a viable emerging pattern, or a novelty limited to leagues without betting-market relevance?
7. **Sports media journalism as a rights-adjacent track.** Amazon's NBA broadcast/studio buildout (Ian Eagle, Kevin Harlan, Stan Van Gundy on the call; studio panel of Dirk Nowitzki, Dwyane Wade, Steve Nash, Blake Griffin, Candace Parker) is already documented locally via the Great Collision post, do not re-confirm it, but do establish whether that operation has any commercial relationship with the rights-data vendors mapped in directives 1-5, or is built entirely on separately-sourced broadcast talent and production. Two cases still need verification from scratch: (a) Netflix's reported exclusive video-rights licensing of Barstool Sports' largest podcasts (confirm terms, exclusivity scope, and date, this sits next to but is analytically distinct from the fandom-retention argument already made in `sports-podcast-fandom.md`, and is not mentioned in the Great Collision post either); (b) NBA Countdown's status as a studio/journalism property that has continued operating on its own footing through recent NBA rights turnover (ESPN/ABC, NBC, Amazon).
8. **ESPN Unlimited as a standalone SVOD/AVOD aggregation layer.** Confirm the actual mechanism by which ESPN Unlimited connects to or authenticates against MLB.tv and NBA League Pass, is this a resale/bundling commercial agreement, a TV Everywhere-style authentication pass-through, or marketing language without a real integration? Get the specific terms if disclosed; neither local source (the espn-retention-analysis or the Great Collision post) confirms this integration exists.
9. **The carriage-dispute-to-DTC-launch pattern, and a sequencing conflict to resolve.** Two local sources disagree on timing in a way that needs primary-source reconciliation, not silent averaging: `espn-retention-analysis.md` frames the YouTube TV blackout as preceding and motivating ESPN Unlimited's acceleration, while the Great Collision post dates ESPN Unlimited's launch to August 2025 and the YouTube TV blackout (Disney pulled ESPN, ABC, and FX for nearly two weeks, Google held firm) to November 2025, after the DTC launch, not before it. Determine the actual sequence and whether "carriage dispute forces DTC launch" is the right causal story here at all, or whether it's a narrative retrofitted onto two events that happened to be close in time. Separately, determine whether any other rights holder (Fox, Warner Bros. Discovery, Paramount Skydance) has run or is running a comparable playbook, pull leverage from a distributor dispute, then launch or accelerate direct distribution.
10. **Fox One, priced and positioned deliberately.** Local material already establishes Fox's NFL rights cost (~$2.2B/year), its classification as a Linear Yield Company for whom those rights are survival cost rather than growth investment, and the ESPN-Fox One skinny bundle price ($39.99/month, ESPN as senior partner), do not re-confirm those. Push past them: confirm Fox One's own standalone launch price (distinct from the ESPN-Fox One bundle price) and its stated rationale, verify whether it was priced specifically to satisfy a Super Bowl streaming-rights requirement while deliberately avoiding cannibalization of traditional pay-TV subscription revenue (consistent with Fox's ad-dependent, not subscription-dependent, revenue model). Confirm the Fox-Roku investment or distribution tie-in, its structure and stated purpose, neither local source mentions it. Characterize Fox One's content mix (reportedly near-entirely live sports and news) against ESPN Unlimited's broader footprint, and evaluate whether "Fox is the ESPN arm of Disney, without a Disney+-equivalent entertainment platform to bundle into" is an accurate structural read or an oversimplification.
