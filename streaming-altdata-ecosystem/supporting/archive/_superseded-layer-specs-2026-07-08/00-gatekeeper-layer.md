## 1. Findings by research objective

**1. Amazon's dual posture.** Amazon runs the same withhold-and-monetize move as Roku, at greater scale. It reports over $70 billion in trailing-twelve-month advertising revenue as of Q1 2026, confirmed directly in Andy Jassy's own words in Amazon's Q1 2026 earnings release (aboutamazon.com, reported April 29, 2026): "Advertising grew to over $70 billion in TTM revenue." Q1 2026 advertising services revenue alone was $17.24 billion, up 24% year over year. Amazon Marketing Cloud is the sell side of that revenue: it withholds granular Prime Video and Amazon Channels subscriber and viewership data from the studios licensing content to it, while selling access to that same audience through AMC's clean-room infrastructure. The studio-complaint side of this is now backed by a stronger citation than earlier readings had: a WGA National Labor Relations Board unfair-labor-practice charge against Amazon over withheld Prime Video data, a formal, current complaint rather than an older FTC letter.

**2. Apple's posture resolved.** Apple runs no clean room and blends Apple TV+ into Services with no disclosed standalone ARPU or churn. The clearest on-record confirmation is Eddy Cue's dated quote (November 2025) stating the strategy plainly: Apple TV+ is ecosystem bait, not a standalone profit center, folded into Services reporting by design rather than by omission.

**3. Roku's centrality.** All three passes quantify Roku's position as structurally central to the ecosystem, on different cuts of the same growth story: FY2025 annual figures split three ways (Advertising / Subscriptions / Devices), Q1 2026 quarterly figures split two ways (ex-Devices), and a single TTM headline number. The cuts differ, the direction doesn't: Roku's Platform (Advertising plus Subscriptions) revenue is the growth engine and Devices is a loss-leading distribution cost, consistent across every framing. No variant isolates Roku Data Cloud/OneView revenue specifically; it stays bundled inside Platform/Advertising in every disclosure and every pass (see Gaps remaining).

**4. Netflix's exception, closing but not closed.** All three passes track the same $3 billion 2026 ad-revenue target for Netflix's ad tier. They disagree sharply on ad-tier subscriber scale, an unresolved order-of-magnitude fault line (see Section 4).

**5. Layer-by-layer flow mapping.** All three describe what gatekeepers withhold that creates vendor-layer demand. None fully closes the reverse direction, what flows back from vendor layers to gatekeepers (see Gaps remaining). Within that shared limitation, Run 3 adds the fullest per-layer detail: Shield-layer fraud-rate data and Sports-Rights toll-layer detail. Run 2 adds the NBA rights-settlement narrative under both Demand/Valuation and Sports-Rights.

**6. PSKY/WBD stress test and the Fox-Roku tie-in.** All three converge on reclassifying Paramount Skydance/WBD as a vendor subject, not a gatekeeper, consistent with `streaming-altdata-ecosystem.md`'s own framing of that entry as the weakest fit of the eight. All three match on Fox-Roku deal mechanics (below). Run 2 carries the fullest NBA settlement account.

**7. Relative centrality ranking.** All three converge on the same ordering: Roku, Fox, and Disney are structurally central to this layer; Amazon, Apple, and Google are side effects of larger businesses that happen to touch this ecosystem; Paramount Skydance/WBD is a distressed subject being acted upon rather than an active gatekeeper in its own right.

## 4. Fault lines

**Resolved, settled going forward:**

Amazon's TTM advertising revenue is $70 billion-plus as of Q1 2026, not the ">$50 billion annually" figure Run 1 carries. Verified directly against Amazon's own Q1 2026 earnings release (aboutamazon.com, reported April 29, 2026): Andy Jassy's own quote states advertising grew to "over $70 billion in TTM revenue," with Q1 2026 advertising services revenue of $17.24 billion, up 24% year over year. Run 1's figure undercounts by roughly $20 billion and should not be cited. This same conflict recurs in the Layer 7 file, resolved once and cross-referenced there rather than re-litigated.

LiveRamp/Publicis pricing was never actually a conflict. Run 2's "$2.54 billion" and Run 3's "$2.16 billion" are both correct, they were measuring different things. Verified against Publicis Groupe's own press release (publicisgroupe.com, dated May 17, 2026): total enterprise value $2.167 billion, acquisition price $38.50 per share, total equity value $2.546 billion, acquired net cash $379 million ($2.546B equity minus $379M net cash equals $2.167B EV, exactly). Use $2.167 billion enterprise value and $2.546 billion equity value going forward, matching `streaming-altdata-ecosystem.md` Section 4.

The AMC lookback-window figures (Run 2's "13 months to 5 years" and Run 3's "13 months to 25 months") describe two different datasets within Amazon Marketing Cloud, not one contested figure. Amazon did two separate things: it extended the ad-traffic lookback window from 13 to 25 months (following its unBoxed 2025 event, matching Run 3), and separately unlocked up to five years of historical data for store-purchase signals and video viewership (matching Run 2, backed by Amazon's own advertising.amazon.com blog post announcing the 5-year retail lookback). Both figures are correct and both belong in the record.

**Open, recorded rather than resolved (per this map's own Section 6 convention):**

Netflix's ad-tier scale carries an order-of-magnitude gap that neither pass closes. Run 3 states the ad tier surpassed 40 million global users by 2025. Run 2 states 250 million monthly active users by the May 2026 upfronts, up from 190 million in late 2025. Both are cited to roughly the same window. Treat as an open fault line pending a primary-source check against Netflix's own investor disclosures.

Roku/Tubi FAST viewing share doesn't reconcile arithmetically. Run 2 states the Roku Channel alone holds 6.3% of US streaming time, second only to YouTube. Run 3 states Tubi plus Roku Channel combined, pre-merger, hold 5.2% (Nielsen Gauge). A combined figure should exceed the standalone figure for the same underlying metric; as stated, it doesn't. Open fault line.

Suits licensing value is presented two ways with no overlap: Run 2 cites $356 million in US/Canada subscriber revenue, Run 3 cites "over $800 million in global licensing value." Different scope (regional versus global), each presented as a standalone headline. This matches the same two figures already sitting side by side, unreconciled, in `streaming-altdata-ecosystem.md` Section 4 (Netflix Licensing Arbitrage citations); treat both as live until a primary source separates regional from global.
